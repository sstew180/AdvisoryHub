SKILL: How to Prepare a Risk Dashboard for Executive Reporting

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Risk & Audit

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

A risk dashboard is the summary-level representation of the organisation's risk position, prepared for executive and Committee consumption. It is the single artefact that tells executives, the ARC, and Council whether the risk position is improving, stable, or deteriorating, and what they need to pay attention to. A good dashboard turns a 150-risk register into a 2-page document that drives decisions. A bad dashboard becomes a decorative artefact that executives flick past. This skill covers how to design, populate, and maintain a risk dashboard that actually gets used.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "design" or "build" a risk dashboard
- User asks to "prepare" the risk section of a Board or ARC paper
- User asks how to "visualise" risk data for executive consumption
- User asks how to "summarise" a risk register for reporting
- User asks what a risk dashboard should "show" or "include"
- User asks how to present risk information to non-specialists
- User asks how to report on risk appetite compliance

 

STRUCTURE

A risk dashboard has five core elements. Each element answers a specific question an executive or Committee member will have.

Element 1 Overall position (answers: "how are we doing?"): A single-page view of the residual risk position across the organisation. Typically a heatmap showing the distribution of risks across the likelihood and consequence matrix, with any risks outside appetite highlighted. Include the total count of strategic risks, the count by residual rating, and the count outside appetite. Include a trend arrow for each rating band compared to the previous reporting period.

Element 2 Risks outside appetite (answers: "what needs attention?"): The subset of risks where the residual rating is outside the organisation's stated appetite. For each, show the rating, the appetite threshold, the owner, the treatment status, and the expected date for return to appetite. This is the single most important element for executive attention.

Element 3 Significant movements (answers: "what has changed?"): Risks that have moved in or out of the risk appetite boundary, or that have moved two rating levels since the last report. For each, a brief narrative explaining the movement (what happened, what was done, what is the impact).

Element 4 Emerging risks (answers: "what is coming?"): Risks that are not yet in the register but are being watched for potential addition. Typically arising from horizon scanning, external reports, peer Council incidents, regulatory change, or internal audit observations. Include source and potential impact.

Element 5 Control and treatment status (answers: "is management acting?"): Summary status of the treatment plans for risks outside appetite, and the results of any control effectiveness testing completed in the period. Green/amber/red indicators for treatment plan progress against milestones. Reference to internal audit findings where relevant.

A dashboard may also include:

Element 6 Incident summary: Brief summary of incidents that occurred in the period, separated into those resulting in actual loss and those resulting in near-miss outcomes. Useful for identifying whether risk ratings reflect actual experience.

Element 7 Insurance position: For organisations with complex insurance programs, a summary of insurance coverage, claims activity, and any material exclusions or limits that affect the residual risk position.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- Design the dashboard backwards from the decisions it needs to inform. The ARC needs information to provide assurance to Council. Executives need information to act. Different audiences often want the same underlying data but presented differently. Decide who the dashboard is for before deciding what is on it.
- One page beats ten. A dashboard that cannot be taken in at a glance is not a dashboard, it is a report. If executives spend more than five minutes on the dashboard itself, it has too much detail. Supporting detail belongs in appendices or in the register, not on the dashboard.
- Colour is a signal, not a decoration. Use colour consistently and sparingly. Red means outside appetite or requiring action. Amber means within tolerance but watch. Green means within appetite and stable. Using colour for anything else dilutes the signal.
- Trend matters more than position. A risk that has been High for two years is less interesting than a risk that moved from Medium to High in the last quarter. Always show movement.
- Avoid false precision. If the risk rating is based on a judgement, do not present it with numerical precision that implies calculation. "High" is a judgement. "High, likelihood score 4.2 out of 5, consequence score 4.1 out of 5, composite score 17.22" is a judgement dressed up as a calculation. The dressed-up version is usually less useful.
- Always state the data source and cut-off date. Executives need to know whether the dashboard is current. Include the date of the underlying register, the reporting period covered, and the date the dashboard was produced.
- Include commentary, not just visuals. A heatmap alone is decoration. A heatmap with a short narrative ("The increase in High-rated compliance risks this quarter reflects the three findings in the June internal audit of contract compliance. Treatments are in progress, expected closure September.") turns visualisation into information.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good dashboard can be read by a Councillor or ARC member in under five minutes, and at the end of those five minutes they know the overall position, the areas requiring attention, what has changed, and whether management is acting.

A dashboard must avoid the following:
- Displaying every risk in the register (the dashboard is a summary, not the register)
- Numerical precision that implies calculated certainty for judgement-based ratings
- Colour coding that does not map to appetite or action thresholds
- Static presentation of dynamic information (always show movement)
- Jargon that requires a glossary ("VaR", "ERM maturity", "integrated controls assurance" used without translation)
- Data that is more than one reporting period old
- Generic commentary that could be copied unchanged from one period to the next

A dashboard must include the following:
- A clear scope (strategic risks only, whole-of-organisation, a specific division, a specific project)
- A clear reporting period and cut-off date
- The overall position with trend indicators
- Risks outside appetite with treatment status
- Significant movements since the previous period
- Emerging risks under watch
- Control and treatment status
- A named author and an endorsement (CEO for executive dashboards, CRO or equivalent for technical detail)

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Risk dashboards at Queensland local governments are typically prepared for three audiences: the Executive Leadership Team (monthly or quarterly), the Audit and Risk Committee (quarterly or half-yearly, consistent with LGR s211), and Council (typically via the ARC's reports under LGR s211(c)).

The ARC dashboard should be designed to support the Committee's role under LGA s105 and LGR s208 to s211, including monitoring the integrity of the financial documents, the effectiveness of internal audit, and the organisation's risk position. The dashboard should explicitly link risk to the internal audit findings and management's response.

The dashboard should include a view of risks relating to fraud, corruption, or misconduct separately from other categories, consistent with the Council's obligations under the Crime and Corruption Act 2001 and the organisation's fraud and corruption control framework.

WHS risk is typically reported through a separate WHS dashboard aligned to the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld) obligations. The risk dashboard should include a summary line cross-referencing the WHS dashboard rather than duplicating it.

The dashboard should reference the organisation's adopted Risk Appetite Statement and should explicitly identify risks outside appetite, consistent with the ARC's oversight role.

For larger councils including Gold Coast, the dashboard may be organised by Directorate or strategic outcome area to align with the Corporate Plan structure. This makes it easier for the ELT and ARC to connect risk to strategic priorities.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Dashboard commentary (overall position): At 31 March 2026, the strategic risk register contained 22 risks. Residual rating distribution: 2 Extreme, 6 High, 11 Medium, 3 Low. Four risks are currently outside appetite. This represents a net reduction of two risks outside appetite since the December 2025 reporting period.

Dashboard commentary (risks outside appetite):
- STRAT-08 (wastewater network capacity): High residual, appetite allows up to Medium. Treatment on track for November 2026. Owner: Director Water and Waste. Capital upgrade proceeding as planned.
- STRAT-12 (ICT cyber resilience): High residual, appetite allows up to Medium. Treatment behind schedule; two of five milestones not met. Owner: Director Business Services. Briefing to ELT scheduled for 14 April.
- STRAT-15 (major contract dependency): High residual, appetite allows up to Medium. Treatment in the procurement phase; replacement arrangement expected to reduce rating by Q3 2026. Owner: Director Economic Development.
- STRAT-19 (capital works program delivery): Extreme residual, appetite does not allow Extreme. New treatment plan approved at the March ELT. Owner: Director Infrastructure. Next report April.

Dashboard commentary (significant movements): Three risks moved during the quarter.
- STRAT-03 (regulatory compliance) moved from High to Medium following completion of the compliance framework refresh. Within appetite.
- STRAT-11 (workforce capability) moved from Medium to High. Root cause: higher than anticipated turnover in professional services roles. Treatment being developed.
- STRAT-18 (community safety) newly added to register. Arising from the December 2025 incident review.

Dashboard commentary (emerging risks): Two risks under watch. Climate adaptation cost escalation (monitoring pending finalisation of the adaptation strategy). AI governance (horizon scanning following peer Council incident and State Government direction).

Dashboard metadata: Prepared by the Manager Risk and Internal Audit on 8 April 2026. Covers the period 1 January to 31 March 2026. Based on the strategic risk register as at 31 March 2026. Endorsed by the Chief Executive Officer.
