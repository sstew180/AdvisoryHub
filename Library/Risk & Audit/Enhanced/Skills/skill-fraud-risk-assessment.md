SKILL: How to Conduct a Fraud Risk Assessment

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Risk & Audit

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

A fraud risk assessment is the structured evaluation of where fraud could occur within an organisation, how likely it is, what the impact would be, and whether the controls in place are adequate. For Queensland local governments, fraud risk assessment is a core component of the fraud and corruption control framework and a key input to the ARC's oversight role. A good assessment identifies real fraud exposures and drives targeted action. A bad assessment produces a generic list of fraud scenarios that could apply to any organisation and never results in a specific control change. This skill covers how to conduct an assessment that produces insight and drives improvement.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "conduct" or "perform" a fraud risk assessment
- User asks to "refresh" or "update" an existing fraud risk assessment
- User asks to "identify" fraud risks or fraud scenarios for a specific area
- User asks to review the effectiveness of "fraud controls" or the "fraud control framework"
- User asks to prepare a fraud risk report for the ARC
- User asks how fraud risk differs from other forms of risk
- User asks how to comply with CCC expectations for fraud and corruption control

 

STRUCTURE

A fraud risk assessment has seven stages.

Stage 1 Establish the scope and framework: Define what is covered. A fraud risk assessment can be whole-of-organisation (typically done every three to five years) or focused on a specific area (annually or in response to an emerging concern). Define fraud for the purposes of the assessment. Fraud commonly includes the categories: asset misappropriation (theft, skimming, fraudulent disbursements, cash receipts fraud), financial statement fraud (intentional misstatement), corruption (bribery, conflicts of interest, secret commissions), and procurement fraud (bid rigging, kickbacks, shell companies, false invoicing). Establish the assessment team and the governance arrangements. The Chief Audit Executive or Chief Risk Officer typically leads, with participation from senior management in the area being assessed.

Stage 2 Identify the fraud scenarios: For each category and each in-scope process or area, identify specific scenarios that could occur. A scenario is a concrete description of what could happen, by whom, through what mechanism. "Procurement fraud" is not a scenario; "a delegated officer directs contracts to a shell company operated by a relative, approving false invoices below their delegation threshold" is a scenario. Use workshops, interviews, process walkthroughs, review of prior incidents, peer Council reports, and external sources (QAO reports, CCC case studies, international fraud research such as the ACFE Report to the Nations).

Stage 3 Assess the inherent fraud risk: For each scenario, assess the likelihood and the consequence assuming no controls are in place. Consider three factors drawn from the fraud triangle and related research: pressure (what would motivate someone to commit this fraud), opportunity (what makes this possible), and rationalisation (how someone might justify it). These factors drive the likelihood assessment more than generic probability ratings. For consequence, consider financial, reputational, regulatory, and operational dimensions.

Stage 4 Identify and evaluate existing controls: For each scenario, identify the controls that exist to prevent, detect, or respond to the fraud. Controls typically include: segregation of duties, authorisation and approval requirements, system controls (workflow, access restrictions, system rules), reconciliations, independent reviews, data analytics, whistleblower arrangements, staff training and awareness, and the fraud and corruption control framework itself. For each control, assess design effectiveness and operating effectiveness.

Stage 5 Assess the residual fraud risk: After existing controls, what is the remaining risk? Compare against the organisation's risk appetite (which for fraud and corruption is typically No Appetite, with limited tolerance for low-likelihood, low-consequence scenarios). Identify the scenarios where residual risk is outside appetite.

Stage 6 Identify treatment options: For each scenario outside appetite, identify actions to bring residual risk within appetite. Treatment options include: strengthening preventive controls, introducing detective controls (including data analytics and targeted transaction monitoring), enhancing response arrangements, conducting targeted audits, updating policies, running training campaigns, or removing the opportunity (for example, by restructuring the process to eliminate a single point of control). Assign each treatment to an owner with a target date.

Stage 7 Report and integrate: Produce a fraud risk assessment report. The report should cover the scope, methodology, scenarios assessed, ratings, control evaluation, residual risk position, treatments, and recommendations. Feed the results into the fraud and corruption control framework, the risk register, the internal audit plan, and the fraud awareness training program. Present to the ELT, then to the ARC. The ARC considers the report, monitors treatment implementation, and reports to Council.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- Fraud is different from other risks. Other risks arise from events and processes. Fraud arises from intent. Controls that work for operational risk (such as documented procedures) may not work for fraud, because fraud involves deliberate circumvention of controls. Design the assessment accordingly.
- Segregation of duties is the single most important preventive control for most fraud scenarios. Where segregation is compromised (small teams, acting arrangements, system-level single points of control), fraud risk rises sharply. The assessment must identify these points explicitly.
- Fraud scenarios must be specific. A list of fraud categories is not an assessment. Specific, named scenarios that describe who could do what, through what mechanism, are the minimum useful output.
- Consider the internal perpetrator. Most fraud is committed by insiders, and most of them are people with long tenure and trust. Do not design the assessment on the assumption that fraud is committed by junior staff in the first year of employment.
- Consider collusion. Many fraud scenarios require collusion between two or more people. Controls that rely on segregation are undermined by collusion. Assess the realistic risk of collusion in each area.
- Use data. Modern fraud risk assessment uses data analytics to identify anomalies, outliers, and unusual patterns. Analytics cannot replace assessment but it can inform it and, once implemented, can become an ongoing detective control.
- The assessment is not a one-off. Fraud schemes evolve. The assessment should be refreshed regularly. Emerging areas (AI-generated invoices, synthetic identities, cryptocurrency payments) should be considered proactively.
- Fraud control is not just fraud prevention. The assessment should consider the response capability as well: how quickly would fraud be detected, how is it investigated, who is responsible, what are the external reporting obligations.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good fraud risk assessment identifies specific, credible fraud scenarios, honestly evaluates the controls in place, and produces targeted treatments that are implemented.

A fraud risk assessment must avoid the following:
- Scenarios expressed as categories ("procurement fraud", "payroll fraud") rather than specific descriptions
- Ratings without reasoning
- Control evaluations based on the existence of controls rather than their operation
- Treatment plans that address all scenarios with generic awareness training
- Assessments conducted without participation from senior management in the areas being assessed
- Reports that understate fraud risk to match an expected narrative
- Failure to address areas where the organisation's actual fraud history is a predictor (areas with prior incidents, areas with recent control changes, areas with new systems)

A fraud risk assessment must include the following:
- A defined scope and methodology
- Specific, named fraud scenarios
- Inherent risk ratings with documented reasoning
- Evaluation of existing controls, covering both design and operation
- Residual risk ratings against the organisation's fraud risk appetite
- Treatments for scenarios outside appetite, with owners and timeframes
- A report endorsed at the right level (CEO, then ARC, then Council)
- Integration with the risk register, internal audit plan, and training program

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Fraud risk assessments at Queensland local governments should align with the Crime and Corruption Commission's (CCC) guidance and the Council's fraud and corruption control framework. The CCC publishes guidance on corruption prevention and case studies that should be referenced in any assessment.

The obligation to notify suspected corrupt conduct to the CCC arises under section 38 of the Crime and Corruption Act 2001. Where a fraud risk assessment identifies actual or suspected corrupt conduct (as distinct from hypothetical scenarios), the notification obligation applies. The assessment team should understand the distinction between identifying a risk (no notification) and identifying conduct (notification required).

The QAO Better Practice Guide: Fraud Control is the primary Queensland reference for fraud risk assessment methodology at local government. Where the organisation has not previously conducted a full fraud risk assessment, starting with this guide is recommended.

Fraud risk assessment is a core component of the internal audit function under LGR s207. The internal audit plan should include periodic fraud risk assessments (typically every three years at whole-of-organisation level) and targeted fraud audits in higher-risk areas.

The ARC's oversight role under LGA s105 and LGR s208 to s211 includes oversight of fraud and corruption control. The ARC should receive the fraud risk assessment report, consider the residual risk position, and monitor the implementation of treatments.

For Gold Coast and other councils operating under strategic contracting (LGR Chapter 6 Part 2), procurement fraud risks should be assessed against the specific governance arrangements under strategic contracting, including the Contracting Plan, Significant Contracting Plans, and Contract Manual. Procurement fraud schemes often exploit the interaction between procurement policy and day-to-day practice, and a good assessment will test this interaction.

Conflicts of interest arising from the integrity provisions of the LGA (Chapters 5A and 5B, for councillors, and the Public Sector Ethics Act 1994 (Qld), for employees) are a specific corruption category that should be addressed in every assessment.

Whistleblower arrangements are governed by the Public Interest Disclosure Act 2010 (Qld). The fraud control framework and the fraud risk assessment should confirm that whistleblower pathways are in place, accessible, and trusted. A Council where staff do not know how to raise a fraud concern has a major control gap that raises residual risk across all scenarios.

The Australian Standard AS 8001-2021 Fraud and corruption control is a useful reference for methodology and is commonly cited in fraud risk assessments across the public sector.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Scope: This fraud risk assessment was conducted between January and April 2026 and covers the procurement and accounts payable processes across the organisation. It refreshes the 2023 whole-of-organisation fraud risk assessment for these specific processes, in response to the move to strategic contracting in 2017 and the finance system upgrade in 2025.

Scenario (procurement fraud): A delegated officer with authority to approve contracts up to $500,000 (under the Contract Manual) directs multiple contracts to a supplier in which a family member has an undisclosed beneficial interest. The officer does not declare the conflict. Contracts are split below the officer's delegation to avoid higher-level review. Payment is approved based on invoices that overstate the work performed, with the family member returning a portion of the payment to the officer in cash.

Inherent risk: Likelihood Possible (3). Reasoning: The control environment provides opportunity through undeclared conflict risk and split-contract risk; pressure varies across the population of delegated officers; rationalisation in similar schemes at other councils has been "everyone does it"; the ACFE research indicates procurement schemes of this type account for approximately 15 percent of occupational fraud cases involving public sector entities. Consequence: Major (4). Reasoning: Financial loss to Council on each scheme documented in peer Council cases ranges from $200,000 to $3 million; reputational loss is significant; mandatory CCC notification applies. Inherent rating: High.

Existing controls:
- C1 (Preventive) Conflict of interest declarations required annually and at the commencement of procurement activities. Design: Effective. Operation: Partially Effective (2025 internal audit found that 18 percent of sampled procurement processes did not have a current conflict declaration on file).
- C2 (Preventive) Contract Manual requires significant contracting plans for contracts above $1 million, subject to review above the delegated officer. Design: Effective. Operation: Partially Effective (2026 audit found weaker compliance, see separate report).
- C3 (Preventive) System controls prevent splitting of related procurements below delegation thresholds. Design: Partially Effective (the system does not detect related procurements across different supplier entities with shared ownership). Operation: Effective within design.
- C4 (Detective) Annual procurement analytics run by internal audit to identify patterns consistent with split contracts, supplier concentration, and unusual payment timing. Design: Effective. Operation: Effective.
- C5 (Detective) Public Interest Disclosure arrangements and anonymous fraud reporting line. Design: Effective. Operation: Not independently tested in this assessment.

Residual risk: Likelihood Possible (3). Consequence: Major (4). Residual rating: High. Outside appetite.

Treatments:
- T1 Enhance the conflict of interest process to require a specific declaration at each procurement stage for contracts above the significant contract threshold. Owner: Chief Procurement Officer. Target: 30 September 2026.
- T2 Implement a data analytics rule to identify contracts to suppliers with common beneficial ownership, using the ABN search and external data. Owner: Manager Internal Audit in conjunction with Chief Procurement Officer. Target: 31 December 2026.
- T3 Incorporate the refreshed scenario into the 2026 to 2027 fraud awareness training program. Owner: Manager People and Culture. Target: 30 November 2026.

Reporting: Fraud risk assessment report presented to the ELT on 10 April 2026, endorsed with the above treatments. Presented to the ARC at the May 2026 meeting. ARC to monitor treatment implementation and to receive a progress update at the November 2026 meeting.
