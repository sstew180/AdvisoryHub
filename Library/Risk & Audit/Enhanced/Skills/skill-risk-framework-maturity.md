SKILL: How to Assess the Maturity of a Risk Management Framework

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Risk & Audit

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

A risk management framework maturity assessment is the structured evaluation of how effectively an organisation's risk management system is operating in practice. It distinguishes between having a framework on paper and having risk management genuinely embedded in how decisions are made. A good maturity assessment produces a clear picture of where the organisation is, where it wants to be, and what the gap looks like. It supports the Audit and Risk Committee's oversight role and, done well, drives real improvement. Done badly, it becomes another compliance artefact. This skill covers how to conduct an assessment that produces insight rather than paperwork.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "assess" the maturity of risk management
- User asks to "benchmark" the risk management framework
- User asks how "mature" the risk management function is
- User asks to conduct a "capability review" or "health check" of risk management
- User asks how to "measure" the effectiveness of the risk framework
- User asks how to prepare a maturity report for the ARC or the CEO
- User asks what "good" looks like in risk management

 

STRUCTURE

A maturity assessment has four stages, applied sequentially.

Stage 1 Define the maturity model: Adopt or adapt a maturity model with defined levels. The common structure uses five levels. Initial (ad hoc, reactive, people-dependent), Developing (frameworks exist but are not consistently applied), Defined (policies, processes, and tools are in place and are applied consistently), Managed (risk management is integrated into decision-making, measured, and improved), Optimising (risk management drives strategic advantage and continuous improvement).

Define what each level means for each domain of the framework. The typical domains for assessment are: Governance and accountabilities, Risk appetite, Risk identification and assessment, Controls and treatment, Risk reporting, Culture and capability, Integration with decision-making, Continuous improvement.

Stage 2 Gather evidence: Evidence is collected across four sources.

Document review: The risk appetite statement, framework, policy, register, past reports, ARC minutes, internal audit reports touching on risk, the Corporate Plan, the procurement policy, the fraud control framework. Look for what is documented and whether documents are current, endorsed, and aligned.

Interviews: Key roles including the CEO, Directors, the Chief Risk Officer (or equivalent), selected Managers and risk owners, the Chair of the ARC, an independent ARC member. Interview questions test both understanding ("what is the organisation's risk appetite for financial risk?") and application ("what was the last risk-related decision you made, and how did the framework influence it?").

Observation: Attendance at ARC meetings, ELT risk discussions, and risk review sessions. Observe how risk is actually discussed, not how it is supposed to be discussed.

Testing: Sample-based testing of risk register entries, control effectiveness assessments, and treatment plan progress. Is the documented position supported by evidence?

Stage 3 Assess against the model: For each domain, assign a maturity level based on the evidence. Document the reasoning for each rating. Identify specific examples supporting the rating, both positive and negative. Produce a heatmap showing the organisation's current position across domains.

Identify the gap between current maturity and the desired maturity level. Desired level is typically set by the ARC or the CEO, reflecting the organisation's strategic context. Not every organisation needs to reach Level 5 in every domain; a Level 3 position may be appropriate for some domains given resources and strategic priorities.

Stage 4 Develop and communicate findings: Produce a report with: the maturity model used, the evidence gathered, the rating for each domain with reasoning, the overall maturity position, the gap to desired maturity, recommendations for improvement (ranked by expected impact and effort), and a proposed action plan with owners and timeframes.

Present to the CEO and ELT first, then to the ARC, then through the ARC's reports under LGR s211(c) to Council. The sequencing matters; the CEO should not first hear of a maturity finding at the ARC meeting.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- A maturity assessment is a judgement, not a calculation. Be explicit about the reasoning for each rating. A rating without reasoning is unreliable and unusable.
- Avoid the common trap of rating what is documented rather than what is practised. A framework that exists on paper but is not used is not Level 3; it is Level 1 with a Level 3 document. Evidence of application is what matters.
- Be honest about self-assessment bias. The Chief Risk Officer conducting their own maturity assessment will tend toward optimism. An assessment conducted by internal audit, or by an external party, or by a peer Council exchange, is more reliable.
- Distinguish between the framework's design and the framework's operation. A good framework poorly operated is a Level 2 or 3 position. A poorly designed framework well operated may be Level 2 or 3 for a different reason. The remediation is different in each case.
- Do not chase maturity levels for their own sake. The goal is risk management that supports decision-making. Level 5 is not the goal for every organisation in every domain. Match maturity ambition to strategic context.
- Include a view on culture and capability. Technical framework maturity is easy to document. Culture and capability are harder but often more important. If people in the organisation do not understand risk management, or do not use it, the technical framework does not matter.
- Be specific about recommendations. "Improve risk culture" is not a recommendation, it is an aspiration. "Include a risk discussion item in the monthly ELT meeting, with the CEO chairing and each Director speaking to their top three risks for five minutes, starting in July 2026" is a recommendation.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good maturity assessment produces a clear and defensible picture of the current position, a credible view of where to aim, and a practical plan to close the gap.

A maturity assessment must avoid the following:
- Ratings based on documentary review only, without testing whether the documents are being applied
- Generic recommendations applicable to any organisation ("embed risk management in decision-making", "increase risk awareness")
- Aspirational language that obscures actual findings
- A plan with too many actions to be implementable
- Failure to distinguish between domains where the organisation is genuinely performing well and domains where it is not
- Failure to distinguish between framework design weaknesses and framework operation weaknesses

A maturity assessment must include the following:
- A defined maturity model with levels described for each domain
- Evidence from multiple sources (documents, interviews, observation, testing)
- A rating for each domain with documented reasoning
- Specific examples supporting each rating
- A clear statement of the gap to desired maturity
- Ranked recommendations with owners and timeframes
- A plan that is achievable within a defined horizon (typically 18 to 24 months)

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Risk management maturity assessments at Queensland local governments should align with ISO 31000:2018 principles and with the QAO Better Practice Guide: Risk Management.

The assessment should cover the organisation's compliance with LGR s207 (internal audit) and the associated requirement to examine risks and control measures. An organisation that lacks a current risk register, or whose register is not connected to the internal audit plan, has a significant maturity gap in this area regardless of how sophisticated other parts of the framework appear.

The ARC has a statutory oversight role for risk management under LGA s105 and LGR s208 to s211. A maturity assessment that does not evaluate how effectively the ARC is discharging this role is incomplete. The assessment should consider the ARC's charter, meeting cadence, composition, access to information, and the quality of the risk reporting it receives.

The assessment should evaluate the integration between the risk management framework and the fraud and corruption control framework. The Crime and Corruption Commission's guidance sets expectations for local government fraud and corruption controls, and the risk framework should give effect to this in the fraud and integrity risk category.

For Gold Coast and other larger councils, the assessment should evaluate how well the risk framework accommodates the strategic contracting arrangements under LGR Chapter 6 Part 2. Procurement risk management under strategic contracting is a specialised maturity domain.

The QAO's Insights on Audit Committees in Local Government publication is a useful reference point for benchmarking governance maturity, particularly the oversight role of the ARC. The publication's skills matrix approach is often cited in maturity assessments.

Where an external maturity assessment is commissioned, it should be procured consistently with the Council's procurement policy. The assessment findings are typically considered by the ARC and, depending on sensitivity, may be the subject of an ARC paper recommending actions to Council.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Scope and approach: This maturity assessment evaluates the risk management framework of [Council name] against a five-level maturity model covering eight domains. Evidence was gathered through review of 24 documents, interviews with 18 officers (including the CEO, all Directors, the Chief Risk Officer, six Managers, and the Chair and two independent members of the Audit and Risk Committee), attendance at two ELT meetings and two ARC meetings between January and March 2026, and sample testing of 40 entries in the strategic and operational risk registers.

Finding (Domain: Risk Appetite): Current maturity assessed at Level 3 (Defined). Evidence supporting the rating: A risk appetite statement was adopted by Council in June 2024 and covers all major risk categories with tolerance thresholds. It is referenced in ARC papers. Evidence against a higher rating: Interviews with six of eight Directors indicated that they could not articulate the appetite statement for their primary risk categories. Sample testing of 40 register entries found that 12 entries recorded a residual rating outside appetite with no treatment plan in place, inconsistent with the decision rules in the appetite statement. Desired maturity: Level 4 (Managed). Gap: The appetite statement exists but is not systematically used to drive decisions. Recommendation: Embed appetite checking into the risk register review cycle (recommendation R4), and run a targeted education program for Directors and Managers on the appetite statement (recommendation R5).

Finding (Domain: Culture and Capability): Current maturity assessed at Level 2 (Developing). Evidence supporting the rating: Risk management training has been delivered to the organisation's 180 Managers over the last two years. Evidence against a higher rating: Staff surveys in 2024 and 2025 indicated that 41% of respondents agreed that risk is considered in decision-making in their area, down from 44% in 2023. Interviews with operational Managers indicated that the risk register is viewed as a task rather than a tool. Risk management is not systematically part of project business cases or procurement planning. Desired maturity: Level 3 (Defined). Gap: Risk culture is not consistent across the organisation, and there is no current strategy to improve it. Recommendation: The Executive Leadership Team to develop and adopt a Risk Culture Strategy by November 2026, including indicators to be reported annually to the ARC (recommendation R9).

Recommendation summary: The assessment makes 12 recommendations, ranked by expected impact and effort. Four recommendations are assessed as high-impact and low-effort (and should be implemented by September 2026). Five recommendations are high-impact and medium-effort (implementation by March 2027). Three recommendations are medium-impact and higher-effort (implementation by December 2027). An implementation plan is proposed for adoption by the ELT and endorsement by the ARC.
