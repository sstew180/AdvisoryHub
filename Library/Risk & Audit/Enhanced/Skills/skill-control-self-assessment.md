SKILL: How to Run a Control Self-Assessment

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Risk & Audit

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

A control self-assessment (CSA) is a structured process through which management evaluates its own controls, with facilitation support from internal audit or the risk function. Done well, a CSA produces credible evidence about how controls are actually operating, identifies gaps that management can address without waiting for audit, and strengthens the first line of defence. Done badly, it produces a pile of self-ratings that overstate effectiveness and undermine trust in the control environment. This skill covers how to design and run a CSA that produces genuine insight and avoids the common traps.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "run" or "design" a control self-assessment
- User asks to "implement" or "introduce" a CSA program
- User asks to "conduct" a control self-assessment for a specific process, system, or business unit
- User asks how CSA differs from internal audit
- User asks how to "facilitate" a control self-assessment workshop
- User asks how to report CSA results to management or the ARC
- User asks how to use CSA as part of the combined assurance approach

 

STRUCTURE

A control self-assessment program has five stages.

Stage 1 Scope and objectives: Define what is being assessed. A CSA can cover a process (accounts payable, procurement, grants administration), a system (the finance system, the HR system), a business unit (a Directorate), or a specific risk (fraud risk in cash handling). The scope must be concrete. "Control environment" is too broad; "the controls over procurement from quote to payment in the [service] team" is right-sized. State the objectives clearly: typically, to assess the design and operation of controls, identify gaps, and develop improvement actions.

Stage 2 Design the CSA method: Choose the method based on the scope and the culture of the organisation. Three common methods:

(a) Facilitated workshop: A structured session, usually two to four hours, where the process owner and the control owners walk through the process, identify the risks, and evaluate the controls. Best for complex processes with multiple participants.

(b) Structured questionnaire: A set of questions about controls, distributed to relevant staff, with responses consolidated and tested. Best for large-scale assessments where workshop logistics are prohibitive.

(c) Management representation: A structured exercise where the responsible manager certifies the operation of specified controls, supported by evidence. Best for ongoing periodic assessments of well-understood processes.

Most programs combine methods. For example, a workshop to identify risks and controls, followed by an annual questionnaire to monitor ongoing effectiveness.

Stage 3 Conduct the assessment: Run the chosen method. For workshops, prepare a process map and a draft risk and control list in advance; use the workshop to challenge and refine, not to generate from scratch. For questionnaires, pilot the questions before release to ensure they are clear and answerable. For management representations, specify the evidence required for each control.

For each control assessed, capture: the control description, the control owner, the control type (preventive, detective, corrective), the control frequency, the person conducting the control, the evidence available, the assessed design effectiveness (is the control designed to address the risk), and the assessed operating effectiveness (is the control actually operating as designed).

Stage 4 Validate the results: The credibility of a CSA depends on validation. Three common approaches:

(a) Self-assessment plus sample testing: The internal audit or risk team samples controls rated Effective and tests them independently. Gaps between self-assessment and test results are analysed and reported.

(b) Reciprocal peer review: Teams review each other's control environments. Works well where similar processes exist in multiple areas.

(c) Management attestation with evidence requirement: Each management response must be supported by documentary evidence, which is reviewed (not re-tested) by the facilitating team.

Stage 5 Report and act: Produce a report that distinguishes between design issues (the control is missing or inadequate), operating issues (the control exists but is not being performed), and effectiveness issues (the control operates but does not prevent or detect the risk). Each issue is assigned to an owner with a remediation timeframe.

Feed the CSA results into the risk register (residual risk ratings may need to be updated) and the internal audit plan (CSA results may indicate areas for targeted assurance).

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- CSA is not internal audit. Internal audit provides independent assurance; CSA is a management tool. The two are complementary, not substitutes. An ARC that replaces internal audit with CSA is weakening its assurance, not strengthening it.
- The credibility of CSA depends on validation. A control rated Effective by management that fails on independent testing calls the entire CSA program into question. Build validation in from the start and make the program less susceptible to optimistic self-assessment.
- CSA works best where the culture supports honest self-reporting. If managers are punished for reporting control weaknesses, they will not report them. The CEO and ELT need to establish the position that CSA findings are a basis for improvement, not a basis for blame.
- Design effectiveness and operating effectiveness are separate. A control that is well designed but not being performed is not effective. A control that is performed but does not address the risk is not effective. Assess both dimensions.
- Keep the scope tight. CSAs that try to cover too much produce superficial results. Better to run five deep CSAs in a year than fifty shallow ones.
- Facilitate, do not audit. When internal audit or risk facilitates a CSA, the role is to ask questions, provide structure, and challenge assumptions, not to form an independent opinion. If the facilitator ends up producing an audit opinion, the distinction between CSA and audit has broken down.
- Document everything. The CSA is a formal process that may be tested by internal or external audit. Workshop outputs, questionnaire responses, evidence reviewed, and conclusions reached must all be on file.
- Integrate with the risk register. CSA findings should flow directly into the risk register. If they do not, the register and the CSA are producing parallel but disconnected views of the control environment.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good CSA produces credible insight into the control environment, identifies specific gaps, and drives targeted action. It gives management a current view of how controls are operating and gives internal audit and the ARC a basis for combined assurance.

A CSA must avoid the following:
- Scopes so broad that the results are superficial
- Ratings without evidence or without validation
- Rating scales that do not distinguish between design effectiveness and operating effectiveness
- Management responses that commit to "review" rather than specific remediation
- Results that are produced but not acted on
- Over-claiming effectiveness (a common pattern where all controls rate Effective regardless of evidence)
- Being used as a substitute for internal audit rather than as a complement to it

A CSA must include the following:
- A defined scope and clear objectives
- A documented method (workshop, questionnaire, or attestation, or a combination)
- Control descriptions, types, owners, and evidence
- Assessment of both design and operating effectiveness
- A validation step, such as independent sample testing or peer review
- A report distinguishing design, operating, and effectiveness issues
- Integration with the risk register and the internal audit plan
- Action plans with owners and timeframes

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Control self-assessment at Queensland local governments should be considered as part of a combined assurance approach, alongside internal audit (LGR s207), external audit (QAO), and specialist functions (WHS, information security, fraud control). The goal is to provide Council and the ARC with comprehensive assurance without duplication.

The ARC's oversight role under LGA s105 and LGR s208 to s211 extends to the overall assurance framework, not only internal audit. The ARC should receive reports on the CSA program's coverage, results, and remediation status, at a summary level consistent with its agenda.

CSA is particularly valuable for processes that are too transactional to be audited frequently but too important to be ignored. Examples at Queensland local governments include grants administration, fees and charges processing, development assessment fees, fleet management, and community facility bookings.

CSA findings relating to financial controls should be mapped to the relevant provisions of the LGR (Chapter 3 financial management) and to the organisation's financial management strategy.

CSA findings relating to procurement controls at Gold Coast and other councils operating under strategic contracting should be framed against LGR Chapter 6 Part 2 and the Council's Contracting Plan and Contract Manual, not against the default procedures.

Where a CSA identifies potential fraud, corruption, or misconduct, the immediate action is to notify the CEO (or, if the CEO is implicated, the Chair of the ARC). Reporting obligations under the Crime and Corruption Act 2001 (section 38) apply.

CSA programs should reference the IIA guidance on control self-assessment, recognising that internal audit's role in CSA is facilitative rather than audit-based.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Scope: This control self-assessment covered the controls over procurement of goods and services from requisition to payment in the Infrastructure Directorate, for the period January to June 2026. The CSA was facilitated by the internal audit function at the request of the Director Infrastructure, in response to the 2025 to 2026 internal audit finding on significant contracting plan compliance.

Method: A three-hour facilitated workshop with the Director Infrastructure, the Manager Business Services, the Senior Procurement Officer, and three delegated officers responsible for procurement across the Directorate. Pre-workshop preparation included a process map, a draft risk and control register, and extracts from the Contracting Plan. Post-workshop, the facilitator circulated the refined register for review and sign-off.

Validation: The internal audit function selected 25 procurement transactions from the period, tested them against the controls self-assessed as Effective, and reported the results to the Director and the Manager Business Services. Of the 25 transactions tested, 22 confirmed the self-assessment; 3 identified weaker operating effectiveness than self-assessed (two related to significant contracting plan documentation, one related to delegation evidence on file).

Finding: The delegation check control (self-assessed as Effective in design and operation) was confirmed as well-designed but with partial operating effectiveness. In 1 of 25 transactions tested, the evidence of delegation check was missing from the file. Design is sound; operation requires improvement. Action: Manager Business Services to circulate a reminder to delegated officers, and to introduce a quarterly spot check on delegation evidence. Implementation by 31 August 2026.

Integration: Three of the CSA findings have been added to the operational risk register (Infrastructure Directorate) as new control gaps. The internal audit plan has been adjusted to replace the planned 2027 procurement audit with a follow-up review of the Infrastructure Directorate CSA and remediation actions.
