SKILL: How to Write an Internal Audit Report

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Risk & Audit

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

An internal audit report is the formal record of an internal audit engagement. It sets out what was audited, what was found, and what management is going to do about it. The report is the single artefact that travels from the audit team to management, to the Audit and Risk Committee, and often to Council. The quality of the report determines whether the work done during the audit translates into actual improvement, and whether the ARC can place reliance on the internal audit function. This skill covers how to write a report that is clear, defensible, and actionable.

- User asks to "write" or "draft" an internal audit report

- User asks how to present findings, observations, or recommendations

- User asks how to present an audit report to the ARC

 

STRUCTURE

An internal audit report has seven sections. The order is fixed. Each section does one job.

Section 1 Executive summary: One page. The audit objective, the overall conclusion (the auditor's opinion on the control environment for the area audited), a summary of the findings by rating, and the key messages. This is what the CEO and ARC read first and often the only part they read in detail.

Section 2 Background and scope: The reason for the audit, its place in the annual plan, the period covered, the scope (what was in and what was out), and any scope limitations. Short. Half a page.

Section 3 Approach: How the audit was conducted. The methodology applied, the standards followed (typically IIA Standards), the locations visited, the people interviewed, the documents reviewed, the samples tested, and the tools used. Half to one page.

Section 4 Overall conclusion: The auditor's opinion on the control environment for the area audited, using the organisation's standard conclusion scale (commonly: Strong, Sound, Partially Effective, Requires Improvement, Unsatisfactory). The conclusion must be supported by the findings. Brief. Two to four sentences.

Section 5 Findings and recommendations: The substance of the report. Each finding is presented using the five-element structure (Criteria, Condition, Cause, Consequence, Recommendation, see the base skill on audit findings). Each finding has a rating (Significant, Moderate, Minor, or High, Medium, Low, per the organisation's scale). Findings are ordered by rating, most significant first.

Section 6 Management response: Management's response to each finding. Typically includes agreement or disagreement, the specific action to be taken, the accountable officer (position title), and the target implementation date. If management disagrees with a finding, the report records that disagreement and may include the auditor's rejoinder.

Section 7 Appendices: Supporting material including the audit universe entry, the detailed testing approach, the sample selection methodology, and the definitions of the rating scales used. Not for the ARC's primary attention but available if questions arise.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- The report must be defensible at every level. Every finding must be traceable to the working papers. Every recommendation must be addressed to the right owner. Every rating must be consistent with the organisation's rating scale. The Chief Audit Executive signs the report and, by doing so, stands behind every word in it.
- Write the executive summary last. The summary should reflect the findings, not the expectation. Too many reports are written from the summary down; this produces summaries that promise findings the report does not deliver.
- The overall conclusion is the auditor's opinion. It is not a summary of the findings. It is a professional judgement about the control environment as a whole, informed by the findings. A report with three Significant findings may have a Partially Effective or Requires Improvement conclusion, depending on the area and the significance.
- Findings must be factual. Opinions belong in the conclusion. Criteria belong in the published standard or policy. Condition belongs in the evidence. Cause and consequence are analytical but grounded. Keep them separate.
- Recommendations should be specific and assignable. "Management should improve controls" is not a recommendation. "Management should implement a monthly reconciliation of X against Y, performed by the Finance Business Partner, reviewed by the Manager Finance, with evidence retained on file, starting July 2026" is a recommendation.
- Management responses should be robust. A response of "agreed, will review" is not a commitment. A good response states the specific action, the owner, and the date. Where management disagrees, the reasoning should be stated and the auditor should consider whether to revise the finding or maintain the position with a rejoinder.
- Consult in draft. The report should be shared with management in draft before being finalised. Factual errors can be corrected, disagreements can be surfaced and resolved, and the final report is stronger. Drafts should be marked as such.
- The report is a record. It will be read years later, sometimes in the context of an external review, a regulatory inquiry, or a CCC matter. Write it with that in mind.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good internal audit report can be read by an ARC member in twenty minutes, and at the end of that reading they understand the area audited, the auditor's conclusion, what was found, and what management is going to do about it.

A report must avoid the following:
- Executive summaries that understate or overstate the findings to match an expected narrative
- Conclusions that do not align with the findings (a Strong conclusion with three Significant findings)
- Findings written as narrative paragraphs rather than in the five-element structure
- Recommendations written as aspirations ("improve", "strengthen", "enhance") without specific action
- Management responses that commit to "reviewing" something but not to doing anything
- Language that criticises individuals or implies intent, rather than describing systems and controls
- Appendices that include the full working papers (they should stay in the working paper file, referenced in the report)
- Reports that are issued more than three months after the fieldwork is completed (the value diminishes rapidly)

A report must include the following:
- A clear statement of the audit objective
- A defined scope with exclusions
- A documented approach
- An overall conclusion using the organisation's rating scale
- Findings using the five-element structure with ratings
- Management responses with named owners and target dates
- The Chief Audit Executive's sign-off
- A date of issue

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Internal audit reports at Queensland local governments should align with the IIA International Standards for the Professional Practice of Internal Auditing, particularly the standards covering communication of results (Standard 2400 series). While the IIA Standards are not mandatory for Queensland local government, alignment is strongly encouraged.

Internal audit reports feed into the ARC's oversight role under LGA s105 and LGR s208 to s211. The ARC reviews reports, monitors management's implementation of recommendations, and provides assurance to Council. Reports should be written with the ARC as a primary audience in mind.

Audit findings relating to financial controls should reference relevant provisions of the LGR (particularly Chapter 3 on financial management) and, where applicable, the Financial Accountability Act 2009 (Qld) for matters involving controlled entities.

Audit findings relating to procurement at Gold Coast and other councils operating under strategic contracting should reference LGR Chapter 6 Part 2 (sections 217 to 222) rather than the default procedures. Findings citing the wrong legislative framework are a sign of an audit team that has not adapted to the Council's specific arrangements.

Audit findings relating to fraud, corruption, or misconduct must be handled with particular care. The reporting pathways under the Crime and Corruption Act 2001 (section 38) sit outside the audit report and may be activated during the audit itself. Where the auditor identifies suspected corrupt conduct during fieldwork, the immediate obligation is to notify the CEO or, if the CEO is implicated, the Chair of the ARC or directly to the CCC.

Audit findings relating to councillor conduct must be handled consistently with the integrity provisions of the LGA (Chapter 5A and 5B) and typically require legal review before inclusion in a report.

QAO's external audit work produces management letters that commonly overlap with internal audit findings. Internal audit reports should acknowledge where a finding also appears in the external auditor's observations, and should cross-reference. The ARC should not see the same finding raised independently by both audit functions without coordination.

For Queensland local governments, the language of audit reports matters. Words like "wilful", "negligent", or "reckless" have legal meaning and should not be used casually. The auditor describes controls and conditions; characterisation of conduct is for others.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Executive summary: This audit examined the operation of procurement controls for contracts entered into under the strategic contracting procedures during the period July 2025 to December 2025. The audit was conducted between January and March 2026 as part of the approved 2025 to 2026 Internal Audit Plan. The audit identified four findings (two Significant, one Moderate, one Minor). The overall conclusion is Partially Effective. The Significant findings relate to the completion of market assessments for contracts above the significant contract threshold and the documentation of exceptions to the contracting plan. Management has agreed with all findings and has committed to implementing the recommendations by 31 December 2026.

Finding (Significant): Criteria: The organisation's approved Contracting Plan (adopted by Council in August 2025, consistent with LGR s220) requires that every procurement of a contract identified as a "significant contract" be preceded by a documented significant contracting plan consistent with LGR s221. Condition: Testing of 14 significant contracts entered into in the period identified 5 (36 percent) where no significant contracting plan was completed before the contract was awarded. In 3 of these 5 cases, no significant contracting plan has been completed at the date of this report. Cause: The procurement management system does not enforce the significant contracting plan requirement before a procurement proceeds, and the periodic review of the contracting plan has not addressed the gap. Consequence: The absence of a significant contracting plan for these contracts means that market assessment, risk analysis, and delegation arrangements were not documented as required by the organisation's own procedures and by LGR s221. Aggregate contract value for the 5 contracts is $14.2 million. Audit trail and governance over these contracts is weaker than the procurement framework contemplates. Recommendation: That the Chief Procurement Officer, by 31 October 2026, (a) implement a system control in the procurement management system requiring a significant contracting plan reference before a procurement above the significant contract threshold can proceed, and (b) complete the five outstanding significant contracting plans and present them to the next ARC meeting following completion.

Management response: Agreed. The Chief Procurement Officer will implement the recommended system control by 30 September 2026, ahead of the recommended date. The five outstanding significant contracting plans will be completed and presented to the December 2026 ARC meeting.

Overall conclusion: Partially Effective. The strategic contracting framework is in place and is operating for most contracts reviewed. However, the significant contracting plan requirements are not being applied consistently, and the supporting system controls are insufficient. The control environment requires improvement in these specific areas to be fit for purpose.

Sign-off: Prepared by the Senior Internal Auditor, reviewed by the Manager Internal Audit, and issued by the Chief Audit Executive on 18 April 2026.
