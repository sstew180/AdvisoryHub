SKILL: How to Design an Internal Audit Program

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Risk & Audit

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

An internal audit program is the plan that sets out what the internal audit function will examine, when, and why. The program exists on two horizons: a strategic (typically three-year) program that maps audit coverage against the organisation's risk profile, and an annual program that sequences the audits to be delivered in a specific financial year. A good program provides coverage where risk is greatest, responds to emerging areas, and leaves room for the unforeseen. A bad program audits what is easy, produces findings that management already knew, and fails the Audit and Risk Committee's need for assurance. This skill covers how to design a program that does its job.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "design" or "develop" an internal audit program
- User asks to "refresh" or "update" an existing audit plan
- User asks to prepare the "strategic" or "long-term" internal audit plan
- User asks to prepare the "annual" internal audit plan

- User asks how to align internal audit coverage with the risk register
- User asks how to prepare the ARC paper seeking endorsement of the audit plan

 

STRUCTURE

An internal audit program has six layers, in order.

Layer 1 Strategic context: The organisation's strategic priorities (Corporate Plan), risk profile (strategic risk register), regulatory environment, and recent changes (new systems, new programs, organisational restructure, significant new risks). This layer anchors the program in the organisation's actual situation. Two to three pages.

Layer 2 Audit universe: A structured list of everything that could be audited. Typical categories: Financial (payroll, accounts payable, general ledger, rates and charges, asset accounting), Operational (by service area), Compliance (by legislation or policy area), Governance (delegations, policies, decision-making, Council committees), Information and Cyber (ICT general controls, application controls, cyber resilience), Fraud and Integrity, Project and Program (major capital works, major procurements), Performance (service delivery outcomes, value for money). The audit universe is typically 80 to 150 items for a large Council.

Layer 3 Risk assessment of the universe: For each item in the audit universe, assess the inherent risk (the significance of the area to the organisation if it went wrong), the control environment (what is known about the maturity of controls, based on prior audits, management representations, and external audit findings), and the audit history (when it was last audited, what was found, whether actions are closed). Produce a prioritisation score.

Layer 4 Strategic audit plan (three years): Using the prioritised universe, allocate audits across a three-year horizon. Higher-priority items get audited earlier or more often. Lower-priority items may be scheduled once in three years or rotated. Cover every major category over the three-year period, even if the specific audits within a category vary.

Layer 5 Annual audit plan: Translate the first year of the strategic plan into a detailed annual plan. For each audit, include: title, objective, scope, timing (typically quarterly bands or specific months), estimated effort (in hours or days), lead auditor, and linked strategic risks. Include a contingency allocation of 10 to 20 percent for unplanned audits.

Layer 6 Resource and delivery plan: How the program will be delivered. Internal capability, co-source arrangements (specialist skills or peak load), outsource arrangements. Total budget. Quality assurance arrangements. Reporting cadence to the Chief Executive Officer and the ARC.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- The audit program must be driven by risk, not by convenience. Auditing the same areas every year because they are well understood is comfortable and adds little value. Auditing the areas the organisation knows least about, or where risks are changing, is harder and adds more.
- The audit universe must be defined at the right level of granularity. Too high and you audit "Finance" once every three years and miss important sub-areas. Too low and you have 500 audit objects and no structure. For a large Council, 80 to 150 items is typical.
- Not every audit in the plan is an assurance audit. Advisory or consulting engagements, control design reviews, and follow-up audits are legitimate inclusions. The plan should be clear about what each audit is intended to achieve.
- The program must leave room for the unforeseen. A rigid plan with 100 percent of capacity allocated twelve months in advance will not survive the first major incident. Build in capacity for flex.
- The program must be endorsed at the right level. The plan is developed by the Chief Audit Executive (or equivalent), reviewed and recommended by the ARC under its oversight role, and adopted by Council or the CEO depending on the organisation's arrangements. Without this endorsement, the program has no authority.
- Link every audit to one or more strategic risks. If an audit cannot be linked to a risk in the register, ask why the audit is in the plan. The plan is not a checklist of topics, it is an assurance program.
- Update the plan during the year. A plan that was right in July is unlikely to be right the following April. Formal mid-year updates should be presented to the ARC.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good internal audit program can be read by an ARC member in forty-five minutes, and at the end of that reading they know what is going to be audited, why, and how the program provides assurance over the organisation's key risks.

An audit program must avoid the following:
- Annual plans that are just a list of audit topics without scope, objective, or linkage to risk
- A strategic plan that is a repackaging of the previous plan with minor edits
- An audit universe that omits significant areas (ICT controls, fraud risk, procurement under strategic contracting)
- A plan that allocates 100 percent of capacity with no contingency
- Audits of areas where management has indicated no issues, at the expense of areas where risks are known or suspected
- Over-reliance on low-risk compliance audits to demonstrate coverage at the expense of risk-based coverage
- A plan that does not address the areas of concern raised by the external auditor in the management letter
- Plans that are endorsed but never revisited during the year

An audit program must include the following:
- A documented strategic context linking the program to the Corporate Plan and the risk register
- A current audit universe with documented risk assessment and prioritisation
- A three-year strategic plan with visible rotation and coverage
- A detailed annual plan with objectives, scope, timing, effort, and linked risks
- A resource and delivery plan covering capability, co-source, outsource, quality assurance, and reporting
- A contingency allocation
- Evidence of endorsement, including the date and the endorsing body
- A documented review and amendment process

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Internal audit programs at Queensland local governments are required under LGR s207. The Regulation requires the internal audit function to have a written plan for each financial year and a written plan for the longer term. This is the statutory basis for the annual plan and the strategic plan.

The plan must be developed in a way that is consistent with the organisation's risk position, which in turn is documented in the risk register and the risk management framework (see the related skills).

The ARC has a statutory role in the oversight of internal audit under LGA s105 and LGR s208 to s211. The ARC reviews and endorses the internal audit plan, monitors its delivery, and reviews the reports produced. The ARC's role extends to providing assurance to Council that the plan is appropriate and that it is being delivered.

Queensland Treasury's Financial Accountability Handbook and the Department of State Development, Infrastructure, Local Government and Planning's bulletins both provide guidance on internal audit arrangements at Queensland local governments, building on the IIA International Standards for the Professional Practice of Internal Auditing. While the IIA Standards are not mandatory for local government, alignment with them is strongly encouraged and is a common benchmarking point in QAO's external audit work.

The Queensland Audit Office (QAO) is the external auditor for every Queensland local government and its management letter typically includes observations that feed into the internal audit plan. The annual plan should explicitly address any open or material recommendations from the external auditor, and the ARC paper seeking endorsement should reference these.

For Gold Coast and other larger councils, the audit program should include coverage of the strategic contracting arrangements under LGR Chapter 6 Part 2. Procurement and contract management is a higher-risk area in any organisation, and the specific governance arrangements under strategic contracting warrant targeted audit coverage.

Fraud and corruption control audits should align with the Crime and Corruption Commission's guidance and with the Council's fraud and corruption control framework. The plan should include fraud risk assessments and targeted fraud-related audits on a cyclical basis.

WHS audits are typically delivered through the WHS management system rather than the internal audit program, with the internal audit program providing oversight-level assurance (for example, auditing the governance and effectiveness of the WHS management system) rather than undertaking technical WHS audits itself.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Strategic context: This three-year internal audit program is aligned to the 2026 to 2031 Corporate Plan, the current strategic risk register (22 risks, refreshed March 2026), and the external audit observations from the 2024 to 2025 management letter. The program reflects the organisation's move to a cloud-first ICT strategy, the 2026 commencement of the Regional Water Security capital program, and the Queensland Audit Office's recent focus on cyber resilience across the local government sector.

Audit selection rationale (selected examples):
- Cyber resilience review (Year 1, Q1): STRAT-12 rated High residual. Appetite breach. QAO observation in 2024 to 2025 management letter. Priority audit.
- Capital works program governance (Year 1, Q3): STRAT-19 rated Extreme residual. New capital program. High inherent risk. Priority audit.
- Strategic contracting compliance review (Year 2, Q1): STRAT-15 rated High residual. First dedicated audit of strategic contracting since 2022. Priority audit.
- Fraud risk assessment refresh (Year 2, Q2): Cyclical. Last full assessment in 2023. Supports the fraud and corruption control framework refresh.
- Payroll controls (Year 3, Q2): Cyclical. Last audited 2024 with no significant findings. Rotational coverage.

Resource and delivery plan: The program will be delivered by the in-house team of four FTE, supplemented by a co-source panel for specialist technical audits (cyber, actuarial, forensic). Estimated annual budget $1.15 million including co-source. Contingency of 15 percent of capacity. Quality assurance through an external quality assessment every five years, consistent with IIA Standards, next scheduled 2027.

Endorsement: Proposed for endorsement by the Audit and Risk Committee at the May 2026 meeting, and for adoption by Council at the June 2026 Ordinary Meeting.
