SKILL: How to Write a Risk Appetite Statement

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Risk & Audit

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

A risk appetite statement is the written expression of the level of risk an organisation is willing to accept in pursuit of its objectives. It translates the Council's strategic priorities into operational boundaries that executives and managers can use to make decisions. Without an appetite statement, every risk rating is untethered. "High risk" means nothing unless there is a stated threshold at which High risk is unacceptable and treatment is required. This skill covers how to write an appetite statement that is specific enough to guide decisions, plain enough to be used, and endorsed at the right level to have authority.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "write" or "draft" a risk appetite statement
- User asks to "review" or "refresh" an existing appetite statement
- User asks how to "define" or "articulate" risk appetite for a category (financial, reputational, compliance, WHS)
- User asks how to "link" appetite to the organisation's strategic objectives or Corporate Plan
- User asks for the difference between appetite, tolerance, and capacity
- User asks how appetite statements are used in practice
- User asks how to seek endorsement of an appetite statement from Council, the CEO, or the ARC

 

STRUCTURE

A risk appetite statement has four layers. Each layer is necessary. Skipping the lower layers produces aspirational language that cannot be used operationally.

Layer 1 Appetite principles: The high-level statements about the organisation's overall approach to risk. Typically four to eight principles. They set the tone and reference the strategic context. Example language: "The Council takes a conservative approach to risks that threaten the safety of residents, staff, or visitors, and has no appetite for reckless or negligent action."

Layer 2 Category statements: A statement for each risk category in the organisation's taxonomy. Each category has an appetite level (ranging from No Appetite, through Low, Moderate, to High) and a short narrative describing what that level means in practice. Categories typically align with the risk register categories: Strategic, Operational, Financial, Compliance and Legal, WHS, Reputational, Information and Cyber, Fraud and Integrity.

Layer 3 Tolerance thresholds: The quantitative or specific boundaries that give effect to the category statements. Expressed as numerical limits, rating thresholds, or specific decision rules. Example: "Financial risk appetite: Moderate. Tolerance: No single transactional risk with a residual loss exposure above $2 million without Executive Leadership Team endorsement. No aggregate uninsured exposure above $10 million."

Layer 4 Decision rules: How appetite is applied in practice. The operational rules that connect the appetite statement to day-to-day decision-making. Example: "Any risk assessed as Extreme must be escalated to the Executive Leadership Team within five working days. Any risk assessed as High that cannot be reduced to within appetite within six months must be escalated to the ARC."

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- Appetite is not the same as tolerance, and neither is the same as capacity. Appetite is what the organisation is willing to accept in pursuit of its objectives. Tolerance is the acceptable variation around appetite. Capacity is the maximum risk the organisation could absorb without failing. Confusing these terms produces unusable statements.
- Appetite varies by category. An organisation can simultaneously have no appetite for WHS risk and a moderate appetite for innovation risk. A single blanket appetite statement is usually a sign the work has not been done.
- Appetite must be endorsed at a level above management. For Queensland local governments, the appetite statement should be endorsed by Council (or at minimum by the Executive Leadership Team and noted by Council and the ARC). Without that endorsement, the statement is not authoritative.
- Appetite should drive decisions, not retrofit them. If the appetite statement is written to justify existing risk positions, it has no value. Write the appetite based on strategic priorities, then test the current risk register against it. The gaps are the areas requiring treatment or escalation.
- Link to the Corporate Plan. Every appetite principle and category statement should be traceable to a strategic priority or principle in the Corporate Plan. If a category statement cannot be traced to a strategic outcome, ask why the category exists.
- Use plain language. The appetite statement is read by councillors, independent ARC members, and operational managers. If it reads like a risk management textbook, it will not be used.
- The appetite statement should be short. A full appetite statement, including principles, category statements, tolerance thresholds, and decision rules, should fit in six to ten pages. Any longer and it becomes a reference document rather than a decision tool.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good appetite statement can be read by a Councillor or an ARC member in fifteen minutes, and at the end of those fifteen minutes they understand what the organisation is prepared to accept, what it is not, and how that translates into what gets escalated.

An appetite statement must avoid the following:
- Principles that do not distinguish the organisation from any other organisation ("We will manage risk prudently", "We will comply with all applicable laws")
- Category statements with appetite levels but no tolerance thresholds
- Tolerance thresholds without decision rules to give effect to them
- Qualitative-only statements for categories that can be quantified (financial, compliance)
- An appetite level that does not match the organisation's actual risk position (claiming "Low" appetite for an area where the residual risk register shows consistent High or Extreme ratings)
- A statement that has not been endorsed at the right level, or that has been endorsed but never revised

An appetite statement must include the following:
- A link to the strategic priorities of the Corporate Plan
- A category statement for every category in the risk taxonomy
- Tolerance thresholds for every category (quantitative where possible)
- Decision rules that connect the statement to escalation and approval pathways
- Evidence of endorsement, including the date and the endorsing body
- A review date, typically every two years or following a significant strategic change

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Risk appetite statements at Queensland local governments should align with the QAO Better Practice Guide: Risk Management and with ISO 31000:2018.

The appetite statement should be endorsed by Council (or, at minimum, by the Executive Leadership Team with noting by Council and the ARC). The ARC's oversight role under LGA s105 and LGR s208 to s211 includes providing assurance that the risk management framework (including the appetite statement) is effective.

For financial categories, the appetite statement should be consistent with the financial sustainability ratios and financial management strategy required under LGR Chapter 3. Inconsistency between the risk appetite and the financial management strategy is a common audit finding.

For WHS categories, the appetite statement should reference Council's obligations under the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld). The conventional position for local government is "No appetite" for WHS risk beyond residual operational exposure, consistent with the statutory duty.

For compliance and legal categories, the appetite statement should reference the obligation under the LGA (including s4 local government principles) to act lawfully and in accordance with the Act. The conventional position is "No appetite" for deliberate non-compliance, with limited tolerance for genuine interpretation-driven error.

For fraud and integrity categories, the appetite statement should reference the Crime and Corruption Act 2001 and the Council's fraud and corruption control framework. The conventional position is "No appetite" for fraud or corrupt conduct.

For reputational categories, the appetite statement should reference the Council's community engagement obligations under LGA s4(c), and should be written in a way that does not suggest the Council will suppress information or avoid legitimate public scrutiny.

ASIC and APRA guidance on risk appetite (published primarily for financial services entities) is often cited as a reference for structure and format, even though the regulatory framework does not apply to local government. Practitioners should treat that guidance as illustrative, not prescriptive.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Appetite principle: The Council is committed to the safety, wellbeing, and livelihood of its residents, staff, and visitors. The Council has no appetite for recklessness, negligence, or deliberate non-compliance with the law. The Council accepts that delivering its Corporate Plan requires it to take measured risks in service innovation, infrastructure investment, and community engagement, and will do so within the boundaries set out in this statement.

Category: WHS. Appetite level: No Appetite. Narrative: The Council has no appetite for residual WHS risk that is reasonably practicable to eliminate or further reduce. Any residual risk rated Medium or higher must be subject to an active treatment plan. Any residual risk rated High or Extreme must be escalated to the Executive Leadership Team within five working days of identification. Tolerance: No WHS incident resulting in a notifiable event under the WHS Act that has not been investigated and a prevention action closed out within 90 days. Decision rule: Any project or procurement with an inherent WHS rating of High or Extreme requires a WHS review by the Principal WHS Advisor before approval.

Category: Financial. Appetite level: Moderate. Narrative: The Council accepts moderate financial risk in pursuit of its infrastructure and service delivery priorities, within the boundaries set by its adopted Financial Management Strategy. The Council has low appetite for uninsured exposure to single catastrophic events, and no appetite for financial misstatement or material internal control weakness. Tolerance: No single transactional exposure with a residual loss potential above $2 million without Executive Leadership Team endorsement. No aggregate uninsured exposure above $10 million. Decision rule: Any proposed transaction or project with a residual financial exposure above $2 million requires a specific risk assessment submitted to the Chief Financial Officer and noted at the next ARC meeting.

Category: Innovation and service delivery. Appetite level: Moderate to High. Narrative: The Council accepts that the delivery of new services, digital solutions, and partnerships requires the acceptance of delivery and transition risk. The Council will accept a residual rating of High for innovation initiatives where the potential community benefit justifies the exposure and where exit provisions are defined. Tolerance: No innovation initiative may proceed where failure could result in loss of an essential service without an agreed continuity arrangement. Decision rule: Innovation initiatives with a residual rating of High require documented acceptance at Executive Leadership Team level and are reported quarterly to the ARC.

Endorsement: Adopted by Council at the Ordinary Meeting of 15 June 2026. Next review: June 2028 or upon significant revision of the Corporate Plan.
