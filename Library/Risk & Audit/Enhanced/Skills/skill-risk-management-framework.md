SKILL: How to Write a Risk Management Framework

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Risk & Audit

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

A risk management framework is the structured set of principles, accountabilities, processes, and tools that an organisation uses to manage risk in a consistent way across all its activities. It is the architecture that holds together the risk appetite statement, the risk register, the risk assessment methodology, and the reporting arrangements. Without a framework, risk management becomes a collection of disconnected activities. This skill covers how to write a framework that is usable, appropriately sized for the organisation, and genuinely integrated with the way decisions are made.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "write" or "develop" a risk management framework
- User asks to "refresh" or "update" an existing framework
- User asks what should be "in" a risk management framework
- User asks how the framework relates to the risk appetite statement, the risk register, the business continuity plan, or the fraud control framework
- User asks for a "risk management policy" or a "risk management strategy" (often used interchangeably with framework)
- User asks how to align the framework with ISO 31000 or with the QAO guidance
- User asks how to get the framework endorsed

 

STRUCTURE

A risk management framework has nine sections. The order matters. Each section builds on the ones before it.

Section 1 Purpose and scope: What the framework covers, who it applies to, and how it fits with other frameworks (fraud and corruption control, business continuity, WHS, information security). Short. One to two pages.

Section 2 Principles: The principles that underpin risk management in the organisation. Typically aligned with ISO 31000:2018 (integrated, structured and comprehensive, customised, inclusive, dynamic, uses best available information, considers human and cultural factors, continual improvement). State the principles and explain briefly how each applies in the organisation's context.

Section 3 Governance and accountabilities: Who does what. Roles and responsibilities of Council, the ARC, the CEO, Executive Leadership Team, Chief Risk Officer (or equivalent), risk owners, control owners, internal audit, and staff generally. This section is typically the most important and the most frequently referenced. Use a responsibility matrix where helpful.

Section 4 Risk appetite: The summary appetite position, with a reference to the full Risk Appetite Statement as a separate endorsed document. The framework does not repeat the full appetite statement but establishes that appetite is the foundation for risk evaluation.

Section 5 Risk management process: The step-by-step process for identifying, analysing, evaluating, treating, monitoring, and reviewing risk. Aligned to ISO 31000. Include the source plus event plus consequence structure for risk descriptions. Include the risk matrix (likelihood and consequence scales with descriptors).

Section 6 Risk categories and taxonomy: The organisation's risk taxonomy. Categories and subcategories. Consistent with the register and with the appetite statement.

Section 7 Reporting and escalation: How risks move through the organisation. The reporting cadence at each level (operational, divisional, executive, ARC, Council). The escalation thresholds that trigger upward reporting. The format of standard reports.

Section 8 Integration with other frameworks: How this framework interacts with others. Where the boundaries are. Where handoffs occur. Typically: fraud and corruption control, business continuity, WHS, information security and cyber, procurement, project management, insurance.

Section 9 Review and improvement: How the framework is kept current. Review cadence (typically two-yearly for the framework, annually for the appetite statement, continuously for the register). Maturity assessment approach. Who endorses what.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- Match the framework to the organisation. A framework that would work for a small Council is unlikely to work for a large one, and a framework borrowed unchanged from another Council is unlikely to reflect the actual governance and decision-making arrangements in the receiving organisation. Customise.
- The framework is not the appetite statement, the register, or the process documents. It is the architecture that connects them. A framework that tries to be all of those things in one document becomes too large to be useful.
- Governance and accountabilities are the heart of the framework. Most risk management failures are not methodology failures; they are accountability failures. The framework must make it clear who decides, who does, who reviews, and who escalates.
- Integration is more important than completeness. A framework that covers every topic but does not explain how risk management interacts with existing decision-making processes will be ignored. A framework that is narrower but is genuinely embedded in how the organisation operates will be used.
- The framework should speak to non-specialists. Operational managers, councillors, and ARC members should be able to read the framework and understand what it requires of them. If it can only be understood by the risk team, it has failed.
- The framework should support, not replace, the exercise of judgement. Risk management is a discipline, not an algorithm. The framework should make good judgement easier by providing structure, not harder by adding compliance overhead.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good risk management framework can be read by an Executive Director or an ARC member in thirty minutes, and at the end of that reading they know who does what, how risks are assessed and escalated, and how this framework connects to their existing accountabilities.

A framework must avoid the following:
- Being written in ISO 31000 language throughout, without translation into the organisation's own context
- Extensive verbatim reproduction of ISO 31000 principles and process steps
- Accountabilities expressed in passive voice ("risks will be managed") rather than by named role
- A risk management process with so many steps and templates that no one actually follows it
- Exhortations about "embedding" or "maturity" without operational detail
- Being longer than 40 pages including appendices (a framework should be usable, not exhaustive)

A framework must include the following:
- A clear statement of scope and purpose
- Principles that reflect ISO 31000 but are applied to the organisation
- A governance and accountabilities section with named roles, not just general references to "management"
- A risk management process consistent with ISO 31000 and proportionate to the organisation
- A taxonomy aligned with the register and appetite statement
- Reporting and escalation arrangements with clear thresholds
- Interfaces with related frameworks
- Review and improvement arrangements

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Risk management frameworks at Queensland local governments should align with ISO 31000:2018 and with the QAO Better Practice Guide: Risk Management.

The framework should be endorsed by Council, consistent with the Council's role under the LGA. The Executive Leadership Team typically develops and reviews the framework, the ARC reviews and endorses it on behalf of Council, and Council formally adopts it. The framework should be a public document (subject to any specific confidentiality considerations).

The framework should explicitly reference LGR s207 (internal audit) and the requirement for the internal audit function to examine the organisation's risks and the controls adopted to manage them. This creates a direct link between the risk management framework and the internal audit function.

The framework should reference the oversight role of the ARC under LGA s105 and LGR s208 to s211. The ARC is the primary governance body for risk management at Councils with a committee.

The framework should integrate with the fraud and corruption control framework required by good practice and supported by the CCC's guidance. Where the Council has a separate fraud and corruption control framework, the risk management framework should define the boundary and the handoff.

The framework should integrate with WHS arrangements under the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld). WHS risk is typically managed through the WHS management system, with summary-level consolidation into the operational risk register.

The framework should integrate with business continuity management, which for Queensland local governments is often driven by the Disaster Management Act 2003 (Qld) obligations in relation to Local Disaster Management Groups. The LDMP and the business continuity framework are typically separate but must be consistent.

For Gold Coast and other larger councils, the framework should reflect the strategic contracting arrangements under LGR Chapter 6 Part 2. The procurement risk category should acknowledge that procurement risk is managed through the Contracting Plan, Significant Contracting Plans, and Contract Manual, with consolidated reporting into the operational risk register.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Purpose and scope: This framework describes how the [Council name] manages risk across all of its activities. It applies to all staff, contractors, and controlled entities, and to the delivery of the Corporate Plan, the Operational Plan, and the capital works program. It does not replace the WHS management system, the fraud and corruption control framework, the information security framework, or the disaster management plan, each of which operates in its own right. The framework defines how these separate systems feed into and receive from the consolidated risk position.

Governance: The Council adopts the framework. The Audit and Risk Committee, under the authority delegated by Council and consistent with LGA s105, provides independent assurance that the framework is effective and recommends updates as required. The Chief Executive Officer is accountable to Council for the operation of the framework. The Executive Leadership Team, chaired by the CEO, provides executive oversight and reviews the strategic risk register at least quarterly. The Chief Risk Officer (Manager Risk and Internal Audit) is responsible for the design and administration of the framework, maintains the strategic risk register, coordinates risk reporting, and provides advice to the CEO, the ELT, and the ARC. Risk owners are the named Executive or Manager accountable for managing each risk; they have the authority to act and are responsible for ensuring the risk is treated, monitored, and reported. Control owners are the named Manager or Senior Officer responsible for the operation of each control. Staff generally are responsible for managing risk in their day-to-day activities consistent with this framework.

Reporting and escalation: Operational risks are reviewed monthly at Manager level. Divisional risks are reviewed quarterly at Director level. Strategic risks are reviewed quarterly by the ELT and half-yearly by the ARC. Any risk moving to High or Extreme residual rating is escalated to the ELT within five working days. Any risk with a residual rating outside appetite that cannot be brought within appetite within six months is escalated to the ARC with a treatment plan.

Endorsement: Adopted by Council on 15 June 2026. Next review: June 2028.
