SKILL: How to Build and Maintain a Risk Register

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Risk & Audit

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

A risk register is the structured record of an organisation's risks, the controls in place to manage them, and the treatments in train to bring residual risk within appetite. For Queensland local governments, the register sits at the centre of the risk management system. Every risk the organisation has identified, assessed, or is actively managing should be capable of being traced to the register. This skill covers how to build a register from scratch, how to maintain it over time, and how to keep it useful rather than letting it drift into a compliance artefact that nobody reads.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "build" or "set up" a risk register
- User asks to "maintain" or "refresh" an existing risk register
- User asks how to "structure" risk data across an organisation, division, or project
- User asks for the "fields" or "columns" a risk register should contain
- User asks how to "consolidate" multiple risk registers
- User asks how to "review" a risk register for quality
- User asks how to "link" the risk register to the internal audit plan, business continuity plan, or strategic plan

 

STRUCTURE

A risk register has two layers: the register structure (what fields it captures) and the register hierarchy (how risks relate to each other). Both matter.

Register fields (minimum):

Risk ID: A unique identifier. Sequential (R001, R002) or structured (STRAT-001, OP-014, PROJ-2026-003).

Risk description: Written in source plus event plus consequence format. One to three sentences.

Risk category: Aligned to the organisation's risk taxonomy. Typical categories for local government: Strategic, Operational, Financial, Compliance and Legal, WHS, Reputational, Information and Cyber, Project, Fraud and Integrity.

Risk owner: The named position (not name) accountable for managing the risk. Must have the authority to act.

Inherent likelihood and consequence: The rating before controls. Documented reasoning required.

Existing controls: Specific, testable, named controls. Owner of each control. An indication of whether the control is preventive, detective, or corrective.

Control effectiveness: An assessment of how well the existing controls are working. Common scales: Effective, Partially Effective, Ineffective. Supported by evidence (control testing, audit results, incident history).

Residual likelihood and consequence: The rating after controls. Documented reasoning required.

Risk appetite position: Whether the residual risk is within or outside the organisation's appetite for that category.

Treatment plan: Additional actions required to bring the risk within appetite, or to further reduce risk where appetite is silent. Each treatment has an owner, a target date, and a status.

Monitoring arrangements: How the risk is monitored, at what cadence, by whom, and where the monitoring results are reported.

Last reviewed: Date, reviewer, and outcome of the review.

Register hierarchy:

Strategic risks: The organisation-wide risks that threaten the delivery of the Corporate Plan or the long-term viability of the Council. Typically 15 to 25 risks. Owned at Executive Leadership Team level.

Operational risks: Risks within a business unit, service, or process. Typically owned at Manager or Senior Officer level. Can be consolidated into a divisional register.

Project risks: Risks associated with specific projects, programs, or major procurements. Maintained in the project's own risk log and escalated to the operational or strategic register if they meet the threshold.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- A register is a working tool, not a compliance artefact. If it is not being used to drive decisions, it is not doing its job. The test is whether executives and operational managers refer to it in the ordinary course of their work, or only when prompted by risk management or audit.
- Every risk must be expressed in source plus event plus consequence format. "Cyber attack" is not a risk, it is a category. "Unpatched external-facing web server allows attacker to access resident data, resulting in notifiable data breach and Information Commissioner action" is a risk.
- A register without documented control effectiveness is a list of concerns, not a risk register. The effectiveness rating is what turns raw risk data into something an ARC or Executive can act on.
- The inherent and residual ratings should almost always be different. If they are the same, either the controls are doing nothing or the controls have not been properly considered.
- Do not confuse risks with issues. An issue has already occurred and is being managed as a matter of fact. A risk is something that might occur.
- Do not confuse risks with causes or consequences. A single risk has one source, one event, and one consequence. If you find yourself writing a risk with multiple "or" statements, split it.
- Every risk must have a living owner. When a person leaves a role, the risk owner changes to the new occupant; the register is updated the week of the change, not at the next annual review.
- Every High or Extreme residual risk should be reviewed at Executive level at least quarterly. Medium risks at least six-monthly. Low risks at least annually.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good risk register is the single source of truth for the organisation's risk position. It is complete, current, consistent in format, and readable by an informed non-specialist.

A register must avoid the following:
- Risks written as single words or categories rather than structured descriptions
- Generic controls listed by name only ("management oversight", "policy", "training") without a description of how they operate
- Ratings without reasoning
- Treatments without owners or dates
- A register that has not been reviewed in the last twelve months
- Duplicate risks (the same underlying risk appearing in multiple divisional registers with different wording)
- Risks that have never been closed, even though the original source or event is no longer present

A register must include the following:
- A documented risk taxonomy (categories and subcategories)
- A documented risk matrix (likelihood and consequence scales with descriptors)
- A documented risk appetite statement for each category
- Source plus event plus consequence descriptions
- Inherent and residual ratings with reasoning
- Specific, testable controls with owners
- Documented control effectiveness
- Treatments with owners, dates, and status
- Evidence of recent review

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Risk registers at Queensland local governments should align with ISO 31000:2018 as the underlying methodology and with the QAO Better Practice Guide: Risk Management.

The obligation to maintain a documented view of risk flows from LGR s207, which requires the internal audit function to examine the organisation's risks and the controls adopted to manage them. Without a documented register, internal audit cannot do its job and the Council cannot demonstrate that it has considered its risks in a structured way.

The register should be reviewed at each Audit and Risk Committee meeting at a summary level, with the full register made available to the Committee at least annually. The ARC's role under LGA s105 and LGR s208 to s211 includes oversight of risk management.

Risks relating to fraud, corruption, or misconduct must be assessed against the reporting obligations under the Crime and Corruption Act 2001 (section 38). Where a risk crystallises into suspected corrupt conduct, the obligation to notify the CCC is mandatory and sits outside the risk register.

Risks relating to the handling of personal information must consider obligations under the Information Privacy Act 2009 (Qld), and risks relating to the release or withholding of information must consider obligations under the Right to Information Act 2009 (Qld).

Risks relating to work health and safety must align with the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld) and, where relevant, with the organisation's safety management system. WHS risks typically sit in a separate WHS risk management system and are consolidated at summary level into the operational risk register.

Risks relating to cultural heritage on Council land must consider the Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2003 (Qld).

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Risk ID: STRAT-008

Risk description: Unplanned failure of the wastewater treatment network during a high rainfall event, resulting in untreated discharge to the Broadwater, Department of Environment and Science enforcement action, remediation cost, and community impact.

Risk category: Operational (environmental).

Risk owner: Director Water and Waste.

Inherent likelihood: Likely (5). Reasoning: The network operates at 78% of capacity on average; rainfall events exceeding 100mm in 24 hours have occurred in three of the last five years; two near-miss events recorded in 2024 and 2025.

Inherent consequence: Major (4). Reasoning: Regulatory action and remediation costs in similar events at other councils have exceeded $15 million. Reputational and environmental consequences substantial.

Inherent rating: High.

Existing controls:
- C1 (Preventive) Capacity management protocol activated at 85% capacity. Owner: Manager Network Operations. Tested monthly.
- C2 (Preventive) Backup pump station at northern interceptor. Owner: Manager Network Assets. Maintained under asset management plan.
- C3 (Detective) Real-time monitoring of discharge points with automatic alerting. Owner: Manager Network Operations.
- C4 (Corrective) Emergency response plan activated within 30 minutes of alert. Owner: Manager Network Operations. Exercised annually.

Control effectiveness: Partially effective. Reasoning: 2025 internal audit found the capacity management protocol is activated consistently but the backup pump station has an availability of 87% against a target of 99%, and the emergency response exercise has not been conducted in the last 18 months.

Residual likelihood: Possible (3). Residual consequence: Major (4). Residual rating: High.

Risk appetite position: Outside appetite. The Council's Risk Appetite Statement for environmental risk allows residual exposure up to Medium.

Treatments:
- T1 Upgrade backup pump station to achieve 99% availability. Owner: Manager Network Assets. Target: 30 November 2026. Status: In progress. $1.8 million in 2026 to 2027 capital program.
- T2 Conduct emergency response exercise and refresh plan. Owner: Manager Network Operations. Target: 30 June 2026. Status: Scheduled.

Monitoring arrangements: Monthly operational performance report to Director. Quarterly summary to Executive Leadership Team. Half-yearly detailed review at Audit and Risk Committee. Environmental incidents reported through the incident management system and escalated immediately.

Last reviewed: 12 March 2026, Chief Risk Officer, rating confirmed, treatment progress noted.
