LEGISLATION EXTRACT: Local Government Act 2009 (Qld) — Governance, Internal Audit and Audit Committee Provisions

JURISDICTION: Queensland

DOMAIN: Risk & Audit

LEVEL: Base

CURRENCY: Based on the Act current as at early 2026. Users should verify section numbers against legislation.qld.gov.au before relying on this extract for advice. Local government legislation is amended regularly.

 

PURPOSE

This extract consolidates the provisions of the Local Government Act 2009 (Qld) that relate to governance, internal audit, and audit committees. It is designed to give a practitioner the relevant statutory text and annotations without requiring them to read the full Act. The related provisions of the Local Government Regulation 2012 are in a separate extract.

 

1. LOCAL GOVERNMENT PRINCIPLES (SECTION 4)

Section 4 establishes the five local government principles that underpin the Act. Every action taken under the Act must be consistent with these principles.

Text summary (s4(2)):

(a) transparent and effective processes, and decision-making in the public interest

(b) sustainable development and management of assets and infrastructure, and delivery of effective services

(c) democratic representation, social inclusion and meaningful community engagement

(d) good governance of, and by, local government

(e) ethical and legal behaviour of councillors, local government employees and councillor advisors

Practitioner note: These principles are frequently cited in audit findings and ARC papers as the high-level reference point for governance issues. Where a control weakness or policy gap relates to transparency, decision-making, or ethical behaviour, cite s4 alongside the specific provision engaged.

 

2. RESPONSIBILITIES OF COUNCILLORS (SECTION 12)

Section 12 sets out what councillors are required to do. Key obligations include acting in the public interest, providing high-quality leadership, working with the CEO to ensure accountability, and ensuring the local government performs its responsibilities in a way that is consistent with the local government principles.

Practitioner note: Section 12 is the anchor for governance expectations at the councillor level. Audit findings that touch on councillor conduct, conflict of interest, or decision-making should reference this section together with the relevant code of conduct provisions.

 

3. RESPONSIBILITIES OF LOCAL GOVERNMENT EMPLOYEES (SECTION 13)

Section 13 sets out the responsibilities of local government employees, including the CEO. Key obligations include implementing the policies and decisions of the local government, carrying out the day to day management of the local government, and ensuring the local government discharges its responsibilities under the Act.

Section 13(1)(e) requires the CEO to comply with requests from councillors for advice to assist the councillor carry out their role, and for information the local government has access to relating to the local government.

Practitioner note: Audit findings involving failure to implement Council resolutions, weaknesses in management oversight, or failures in operational governance should cite s13.

 

4. CHIEF EXECUTIVE OFFICER (SECTIONS 193 AND 194)

Section 194 sets out the functions of the CEO, which include managing the local government in a way that promotes the effective and efficient implementation of the Council's policies, managing the other local government employees, and providing the local government with the advice it needs to make informed decisions.

Practitioner note: The CEO's accountability for internal controls, risk management, and the internal audit function flows from this section, together with s194(1)(c) of the LGR.

 

5. DELEGATIONS (SECTIONS 257, 258 AND 259)

Section 257 sets out the powers the local government may delegate to the mayor, the CEO, a committee of the local government, or a chairperson of a standing committee.

Section 258 sets out matters that cannot be delegated, including making rates and charges, making local laws, and adopting the budget.

Section 259 sets out the powers the CEO may delegate to other local government employees.

Practitioner note: All procurement delegations, payment approvals, and contract execution authorities flow from these sections. Audit findings relating to breach of delegation, unauthorised expenditure, or signatory authority should cite s257 (for Council delegations to CEO) or s259 (for CEO delegations to officers). Always verify the delegated officer's authority against Council's delegations register before relying on a decision they have made.

 

6. SOUND CONTRACTING PRINCIPLES (SECTION 104)

Section 104 establishes the financial management system of a local government and, in subsection (3), sets out the five sound contracting principles that must be applied to all contracting activity.

Text summary (s104(3)):

The sound contracting principles are:

(a) value for money

(b) open and effective competition

(c) the development of competitive local business and industry

(d) environmental protection

(e) ethical behaviour and fair dealing

Practitioner note: These five principles are the foundation of all local government procurement in Queensland. Every procurement policy, contract risk review, and audit finding relating to procurement must be capable of being tested against each of the five principles. The principles do not all carry equal weight in every procurement; Council must have regard to each one, and the weighting is a matter of judgement exercised within the Procurement Policy. See the separate extract on procurement provisions for the operational detail in the LGR.

 

7. AUDIT COMMITTEE (SECTION 105)

Section 105 provides the statutory foundation for audit committees at Queensland local governments. The section establishes that an audit committee is a committee of a local government that:

(a) monitors and reviews the integrity of the financial documents of the local government

(b) monitors and reviews the effectiveness and objectivity of the internal auditors of the local government

(c) makes recommendations to the local government about any matters that the committee considers need action or improvement.

The operational detail about audit committee composition, meetings, and procedures is in the Local Government Regulation 2012 (see separate extract).

Practitioner note: Only "large local governments" (those in remuneration category 3 or higher, or specifically listed in LGR Schedule 3A) are required to have an audit committee. City of Gold Coast is a large local government and is required to maintain an audit committee. The audit committee is an advisory committee to Council; it does not make decisions on behalf of Council and cannot bind Council.

 

8. INTERNAL AUDIT (SECTION 105 AND LGR)

The LGA requires local governments to establish an efficient and effective system of internal audit. The operational requirements (internal audit plan, reporting) are set out in LGR s207 and are covered in the related LGR extract.

 

9. INTEGRITY AND MISCONDUCT (SECTION 150 AND RELATED)

Section 150 and the related provisions in Chapter 5A deal with integrity, conduct, and misconduct of councillors. Where audit findings or ARC papers touch on councillor conduct, conflicts of interest, or inappropriate conduct, this Chapter is the relevant reference.

Practitioner note: Audit findings relating to councillor conduct must be handled with particular care, and typically require legal review before the finding is issued. The reporting pathways for misconduct (to the Office of the Independent Assessor) and corrupt conduct (to the Crime and Corruption Commission under the Crime and Corruption Act 2001, s38) are separate and mandatory.

 

10. CONFLICTS OF INTEREST FOR COUNCILLORS (SECTIONS 150EA TO 150EW)

Chapter 5B (inserted by amendment) sets out detailed obligations for councillors in relation to declarable and prescribed conflicts of interest, including when they arise, how they must be declared, and the consequences for decision-making.

Practitioner note: Audit work touching on Council decision-making should include a review of the conflict of interest declarations for the relevant meetings. Missing or late declarations are a frequent audit finding.

 

11. COMPLAINTS ABOUT LOCAL GOVERNMENT EMPLOYEES (SECTION 268)

Section 268 establishes the framework for complaints about the conduct of local government employees, including the requirement for Council to have an administrative complaints procedure.

Practitioner note: Where audit findings arise from a complaint or where issues identified during an audit may themselves constitute complaint matters, the complaint handling framework under s268 and Council's own policy applies.

 

RELATED LEGISLATION

The LGA does not stand alone. Practitioners should also be aware of:

- Local Government Regulation 2012 (Qld): the operational detail, particularly Chapter 6 (procurement) and Part 11 (internal audit and audit committees). See the separate LGR extract.
- Financial Accountability Act 2009 (Qld): while this Act primarily applies to State public sector entities, its principles of financial management influence the reading of LGA s104.
- Auditor-General Act 2009 (Qld): the basis for the Queensland Audit Office's audit of Queensland local governments. The QAO is the external auditor for every Queensland local government.
- Crime and Corruption Act 2001 (Qld): establishes the Crime and Corruption Commission and sets out the obligation (at s38) to notify suspected corrupt conduct.
- Public Records Act 2002 (Qld): governs the retention and disposal of local government records.
- Information Privacy Act 2009 (Qld) and Right to Information Act 2009 (Qld): apply to the information held and created by local governments.

 

QUEENSLAND AUDIT OFFICE GUIDANCE

Where practitioner guidance is needed on how the LGA requirements translate into practice, the QAO Better Practice Guides are the primary reference. The guides on Risk Management, Internal Audit, Governance, and Fraud Control are each relevant depending on the matter at hand.
