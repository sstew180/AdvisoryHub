LEGISLATION EXTRACT: Local Government Regulation 2012 (Qld) — Internal Audit and Audit Committee Provisions

JURISDICTION: Queensland

DOMAIN: Risk & Audit

LEVEL: Base

CURRENCY: Based on the Regulation current as at early 2026. Users should verify section numbers against legislation.qld.gov.au before relying on this extract for advice. Local government legislation is amended regularly.

 

PURPOSE

This extract consolidates the provisions of the Local Government Regulation 2012 (Qld) that relate to internal audit and audit committees. These are the operational provisions that give effect to the high-level requirements in the Local Government Act 2009. Part 11 of Chapter 5 of the Regulation is the key location for these provisions.

 

STRUCTURE OF THE REGULATION

Part 11 of Chapter 5 of the LGR deals with internal audit and audit of local government financial statements. It is organised as follows:

- Section 207: Internal audit
- Section 208: Application of Subdivision 2 (audit committee provisions)
- Section 209: Establishment of audit committee
- Section 210: Audit committee composition
- Section 211: Audit committee meetings
- Sections 212 to 213: External auditing and auditor-general's observation report

 

1. INTERNAL AUDIT (SECTION 207)

Section 207 requires every local government in Queensland to establish an efficient and effective internal audit function. The section sets out minimum requirements for what the internal audit function must have in place.

Core requirements include:

(a) A statement of the risks the internal audit function has examined, or proposes to examine, and the control measures the local government has adopted, or proposes to adopt, to manage those risks. This is typically documented in the internal audit strategy and the annual internal audit plan.

(b) A written plan for the internal audit function for each financial year, and a written plan for the internal audit function for the longer term (typically three years).

(c) A written procedure covering the conduct of the internal audit function.

The internal audit function must be carried out independently of the operational functions of the local government.

Practitioner note: Section 207 applies to every local government, not only large ones. Even a small shire council must have an internal audit function. The internal audit function can be insourced, outsourced, or co-sourced, but it must be genuinely independent of the operational areas it is reviewing. Independence is a key QAO focus area and a common cause of qualified internal audit arrangements.

Practitioner note on alignment with standards: While the LGR does not mandate alignment with the IIA International Standards for the Professional Practice of Internal Auditing, the Department of State Development, Infrastructure, Local Government and Planning and the QAO both strongly encourage alignment. Councils' internal audit charters typically reference the IIA Standards.

 

2. APPLICATION OF AUDIT COMMITTEE PROVISIONS (SECTION 208)

Section 208 sets out which local governments are required to have an audit committee. The audit committee requirements (in sections 209 to 211) apply to:

(a) Brisbane City Council

(b) A local government that is a "large local government"

"Large local government" is defined by reference to remuneration categories. A local government in remuneration category 3 or higher is a large local government for this purpose. Schedule 3A of the Regulation lists the relevant local governments.

Councils not listed as large are not required to have an audit committee under the LGR, although they are strongly encouraged to establish one. Many small councils choose to do so voluntarily.

Practitioner note: City of Gold Coast is a remuneration category 8 council (the highest) and is therefore a large local government. The audit committee requirements apply in full.

 

3. ESTABLISHMENT OF AUDIT COMMITTEE (SECTION 209)

Section 209 requires each large local government to establish an audit committee. The committee is established by Council resolution.

Practitioner note: The audit committee is a committee of the local government, established under the LGA's general power to establish committees. It is an advisory committee; it makes recommendations to Council but does not have decision-making power on behalf of Council.

 

4. AUDIT COMMITTEE COMPOSITION (SECTION 210)

Section 210 sets out the composition requirements for audit committees. Key requirements are:

(a) Minimum three members and maximum six members.

(b) At least one member must have significant experience and skills in financial matters.

(c) For councils other than Brisbane City Council, the committee must include at least one but no more than two councillors.

(d) The CEO cannot be a member of the committee, but may attend meetings.

(e) A person who is responsible for, or involved in, the local government's financial or internal audit functions cannot be a member (the independence principle).

Practitioner note: The Department recommends, and QAO supports, that the committee include at least one independent external member with significant financial experience. Many large councils appoint two or three independent members alongside the councillor members. The Chair of the audit committee is often (though not always required to be) an independent member, for reasons of independence and perceived objectivity.

Practitioner note: Where an audit committee is being refreshed or where a new member is being appointed, use a skills matrix to identify gaps. QAO's Insights on Audit Committees in Local Government specifically recommends this practice and includes an example matrix.

 

5. AUDIT COMMITTEE MEETINGS (SECTION 211)

Section 211 sets out the meeting and reporting requirements for audit committees. Key requirements are:

(a) The audit committee must meet at least twice each financial year.

(b) After each meeting, a report must be prepared about the matters reviewed at the meeting and the committee's recommendations.

(c) The report must be given to the CEO. The CEO must table the report at the next Council meeting for consideration by Council.

Practitioner note: Twice a year is the legislative minimum. Large councils typically meet four or more times a year to align with the external audit cycle (planning, interim, final, management letter) and the internal audit cycle. Gold Coast's audit committee meets more frequently than the minimum.

Practitioner note: The requirement for the CEO to table the committee's report at the next Council meeting is often overlooked. The report should be a standard agenda item after each committee meeting.

 

6. INTERNAL AUDIT AND AUDIT COMMITTEE RELATIONSHIP

The LGR does not prescribe the detail of the relationship between internal audit and the audit committee, but in practice the audit committee plays a central role in the oversight of the internal audit function. This typically includes:

(a) Reviewing and endorsing the internal audit plan (annual and longer-term).

(b) Monitoring delivery against the internal audit plan.

(c) Reviewing internal audit reports, findings, and management's response.

(d) Tracking the implementation of management actions in response to findings.

(e) Assessing the independence, resourcing, and effectiveness of the internal audit function.

Practitioner note: The audit committee's charter should make this relationship explicit. The QAO and the Department both publish examples. Queensland Treasury's Audit Committee Guidelines: Improving Accountability and Performance are frequently used as a template.

 

7. EXTERNAL AUDIT (SECTIONS 212 AND 213)

Sections 212 and 213 deal with the external audit of the local government's financial statements by the Auditor-General, and the requirement for the Auditor-General to prepare an observation report and the local government to present it.

Practitioner note: The Queensland Audit Office (QAO) is the external auditor for every Queensland local government, either directly or through a Contract Audit Service Provider acting on behalf of the Auditor-General. The audit committee's role in relation to external audit includes: reviewing the external audit plan, reviewing the draft financial statements before they are certified, reviewing the external auditor's report, and monitoring management's response to any findings in the management letter.

 

8. CONTROLLED ENTITIES (SECTIONS 213A AND 213B)

Sections 213A and 213B require the local government to give certain notices about controlled entities and to obtain copies of the audited financial statements of each controlled entity.

Practitioner note: Where a council has controlled entities (beneficial enterprises, corporatised entities, or other subsidiaries), the audit committee's remit typically extends to oversight of these entities' financial reporting and internal control environment.

 

9. RISK MANAGEMENT REQUIREMENT

The LGR does not contain a standalone risk management section of the same prominence as the internal audit and audit committee sections. However, s207 (internal audit) requires a statement of the risks the internal audit function has examined and the control measures adopted. This creates an implicit requirement for the local government to have a documented view of its risks.

In addition, QAO has reported that the regulatory framework requires councils to maintain a written record of their risks and control measures. Practitioners should treat a documented risk register and a risk management framework as essential to compliance with the LGA and LGR as a whole, not optional.
