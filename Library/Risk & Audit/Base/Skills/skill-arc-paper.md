SKILL: How to Prepare an ARC Paper

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Risk & Audit

LEVEL: Base

 

PURPOSE

An Audit and Risk Committee (ARC) paper is a structured document prepared for the Council's Audit and Risk Committee to inform, update, or seek a decision on a risk, audit, or governance matter. The ARC is a formal advisory body with a statutory foundation in the Local Government Regulation 2012, and papers going to it must meet a higher standard of rigour than routine internal documents.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "prepare a paper for the ARC" or "for the Audit and Risk Committee"
- User asks to "write an ARC report" or "draft an Audit Committee update"
- User asks for a "standing report" (risk update, internal audit status, fraud update, external audit update)
- User asks to "present findings" or "report outcomes" to the Audit and Risk Committee
- User asks to "prepare the risk section" or "the audit section" of an ARC agenda
- User asks to seek ARC endorsement, noting, or approval of a document, plan, or framework

 

STRUCTURE

Every ARC paper uses the same heading structure. The sections are fixed. Additional appendices can be attached but the core paper structure does not change.

Purpose: One sentence. What the paper is for (inform, endorse, approve, seek advice).

Recommendation: What the paper is asking the Committee to do. Phrased as a formal motion: "That the Audit and Risk Committee note the..." or "That the Audit and Risk Committee endorse the..." or "That the Audit and Risk Committee recommend to Council..." The recommendation must be the first substantive thing the Committee reads.

Background: Two to four sentences. The essential context for a Committee member who may not have been across the matter in detail. Reference prior ARC decisions on the same topic if relevant.

Key matters: The substance of the paper. This section is titled to reflect the topic (e.g. "Internal Audit Progress", "Enterprise Risk Update", "Fraud Incident Report"). Use short paragraphs and only use bullets for genuine lists (findings, risks, recommendations).

Risk and compliance implications: A specific statement of the risks the matter gives rise to, the risks to Council if the matter is not addressed, and any compliance or legislative implications (LGA, LGR, QAO, CCC, FAA).

Financial implications: The financial cost or exposure associated with the matter. If nil, state "Nil" and briefly explain why. Do not omit this section.

Next steps: What will happen after this paper. Who does what, by when, and what the Committee should expect to see back.

Attachments: List of supporting documents provided as annexures. Reference them by letter (Attachment A, Attachment B).

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- The ARC is a non-executive body. Its members are typically independent, with diverse backgrounds. Do not assume operational knowledge. Define acronyms on first use.
- The recommendation drives the paper. Write the recommendation first, then write the paper that supports it. If you cannot write a clear recommendation, the paper is not ready.
- Committees read at the board level. Dense paragraphs with specific numbers and dates are appropriate. Generic language and aspirational statements are not.
- Every claim must be defensible. If a Committee member asks "how do we know that", there must be a clear answer pointing to evidence, testing, data, or a cited source.
- Limit the paper to what the Committee needs to know to act. Detail belongs in attachments, not in the body.
- Do not use the paper to advocate. Present the facts, analysis, and options, and make a clear recommendation. The Committee decides.
- Use the Council's ARC paper template if one exists. The Committee Chair and Secretary will expect consistency across papers.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good ARC paper can be read in five minutes by a Committee member who has not been briefed in advance, and at the end of those five minutes they understand what is being asked of them, why, and what the consequences of their decision are.

A good ARC paper passes the "interruption test". If a Committee member stops the author mid sentence and asks "what's the number" or "which section of the Act" or "who signed that off", the author has an immediate answer drawn from the paper itself.

An ARC paper must avoid the following:
- Recommendations phrased as descriptions ("The Committee is asked to consider the attached report")
- Background sections that reprise history instead of framing the decision
- Bullet lists used as a substitute for analysis
- Financial implications sections that say "to be determined" or "not applicable" without explanation
- Risk sections that say "no material risk" without supporting reasoning

An ARC paper must include the following:
- A formal, action based recommendation
- Specific numbers, dates, and cited sources
- A risk and compliance section that names the actual risks and the actual legislative or policy instruments
- A financial implications section, even if the financial implication is nil
- Clear accountability for next steps

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

The Audit and Risk Committee at Queensland local governments is established under section 105 of the Local Government Act 2009 and section 207 to 212 of the Local Government Regulation 2012. Papers should reflect an understanding of the Committee's statutory role, which is to advise Council on financial statements, risk, internal control, and internal and external audit.

ARC papers should reference the QAO Better Practice Guide: Audit Committees where questions of Committee practice or remit arise.

ARC papers dealing with fraud, corruption, or misconduct matters should reference the CCC Corruption Prevention framework and must consider whether the matter requires reporting to the CCC under section 38 of the Crime and Corruption Act 2001. If reporting has occurred or is required, state this in the paper.

ARC papers dealing with internal audit matters should align with the IIA International Standards for the Professional Practice of Internal Auditing and with the Council's internal audit charter.

ARC papers dealing with external audit matters should be coordinated with the QAO engagement lead and should not pre empt QAO positions on audit findings or opinions.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Purpose: This paper seeks the Audit and Risk Committee's endorsement of the 2026 to 2028 Strategic Internal Audit Plan, prior to recommendation to Council.

Recommendation: That the Audit and Risk Committee endorse the 2026 to 2028 Strategic Internal Audit Plan (Attachment A), and recommend it to Council for adoption at the June 2026 Ordinary Meeting.

Background: The current Strategic Internal Audit Plan covers 2023 to 2025 and concludes on 30 June 2026. The Committee endorsed the development of a new three year plan at its December 2025 meeting (minute ARC 2025 12 04). The draft plan attached has been developed through consultation with the Executive Leadership Team and reflects the updated enterprise risk profile endorsed by the Committee in March 2026.

Financial implications: The plan is costed at $285,000 per annum, consistent with the existing internal audit operational budget. No additional funding is required. If the Committee wishes to include the optional ICT general controls review in Year 1, an additional $65,000 would be required, to be funded from the Corporate Services contingency.
