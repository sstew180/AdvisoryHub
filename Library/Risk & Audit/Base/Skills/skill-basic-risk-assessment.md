SKILL: How to Conduct a Basic Risk Assessment

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Risk & Audit

LEVEL: Base

 

PURPOSE

A basic risk assessment is the structured identification, analysis, and evaluation of risks affecting a specific activity, project, process, or decision. This skill covers the practitioner level approach that any officer should be able to conduct for their area of responsibility, without requiring specialist risk management expertise.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "do a risk assessment" or "assess the risks" of a project, activity, or decision
- User asks to "identify risks" associated with a proposal, process, or change
- User asks to "prepare a risk register" for a single project or initiative (use the enhanced register skill for organisation wide registers)
- User asks to "think through the risks" of a course of action
- User asks to "populate a risk assessment template"
- User asks to "rate" or "score" risks
- User asks how to prepare the risk section of a business case, briefing note, or Council paper

 

STRUCTURE

A basic risk assessment follows six steps. Each step must be completed before moving to the next.

Step 1 Establish the context. One short paragraph describing what is being assessed, what the objectives are, and what the scope includes and excludes. This anchors every subsequent step. Without a clear scope, the risk list will drift.

Step 2 Identify the risks. List the events that could prevent the objectives being achieved, or that could have unintended negative consequences. Use the source plus event plus consequence format: "Source of risk leads to event which results in consequence." Aim for 8 to 15 risks for a basic assessment. Fewer than 5 usually means the identification was too narrow. More than 20 usually means events and consequences have been confused.

Step 3 Analyse each risk. For each risk, assess the likelihood and the consequence using the organisation's risk matrix. If no matrix is specified, use a 5 by 5 matrix with likelihood levels (Rare, Unlikely, Possible, Likely, Almost Certain) and consequence levels (Insignificant, Minor, Moderate, Major, Severe). State the rating and the reasoning in one sentence each.

Step 4 Evaluate each risk. Compare the assessed rating against the organisation's risk appetite or tolerance. Risks above tolerance require treatment. Risks within tolerance are accepted and monitored. If no appetite statement exists, apply the convention that High and Extreme risks require treatment, Medium risks require monitoring, Low risks are accepted.

Step 5 Identify existing controls and treatment options. For each risk requiring treatment, list the controls already in place and whether they are effective. Then identify additional treatments using the hierarchy: avoid, reduce likelihood, reduce consequence, transfer, accept. Assign an owner and a target date for each treatment.

Step 6 Document and escalate. Record the assessment in the organisation's risk register or the project's risk log. Any risk rated High or Extreme after treatment should be escalated to the responsible executive and, where relevant, to the Audit and Risk Committee.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- Assess the inherent risk first (before controls), then the residual risk (with existing controls in place). The gap between the two tells you whether your controls are actually doing anything.
- Separate the source, the event, and the consequence. "Cyber attack" is not a risk. "Unpatched web server allows external attacker to access resident data, resulting in privacy breach and notifiable data breach obligation" is a risk.
- Do not confuse issues with risks. An issue is something that has already happened. A risk is something that might happen. If it has already occurred, it belongs in an incident report, not a risk register.
- A risk rating is a judgement, not a calculation. Document the reasoning, not just the score.
- Controls must be specific and testable. "Management oversight" is not a control. "Monthly reconciliation of the asset register to the finance system, performed by the Senior Finance Officer and reviewed by the Manager Finance" is a control.
- The person who owns the risk must have the authority to act on it. If the treatment requires decisions above the owner's delegation, the risk owner is at the wrong level.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good basic risk assessment can be read by someone who was not involved in the workshop or analysis, and they can understand what is being assessed, what the risks are, how serious they are, and what is being done about them.

A risk assessment must avoid the following:
- Risks phrased as single words or vague categories ("reputation", "compliance", "financial")
- Likelihood and consequence ratings without reasoning
- Controls listed by name only without a description of how they operate
- Treatments without owners or dates
- A risk list that looks identical to the last project's risk list (copy and paste is a red flag)

A risk assessment must include the following:
- A defined scope and set of objectives
- Risks written in source plus event plus consequence format
- Inherent and residual ratings with documented reasoning
- Specific, testable controls
- Treatments with owners, dates, and clear accountability

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Risk assessments at Queensland local governments should align with the QAO Better Practice Guide: Risk Management and should reference ISO 31000:2018 as the underlying methodology.

Where the risk assessment is for a matter going to Council, the risk section of the Council paper should summarise the High and Extreme risks only, with a reference to the full assessment held on file.

Where the risk assessment relates to fraud or corruption risk, refer users to the enhanced skill "How to Conduct a Fraud Risk Assessment" and to the CCC Corruption Prevention framework.

Where the risk assessment is for a capital project above the organisation's project risk threshold (commonly $1 million or $5 million depending on the Council), ensure it complies with the Council's project management framework and any Department of State Development, Infrastructure and Planning requirements that apply to funded projects.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Scope: This risk assessment covers the proposed migration of Council's customer request management system from the current on premise platform to a cloud hosted solution, scheduled for Q4 2026. It excludes the related changes to the customer facing website, which are covered under a separate assessment.

Risk: Incomplete data migration from the legacy system results in loss of historical customer request records, leading to an inability to respond to resident enquiries about ongoing matters and a potential Right to Information complaint.

Analysis: Inherent likelihood Possible, inherent consequence Major (rating High). The legacy system has 14 years of data, the schema has been modified three times, and similar migrations at other councils have experienced partial data loss.

Control: A full data integrity reconciliation will be performed after migration, comparing record counts, key field completeness, and a sample of 200 records across the date range. The reconciliation will be signed off by the Manager Customer Service before the legacy system is decommissioned.

Residual rating: Likelihood Unlikely, consequence Major (rating Medium). Accepted subject to the reconciliation being completed.

Treatment owner: Manager Customer Service. Target date: 15 October 2026.
