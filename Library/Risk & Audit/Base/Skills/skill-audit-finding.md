SKILL: How to Write an Audit Finding

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Risk & Audit

LEVEL: Base

 

PURPOSE

An audit finding is the structured written record of an issue identified during an internal or external audit. It describes what was expected, what was observed, the gap between the two, why it matters, and what should be done about it. A well written finding is the primary currency of the audit profession and must stand up to scrutiny from management, the Audit and Risk Committee, and external reviewers.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "write an audit finding"
- User asks to "draft a finding" or "document an issue" from an audit
- User asks to "write up" an observation, control weakness, or compliance gap
- User asks to "prepare audit report findings" or "structure audit results"
- User asks to "turn my notes into a finding"
- User asks for help converting fieldwork evidence into a written finding

 

STRUCTURE

Every audit finding follows the same five element structure. The order is fixed.

Criteria: The standard, policy, legislation, procedure, or expected practice. What should be happening. One or two sentences, with a specific reference to the source document, section, or requirement.

Condition: What was observed. The facts established through audit testing. One to three sentences. No interpretation, no opinion, only the evidence.

Cause: Why the gap exists. The underlying reason the condition occurred. Not speculation about motives, but a factual statement about what is missing, broken, or misaligned.

Consequence: The actual or potential impact if the condition is not addressed. Quantify where possible. Financial exposure, compliance breach, operational disruption, reputational risk.

Recommendation: What management should do to close the gap. Specific, assignable, time bound. One recommendation per finding unless multiple actions are genuinely separable.

A rating is usually assigned to the finding, typically High, Medium, or Low, based on the consequence and the likelihood of recurrence. Use the rating scale defined in the audit engagement's terms of reference. If none is defined, use the QAO convention of Significant, Moderate, Minor.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- Every finding must be supported by evidence on the working paper file. If you cannot point to the testing that established the condition, the finding is not ready to be written.
- Separate the facts from the interpretation. Condition is what you saw. Cause is why. Do not blend them.
- Write in the third person and the past tense for condition. Present tense for criteria and consequence.
- Do not name individuals. Findings address systems, processes, and controls, not people.
- If you cannot quantify the consequence, describe the specific exposure in concrete operational terms.
- The recommendation must be something management can actually do. If it requires a budget submission, say so. If it requires Council approval, say so.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good audit finding can be read by someone outside the audit team and understood without further explanation. It tells a complete story in five paragraphs or less.

A finding must pass the "where is that written or recorded" test. Every claim in the condition must be traceable to a working paper, a document, a system report, or a confirmed interview note. A finding that rests on "the auditor noted" without underlying evidence is not a finding, it is an opinion.

A finding must avoid the following:
- Vague criteria ("better controls are needed")
- Unsupported condition statements ("controls are weak")
- Speculative cause statements ("management does not prioritise this area")
- Generic consequences ("this could lead to risk")
- Recommendations that are not assignable ("controls should be improved")

A finding must include the following:
- A specific, cited source for the criteria
- A factual, evidenced condition
- A cause that identifies a specific missing or deficient element
- A consequence tied to a number, a compliance obligation, or an operational impact
- A recommendation with an owner (or a request that management nominate one) and a timeframe

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Audit findings at Queensland local governments should reference the QAO Better Practice Guide: Internal Audit where relevant, particularly for issues relating to audit methodology, independence, and reporting.

Findings relating to financial controls should reference the Local Government Regulation 2012 and the Financial Accountability Act 2009 where applicable.

Findings relating to fraud, misconduct, or corruption risk should reference the Crime and Corruption Commission's Corruption Prevention framework and should consider whether the matter meets the reporting threshold under the Crime and Corruption Act 2001.

Findings going to the Audit and Risk Committee should use the rating scale and reporting format adopted by that committee. If the committee uses the QAO convention (Significant, Moderate, Minor), apply that. If the committee has adopted a different scale, apply that.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Criteria: Council's Procurement Policy (section 4.2) requires that purchases above $15,000 and below $200,000 be supported by at least three written quotes, documented on the purchase requisition.

Condition: Testing of 30 purchase transactions between July and December 2025, selected from the finance system, identified 7 transactions above $15,000 where fewer than three written quotes were held on file. In 4 of the 7 cases, only a single quote was documented.

Cause: The purchase requisition form does not require quotes to be attached before approval, and the finance system does not reject requisitions with missing quote documentation.

Consequence: Purchases totalling $412,000 across the 7 transactions were approved without evidence of competitive pricing. This represents a breach of Council's Procurement Policy and exposes Council to value for money risk and potential audit qualification.

Recommendation: That the Chief Financial Officer require, by 30 June 2026, that the finance system be configured to prevent approval of purchase requisitions above $15,000 without evidence of the required number of quotes, and that the Procurement Policy be updated to reflect the system control.
