LEGISLATION EXTRACT: Local Government Act 2009 (Qld) and Local Government Regulation 2012 (Qld) — Procurement and Contracting Provisions

JURISDICTION: Queensland

DOMAIN: Contract Management

LEVEL: Base (with some Enhanced material where indicated)

CURRENCY: Based on the Act and Regulation current as at early 2026. Users should verify section numbers against legislation.qld.gov.au before relying on this extract for advice. Local government legislation is amended regularly.

 

PURPOSE

This extract consolidates the provisions of the Local Government Act 2009 (Qld) and the Local Government Regulation 2012 (Qld) that govern procurement and contracting by Queensland local governments. It is designed to give a practitioner the relevant statutory text and annotations without requiring them to read the full Act and Regulation. The procurement framework combines high-level principles in the LGA with operational detail in the LGR.

 

STRUCTURE OF THE PROCUREMENT FRAMEWORK

The Queensland local government procurement framework has a two layer structure:

- The Local Government Act 2009, section 104(3): the five sound contracting principles that apply to all procurement activity.
- The Local Government Regulation 2012, Chapter 6: the operational procedures, including default contracting procedures, strategic contracting procedures, and exceptions.

Councils choose between the "default contracting procedures" (the standard rules for quotes and tenders by contract value) and the "strategic contracting procedures" (an opt-in approach that allows a more flexible, plan-based approach). Most Queensland councils apply the default procedures; some larger councils have opted into strategic contracting.

Practitioner note for City of Gold Coast: City of Gold Coast adopted strategic contracting procedures in 2017 and operates under Chapter 6, Part 2 of the LGR (sections 217 to 222), not the default procedures in Part 3. This means the provisions in Section 6 below (strategic contracting) are the primary operating framework for Gold Coast's procurement, and the default contracting sections (Sections 3 to 5 below) are included for context and for dealings with other councils. Gold Coast's contracting plan, significant contracting plans, and contract manual established under strategic contracting are the operational reference points, together with Council's own Procurement Policy.

 

1. SOUND CONTRACTING PRINCIPLES (LGA SECTION 104(3))

Section 104 of the LGA establishes the financial management system of a local government. Subsection (3) sets out the five sound contracting principles that every local government must have regard to in all procurement activity.

Text summary (s104(3)):

The sound contracting principles are:

(a) value for money

(b) open and effective competition

(c) the development of competitive local business and industry

(d) environmental protection

(e) ethical behaviour and fair dealing

Practitioner note: These principles are not in any order of importance, and not every principle will carry equal weight in every procurement. The procuring officer must have regard to each one and must be able to demonstrate that consideration. "Value for money" is not the same as lowest price; it includes consideration of fitness for purpose, quality, service support, warranty, whole-of-life cost, and contribution to the other principles.

Practitioner note: Audit findings relating to procurement will typically test whether each of the five principles was considered and documented. Procurement files should include evidence of that consideration, not just the evaluation scoring.

 

2. PROCUREMENT POLICY (LGR SECTION 198)

Section 198 of the LGR requires each local government to prepare and adopt a procurement policy. The policy must:

(a) be reviewed annually

(b) include the principles that apply to all procurement activity by the local government

(c) give effect to the sound contracting principles in LGA s104(3)

Practitioner note: The procurement policy is a public document and is frequently the subject of external review. It should be cited in every contract risk review and contract management plan. The annual review requirement is often missed; check the adoption date before relying on the policy.

 

3. DEFAULT CONTRACTING PROCEDURES (LGR CHAPTER 6, PART 3)

Practitioner note for City of Gold Coast: Gold Coast operates under strategic contracting (Part 2, see Section 6 below), not the default procedures. The following summary of default procedures is retained for context, for dealings with other councils, and because the underlying concepts (value bands, tender thresholds) influence how strategic contracting plans are usually structured.

The default contracting procedures are the standard rules for entering into contracts for goods and services. They are based on the expected value of the contract over its life (not just the immediate purchase price).

Contract value bands under the default procedures:

(a) Small contractual arrangements: below the threshold for written quotes (Council's procurement policy usually sets this, commonly at $15,000 or $25,000). Minimum process, usually a single quote.

(b) Medium-sized contractual arrangements: contracts above the small threshold but below $200,000. Three written quotes are required. LGR s224.

(c) Large-sized contractual arrangements: contracts at or above $200,000. A public tender process is required. LGR s228.

Practitioner note: The $200,000 threshold is the tender threshold for Queensland local governments. Any contractual arrangement at or above this value requires a public tender under the default procedures, unless an exception applies.

Practitioner note: The expected value of a contract includes the total value of all related contracts with the same supplier during the financial year, or over the full term of the arrangement. LGR s224(4) and equivalent provisions. Splitting contracts to avoid the threshold is a significant audit and integrity risk.

 

4. TENDER PROCESS (LGR SECTION 228)

Section 228 sets out the minimum requirements for a public tender under the default contracting procedures. Key requirements include:

(a) The local government must invite written tenders by advertisement.

(b) The advertisement must allow a reasonable time for tenders to be submitted (typically no less than 21 days).

(c) The local government must consider each conforming tender received.

(d) The local government must apply the sound contracting principles in evaluating the tenders.

Practitioner note: Most Queensland councils use an electronic tender portal (commonly referred to as a "tenderbox") for all tender processes, consistent with s228. Tenders submitted through any other method (email, hard copy, fax) are generally not accepted. This should be stated clearly in the tender documentation.

Practitioner note: Late tenders under the default procedures must generally be rejected. A single exception is usually only permitted where the delay was caused by the local government itself (e.g. an outage of the tender portal), and this is rare and should be carefully documented.

 

5. EXCEPTIONS TO THE DEFAULT PROCEDURES (LGR SECTIONS 229 TO 235)

The LGR sets out a number of specific exceptions where a council may enter into a contract without following the default quote or tender process. The main exceptions are:

(a) Quote or tender consideration plan prepared: LGR s229. A resolution of Council to enter into a specific contract, supported by a consideration plan.

(b) Contractor on approved contractor list: LGR s230 and s231. Where the council has established an approved contractor list through an expression of interest process, contracts may be awarded from that list.

(c) Register of pre-qualified suppliers: Used where the goods or services are specialised or where ongoing relationships are required.

(d) LGA arrangements (Local Buy): LGR s234. Where a contract is entered into under an arrangement established by the Local Government Association of Queensland's Local Buy, the default procedures do not apply.

(e) Emergency purchases: LGR s235 and related. Where there is an immediate need, limited to the extent of the emergency.

(f) Contracts with government agencies: Procurement from another government entity.

(g) Sole supplier: Where the goods or services are only available from a single supplier.

(h) Auctions and second-hand goods: Specific exceptions under the Regulation.

Practitioner note: Every use of an exception must be documented. The file should record the exception relied on, the reasoning, and the evidence supporting the use (e.g. confirmation that the supplier is on an approved list, confirmation that Local Buy terms have been accessed, documentation of the emergency circumstances).

 

6. STRATEGIC CONTRACTING PROCEDURES (LGR CHAPTER 6, PART 2, SECTIONS 217 TO 222)

A local government may resolve to adopt strategic contracting procedures instead of the default procedures. Strategic contracting is a plan-based approach that allows a more flexible response to market conditions, in exchange for more structured upfront planning.

City of Gold Coast adopted strategic contracting in 2017. For Gold Coast officers, the sections below describe the operating framework. For officers dealing with other Queensland councils, the default procedures in Sections 3 to 5 are likely to apply.

Key requirements under strategic contracting include:

(a) Power to choose the strategic approach: LGR s217 allows a local government to resolve to take a strategic approach to its contracting. The resolution engages Part 2 and displaces the default procedures in Part 3.

(b) Contracting plan: LGR s220. The contracting plan must identify the types of contracts the local government proposes to enter into, the principles and strategies for performing those contracts, proposed delegations for entering into contracts, and a market assessment for each type of contract. The contracting plan must also identify contracts the local government considers to be "significant contracts".

(c) Significant contracting plan: LGR s221. For each contract identified as significant in the contracting plan, the local government must prepare a significant contracting plan that addresses the specific risks, market dynamics, and governance arrangements for that contract.

(d) Contract manual: LGR s222. A written manual setting out the procedures the local government will follow when entering into contracts under the strategic approach. The contract manual is the operational reference document for officers.

Practitioner note: Under strategic contracting, there is no default $200,000 tender threshold triggering a public tender. Instead, the contracting plan and the contract manual set out the process for each category of contract. This does not remove the obligation to test the market; it replaces the prescriptive threshold with a planned approach documented in advance.

Practitioner note: The sound contracting principles in LGA s104(3) continue to apply in full under strategic contracting. The five principles are not displaced by the strategic approach; they are the underlying obligation that the contracting plan and contract manual must give effect to.

Practitioner note: The publication obligation in LGR s237 (publishing details of contracts worth $200,000 or more) applies regardless of whether the council uses default or strategic contracting.

Practitioner note for City of Gold Coast: When preparing a contract risk review, contract management plan, or audit finding, the primary reference documents are Council's Procurement Policy, the Contracting Plan, any applicable Significant Contracting Plan, and the Contract Manual. Cite these alongside LGR Part 2 and LGA s104(3). Do not cite the default procedure sections (s224, s228) as though they were binding on Gold Coast procurement, because they are not.

 

7. PUBLISHING DETAILS OF CONTRACTS (LGR SECTION 237)

Section 237 requires the local government to publish details of each contractual arrangement worth $200,000 or more (exclusive of GST), as soon as practicable after entering into the arrangement. The details must include:

(a) the person with whom the contract has been entered into

(b) the value of the contract

(c) the purpose of the contract

Practitioner note: The publication requirement is usually met by publishing on the council's website. Audit findings sometimes arise where the publication is incomplete, delayed, or missing. Contract management plans should specify who is responsible for ensuring publication occurs.

 

8. ENTERING INTO A CONTRACT UNDER A DELEGATION (LGR SECTION 257 LINK)

A contract is entered into on behalf of the local government by an officer exercising a delegation made under LGA s257 (for Council delegations to CEO) or LGA s259 (for CEO delegations to officers). The delegation must cover the value of the contract.

Practitioner note: Before signing any contract, verify:

(a) The officer has the relevant delegation.

(b) The delegation covers the contract value including all extensions, options, and variations.

(c) The delegation has not been suspended or revoked.

(d) The procurement process has been followed or a valid exception applies.

Contracts above the CEO's delegation require Council resolution. Attempting to sign a contract without the necessary delegation is a significant governance failure and can expose the officer personally.

 

9. CONTRACTS FOR CONSTRUCTION WORK

Contracts for building or construction work are subject to additional provisions under the Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Act 2017 (Qld). This Act establishes statutory rights and obligations around payment claims, payment schedules, and adjudication of payment disputes.

Practitioner note: Construction contracts require specialist contract management knowledge. The payment claim and payment schedule provisions have strict statutory time frames that sit alongside the contract. Late responses to payment claims can result in the supplier's claimed amount becoming payable by default.

 

10. MODERN SLAVERY, PRIVACY, AND RELATED OBLIGATIONS

Contracts entered into by local governments are also subject to obligations under:

- Modern Slavery Act 2018 (Cth): reporting requirements for entities with consolidated revenue at or above the reporting threshold, typically including large councils.
- Information Privacy Act 2009 (Qld): contracts involving the handling of personal information must include privacy obligations on the supplier.
- Right to Information Act 2009 (Qld): contract documents may be subject to RTI applications.
- Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld): obligations on Council as a Person Conducting a Business or Undertaking (PCBU) extend to contracted work.
- Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2003 (Qld): duty of care for Aboriginal cultural heritage extends to contracted activity on relevant land.
- Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld): Council's decision-making must have regard to relevant human rights; procurement decisions are not exempt.

Practitioner note: These obligations do not change the core procurement framework but they must be addressed in the contract itself and in the contract management plan. Each is a potential audit finding if not addressed.

 

11. COMPETITIVE NEUTRALITY (LGR SECTION 133 AND RELATED)

Where a local government carries on a business activity, competitive neutrality obligations under the National Competition Policy may apply. This is relevant where the council itself is tendering for work in competition with the private sector.

Practitioner note: Competitive neutrality is a specialist area. Where a council's contracting activity involves a council business unit competing with external suppliers, or where the council is bidding for external work, obtain specialist advice.
