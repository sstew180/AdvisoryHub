SKILL: How to Write a Contract Management Plan Summary

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Contract Management

LEVEL: Base

 

PURPOSE

A Contract Management Plan (CMP) summary is a short, structured document that captures how a specific contract will be managed across its life. It is the operational companion to the contract itself. Where the contract sets out the legal obligations of each party, the CMP sets out how Council will discharge its obligations, oversee the supplier, and manage risks through the term. This skill covers the summary level plan suitable for most contracts. Complex or strategic contracts may warrant a full plan using the enhanced skill.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "prepare a contract management plan" or "CMP" for a specific contract
- User asks to "document how we're managing" a contract or supplier
- User asks to "set up" the management arrangements for a newly executed contract
- User asks to "prepare a handover" document for a contract being transferred to a new contract manager
- User asks what to "put in place" when a contract is signed
- User asks for a "contract summary" for operational use (as distinct from a legal summary)

 

STRUCTURE

A Contract Management Plan summary has nine sections. Each should be short (one paragraph or a small table) and operational in tone. The reader is typically the contract manager, their delegate, and anyone who might need to step in.

Contract identification: Title, parties, contract value, commencement date, expiry date, extension options, contract reference number.

Scope summary: What is being delivered, in plain language. Not a copy of the contract scope clause. Three to five sentences that a new contract manager could use to describe the contract to a colleague.

Key dates and milestones: A table of dates that matter. Commencement, key milestones or deliverables, payment schedule dates, reporting dates, renewal notice date, expiry date. Include the "notice before" date for any decision point, not just the date itself.

Roles and responsibilities: Who on Council's side owns the contract, who approves payments, who approves variations, who is the supplier's primary contact. Include position titles, not just names, so the plan survives staff changes.

Performance measures: How performance will be measured. List the KPIs or service levels drawn from the contract, the reporting cadence, and how failure to meet them will be handled.

Risk and controls: The key risks identified in the contract risk review, the treatments in place, and the owner of each treatment. If a contract risk review has not been completed, this is a prompt to do one.

Governance and meeting cadence: How the relationship will be governed. Frequency of operational meetings, frequency of strategic or executive reviews, whether a steering committee applies, and where meeting minutes will be held.

Documentation and records: Where the contract and associated records are held (contract register, document management system, shared drive), who has access, and what the retention requirements are.

Payment and variation approval: The payment schedule, the approval pathway for each invoice, the delegation required for variations, and the process for requesting and approving variations.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- The CMP is a working document, not a showpiece. If it lives in a drawer and is never opened, it has failed. Write it for the person who has to use it.
- Every date in the plan must have an owner. A date without an owner will be missed.
- The plan should survive the departure of the current contract manager. Write it so that someone stepping into the role can pick it up and understand how the contract is being run.
- Update the plan when the contract changes. A CMP that no longer reflects the signed agreement is worse than no plan at all because it gives a false sense of control.
- The plan should reference the contract, not repeat it. Use clause numbers. Keep the CMP short.
- Risks identified in the contract risk review must flow through to the CMP. If they do not appear here, they are not being managed.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good Contract Management Plan summary can be read in fifteen minutes by a new contract manager, and at the end of those fifteen minutes they know what the contract is, when the critical dates are, what the risks are, who is accountable for what, and where to find the supporting information.

A CMP must avoid the following:
- Long narrative descriptions of the scope when the contract itself is clearer
- Generic "roles" sections that list departments rather than specific position titles
- KPIs copied from the contract without the reporting cadence or failure response
- A risk section that does not name specific risks and treatments
- Dates listed without owners
- Plans that do not reference the contract clause numbers they relate to

A CMP must include the following:
- A clear date based action schedule with owners
- Named position titles for all key roles
- Specific performance measures with reporting cadence
- A risk summary that flows from the contract risk review
- A governance structure with meeting cadence
- Payment and variation approval pathways

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Contract Management Plans at Queensland local governments should align with the Queensland Government Contract Management Framework and the QGCPO Contract Management Guide.

The CMP must reflect Council's delegations register (made under section 257 of the Local Government Act 2009), particularly for payment approval and variation authority.

Record keeping requirements under the Public Records Act 2002 (Qld) apply to contract documentation. The CMP should specify the retention category and storage location consistent with Council's Records Management policy and the applicable General Retention and Disposal Schedule.

For contracts involving personal information, the CMP should reference the Information Privacy Act 2009 (Qld) obligations and specify how privacy obligations will be monitored.

For contracts involving work on Council land or infrastructure, WHS obligations under the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld) apply. The CMP should identify the Council officer responsible for WHS oversight of the contracted work.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Contract: Facilities Maintenance Services Agreement. Parties: City of Gold Coast and [Supplier]. Value: $4.2 million over 3 year base term. Commencement: 1 April 2026. Expiry: 31 March 2029. Extensions: 2 x 1 year options. Contract reference: CON 2026 018.

Key dates: Quarterly performance reviews on the 15th day of the month following quarter end. Annual contract review in March each year. Renewal notice date: 31 December 2028 (90 days before expiry). Owner for all dates: Contract Manager, Facilities Services.

Performance measures: Response times measured against the schedule in clause 8.2. Monthly performance reports due by the 10th of the following month. Service credits apply under clause 8.5 for any monthly score below 90%. First missed KPI triggers a written notice from the Contract Manager. Second consecutive missed KPI triggers escalation to the Executive Director Infrastructure.

Risk summary: Three risks identified in the contract risk review. Risk 1 (limitation of liability cap): escalated to Legal Services, negotiation outcome to be included in the executed amendment. Risk 2 (auto renewal notice): calendared, owned by Contract Manager, 90 day warning set for 30 September 2028. Risk 3 (CPI cap): monitored annually, reviewed at the annual contract review in March.
