SKILL: How to Review a Contract for Key Risks

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Contract Management

LEVEL: Base

 

PURPOSE

A contract risk review is a structured read of a contract (or a draft contract) to identify the clauses that expose Council to commercial, operational, legal, or reputational risk. This skill covers the practitioner level review that any officer managing a contract should be able to perform, without requiring legal qualifications. The review identifies the matters that need attention, escalation, or legal advice. It does not replace legal review.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "review a contract" or "check a contract" for risks
- User asks to "identify the risks" in a contract, agreement, MOU, or deed
- User asks to "summarise the key risks" of a supplier agreement
- User asks what to "watch out for" in a contract before signing or renewing
- User asks to "prepare a contract risk summary" for a manager, executive, or legal review
- User asks for help reading a contract as a non lawyer

 

STRUCTURE

A contract risk review covers seven areas, in order. Each area produces one short paragraph or a short list of identified risks. If there is nothing to flag in an area, state "No material risk identified" and move on.

Parties and authority: Who the parties are, whether the counterparty has the legal capacity to contract (company vs trust vs sole trader vs joint venture), and whether the signatory for Council has the delegated authority for the contract value.

Scope and deliverables: What is being bought or sold, how clearly it is described, and whether the scope is tight enough to avoid disputes about what is included. Vague scopes are the single largest source of contract disputes.

Term, commencement, and termination: When the contract starts, how long it runs, how it can be extended, and how either party can terminate. Pay particular attention to termination for convenience clauses, auto renewal, and notice periods.

Payment and price: How much is being paid, when, in what currency, how price escalation is handled, and whether there are hidden costs (minimum spend, early termination fees, price review clauses).

Risk allocation: How risk is allocated between the parties. The four clauses to identify are: indemnity, limitation of liability, insurance requirements, and warranty. These are the commercial risk backbone of the contract.

Performance and remedies: How performance is measured (KPIs, service levels, milestones), what happens if performance falls short (service credits, step in rights, suspension), and what dispute resolution pathway applies.

Compliance and statutory obligations: Data protection and privacy obligations, right to information implications, modern slavery declarations, work health and safety obligations, Aboriginal cultural heritage, environmental obligations, and any other statutory obligation that attaches to the subject matter of the contract.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- Read the contract top to bottom at least once before starting the risk review. Do not jump straight to the "important" clauses. Risk often lives in definitions, recitals, and cross references.
- Flag every clause you do not understand. Not understanding a clause is itself a risk. Mark it for legal review rather than guessing.
- Every risk flagged must point to a specific clause number. "The termination clause is weak" is not useful. "Clause 14.3 allows the supplier to terminate for convenience on 30 days' notice, with no obligation to complete in progress work" is useful.
- Risk allocation clauses (indemnity, limitation of liability, insurance, warranty) must always be read together. A strong indemnity is worthless if the limitation of liability cap is $1,000.
- A contract review is not an opinion on whether to sign. It is an identification of risks and recommendations for what to do about them (accept, negotiate, seek legal advice, escalate). The decision to sign belongs to the delegated officer.
- Where a clause is unusual, disadvantageous, or unclear, escalate to legal review. The cost of a legal opinion is always less than the cost of a disputed contract.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good contract risk review can be read by the delegated signatory in under ten minutes, and at the end of those ten minutes they know what they are being asked to sign, what the main risks are, and what they need to do about them before signing.

A contract risk review must avoid the following:
- Generic statements about risk with no clause reference
- Lists of every possible risk from a textbook rather than the risks actually present in the contract in front of the reviewer
- "Low risk" ratings without a reason
- Recommendations to "seek legal advice" as a default, without specifying why
- Silence on clauses the reviewer did not read or did not understand

A contract risk review must include the following:
- A specific clause reference for every identified risk
- A short plain language description of what the clause does and why it is a risk
- A rating or prioritisation (High, Medium, Low, or Critical, Significant, Minor) with reasoning
- A recommendation for each identified risk (accept, negotiate the clause, seek legal advice, add a risk treatment to the contract management plan)
- Confirmation that the delegated signatory has the authority for the contract value

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Contract reviews at Queensland local governments should apply the Queensland Procurement Policy and the Queensland Government Contract Management Framework. Where the contract is for building or construction work, the Queensland Government Building and Construction Contracts policy and the Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Act 2017 apply.

Contracts must be signed by an officer with the relevant delegation under Council's delegations register, made under section 257 of the Local Government Act 2009. Verify the delegation before signing. Contracts above the Chief Executive Officer's delegation require Council resolution.

Contracts involving the collection, storage, or disclosure of personal information must address the Information Privacy Act 2009 (Qld), including the Information Privacy Principles.

Contracts for services provided to residents or ratepayers should address the Right to Information Act 2009 (Qld), particularly where contract documents or performance data may be subject to RTI applications.

Contracts above $100,000 generally require a modern slavery statement from the supplier where the supplier falls within the scope of the Modern Slavery Act 2018 (Cth). Councils with procurement above the reporting threshold are themselves reporting entities.

Where the contract involves work on land, verify that Aboriginal cultural heritage obligations under the Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2003 (Qld) are addressed in the contract or in Council's management of the site.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Contract: Facilities Maintenance Services Agreement between City of Gold Coast and [Supplier], dated 15 March 2026, 3 year term with 2 x 1 year extension options, total contract value $4.2 million.

Risk 1 (High): Limitation of liability (clause 18.2) caps the supplier's total liability at the fees paid in the 12 months preceding the claim, with no carve out for indemnified matters. Combined with the narrow indemnity in clause 17.1 (third party property damage only), Council's recovery options in the event of a major service failure are limited to approximately $1.4 million. Recommendation: Negotiate an uncapped liability carve out for breach of confidentiality, privacy, and WHS obligations.

Risk 2 (Medium): Auto renewal clause (clause 5.4) extends the contract for a further 12 months unless Council gives 90 days' notice before the expiry of each term. Recommendation: Calendar the notice date and assign ownership to the Contract Manager. Add to the Contract Management Plan.

Risk 3 (Low): Price review clause (clause 9.3) permits CPI adjustment annually, capped at 3.5%. Current CPI is trending above this cap. Recommendation: Accept. Monitor annually.

Authority: Contract value ($4.2 million over base term) exceeds the Executive Director's delegation ($2 million). Council resolution required.
