SKILL: How to Escalate a Contract Performance Issue

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Contract Management

LEVEL: Base

 

PURPOSE

Contract performance issues rarely resolve themselves. When a supplier is missing KPIs, delivering late, breaching specifications, or otherwise failing to meet the terms of the contract, the contract manager must escalate in a structured and documented way. Escalation is the process of moving an issue from operational management into formal contractual territory, where the contract's remedies and dispute resolution mechanisms become available. This skill covers how to do that well, so that Council's position is preserved, the supplier has a clear opportunity to correct, and any subsequent contractual action (service credits, suspension, termination, dispute) rests on defensible ground.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks how to "escalate" a contract issue
- User asks how to "put a supplier on notice" or "issue a show cause notice"
- User asks how to "document" poor supplier performance
- User asks what to "do about" a supplier who is missing KPIs, delivering late, or not meeting specifications
- User asks to "draft a letter" to a supplier about performance
- User asks how to "preserve Council's rights" under a contract
- User asks how to "trigger remedies" under a contract

 

STRUCTURE

Escalation happens in stages. Each stage must be completed (and documented) before moving to the next. Skipping stages weakens Council's position if the matter becomes a dispute.

Stage 1 Operational discussion: The contract manager raises the issue with the supplier's primary contact. Verbal in the first instance, then confirmed in writing. The purpose is to ensure the supplier is aware of the issue, understands the gap between expected and actual performance, and has an opportunity to explain and correct. Most issues resolve at this stage. Record: file note, email confirmation, minutes of meeting.

Stage 2 Written notification: If the issue persists or is serious, a formal written notification is sent to the supplier. This is a letter or formal email from the contract manager identifying the specific performance issue, the contractual clause engaged, the required remedy, and the timeframe. This is not yet a contractual notice but is an important step in the paper trail. Record: signed letter on file, supplier's response.

Stage 3 Formal notice under the contract: If the issue is not resolved, a formal notice is issued under the contract's notice provisions. This typically takes one of three forms depending on the contract and the severity: a performance improvement notice, a breach notice, or a show cause notice. Each has a specific contractual effect. The notice must comply exactly with the contract's requirements for form, content, service method, and notice period. Legal review of the notice is required before issue. Record: signed notice, proof of service, calendar for response deadline.

Stage 4 Internal escalation to senior management: In parallel with Stage 3, the contract manager briefs their manager and the relevant executive director. The briefing covers the issue, what has been done, what is being considered, the financial and reputational exposure, and the range of options open to Council. A written briefing note is prepared. Record: briefing note signed by the Executive Director.

Stage 5 Contractual remedy or dispute resolution: Based on the supplier's response (or failure to respond) to the formal notice, Council decides on the next step. Options typically include: service credits or liquidated damages, suspension of work, step in rights, termination for cause, dispute resolution under the contract (usually mediation before litigation). Each of these requires specific steps under the contract. Legal advice should be taken before any action in this stage. Record: decision brief, legal advice, executed action.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- Document as you go. Every conversation, every email, every phone call. The quality of the documentation at the start of the issue determines the strength of Council's position at the end.
- Stick to the facts. Escalation letters and notices describe what happened, not what you think of the supplier or their motives. Adjectives weaken a notice. Dates, clauses, and numbers strengthen it.
- Do not issue a formal contractual notice without legal review. A defective notice can reset the clock, waive rights, or be ignored without consequence.
- Follow the contract's notice provisions exactly. Wrong address, wrong method of service, or wrong signatory can invalidate a notice.
- Give the supplier a genuine opportunity to remedy. Not because the contract requires it (it often does) but because most performance issues are recoverable, and termination is expensive and disruptive for Council.
- Keep the commercial relationship in view. The goal is usually to get the supplier to perform, not to end the contract. Escalation should increase pressure to perform, not signal that the relationship is irrecoverable.
- Brief senior management early. Executives do not want their first knowledge of a problem to be when the supplier rings them directly.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good escalation can be traced from the first operational conversation to the final resolution, with every step documented, every contractual requirement met, and Council's position defensible at every stage.

An escalation process must avoid the following:
- Jumping straight to formal notices without first giving the supplier an operational opportunity to correct (unless the matter is so serious that it warrants immediate formal notice, in which case that reasoning should itself be documented)
- Notices issued without legal review
- Written correspondence that editorialises or attributes motives
- Internal escalation that happens only after Council is committed to a course of action
- Gaps in the documentation trail

An escalation process must include the following:
- A clear statement of the specific performance gap, with reference to the contract clause and the evidence
- A documented sequence of steps, with dates and records
- Legal review of any formal contractual notice before issue
- Early internal escalation to the relevant executive
- Consideration of the commercial relationship alongside the contractual position

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Contract escalation at Queensland local governments should apply the Queensland Government Contract Management Framework and the QGCPO Contract Management Guide.

Where the contract is for building or construction work, the Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Act 2017 may apply to payment related disputes. Payment claims and payment schedules under that Act have strict statutory time frames that sit alongside the contract.

Where the issue involves a potential breach of WHS obligations by the supplier, the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld) obligations on Council as a PCBU apply. Escalation of WHS matters should involve Council's WHS team and may require regulatory notification.

Where the issue involves a potential fraud, corruption, or misconduct matter, it must be assessed against the reporting obligations under the Crime and Corruption Act 2001 and Council's fraud and corruption control framework.

Decisions to terminate a contract, to commence legal proceedings, or to accept a material settlement typically exceed the Chief Executive Officer's delegation and require Council resolution. The briefing note to Council should include legal advice and a risk assessment of the options.

All escalation records form part of Council's contract file and are subject to the Public Records Act 2002 (Qld) and potentially to the Right to Information Act 2009 (Qld). Write every document on the assumption that it could be produced under an RTI application.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Stage 1 file note (example): On 10 April 2026 the Contract Manager met with [Supplier] Account Manager at [location] to discuss the March performance report, which showed a monthly service score of 82% against a KPI of 90%. The primary driver was the delayed response to category B work orders (average 6.2 days against a KPI of 4 days). The Account Manager attributed the delay to staff turnover in the regional team and committed to reinstating the target response time by the end of April. The Contract Manager requested a written action plan by 15 April. This file note was emailed to the Account Manager for confirmation the same day.

Stage 2 notification (example extract): "I refer to our meeting on 10 April 2026 and subsequent correspondence. [Supplier]'s performance against the service levels in Schedule 2 has been below the required standard for three consecutive months (January 84%, February 87%, March 82%, against a KPI of 90%). Under clause 8.5, service credits apply for each month below 90%. In addition, Council requires [Supplier] to provide, by 22 April 2026, a written performance improvement plan addressing the category B response time issue and specifying the remedial actions, timeframes, and accountabilities. If the January to March performance is not remedied in the April and May reporting periods, Council reserves all rights under the contract, including the issuing of a formal breach notice under clause 20."

Internal escalation extract: Performance against the contracted service levels has declined for three consecutive months. Two rounds of informal discussion and a written notification have been issued. Financial exposure to date: $42,000 in service credits. Reputational exposure: residents in the northern districts have raised complaints to Councillors regarding response times. Recommendation: that the Executive Director Infrastructure be briefed prior to the May performance meeting, and that legal advice be obtained on the form of a potential breach notice should April and May performance not return to KPI.
