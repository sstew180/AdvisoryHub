SKILL: How to Conduct a Supplier Performance Review

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Contract Management

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

A supplier performance review is the structured evaluation of a supplier's performance against the contract, typically conducted at defined intervals (quarterly, half-yearly, annually) and at key decision points (renewal, extension, termination). It differs from routine monthly performance reporting in that it takes a broader view of the relationship, considers qualitative as well as quantitative factors, and produces a documented outcome that drives action. A well-run review catches issues before they become disputes, recognises good performance that warrants continuation, and feeds the organisation's understanding of the supplier market. A poorly run review produces a compliance artefact and misses the signals that matter. This skill covers the enhanced practitioner approach.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "conduct" or "run" a supplier performance review
- User asks to "evaluate" a supplier's performance over a period
- User asks to prepare for a "contract renewal" or "extension" decision
- User asks how to "assess" a supplier before a major variation or commitment
- User asks how to structure a "vendor scorecard" or "performance dashboard"
- User asks to document a "supplier capability review" or "service review"
- User asks how to balance quantitative and qualitative performance data

 

STRUCTURE

A supplier performance review has six elements.

Element 1 Quantitative performance: The measurable outputs of the contract. KPIs and service levels defined in the contract, measured against the agreed thresholds. Typically includes: availability or uptime; response times; completion times; quality measures (defect rates, rework rates, customer satisfaction scores); financial measures (price against benchmark, value delivered against budget). Presented with trend data covering at least the current review period and the prior period.

Element 2 Qualitative performance: The aspects of the relationship that are not directly measured by KPIs. Typically includes: responsiveness to Council's needs, quality of communication, proactivity, willingness to flex, quality of people assigned, management of issues, transparency about problems, cultural fit. Qualitative assessment requires structured input, not just an impression; use a short questionnaire to the Council staff who interact with the supplier.

Element 3 Contract compliance: Whether the supplier is operating in accordance with the contract. Includes: insurance currency, security clearances, licensing, WHS compliance, reporting obligations met, invoicing compliance, modern slavery declarations if required, subcontractor management. Compliance is usually a pass or fail rather than a score; failures should be flagged even if quantitative KPIs are being met.

Element 4 Risk and issue status: Open risks and issues associated with the contract or the supplier. Includes: current risk position (from the contract risk analysis), open incidents and complaints, pending or historical disputes, supplier financial health (for larger contracts), any matters raised by the Council or the supplier that are unresolved.

Element 5 Commercial position: The commercial state of the contract. Includes: actual spend against budget and against contract value; utilisation of any minimum or maximum volumes; variations executed or pending; price review status; comparison to market pricing where relevant (benchmarking).

Element 6 Outcome and action: The conclusion of the review. A documented assessment of performance (typically on a scale such as: Exceeds Expectations, Meets Expectations, Partially Meets, Does Not Meet), a summary of issues requiring attention, agreed actions with owners and dates, and decisions (renew, extend, not extend, renegotiate, terminate) where the review informs such a decision.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- Separate measurement from meeting. The data collection and analysis should be done before the review meeting with the supplier, not during it. The meeting should be about the significance of the data, not about the data itself. Suppliers should receive the data in advance and have the opportunity to respond.
- Qualitative matters as much as quantitative. A supplier hitting every KPI but difficult to work with, slow to respond, and opaque in communication is a higher-risk supplier than the numbers suggest. A supplier missing some KPIs but consistently transparent, responsive, and collaborative may be a lower risk than the numbers suggest. Both dimensions should inform the conclusion.
- Use the review to have the hard conversations early. If a supplier's performance is slipping, the review meeting is where it is formally addressed. Avoiding the conversation here does not make the problem go away; it ensures that the first conversation happens when the situation is worse and the options are fewer.
- The review feeds two audiences: the internal organisation and the supplier. The internal report informs Council decisions about the contract. The outcome discussion with the supplier informs the supplier's behaviour. Both audiences need to hear consistent messages; inconsistency between internal assessment and supplier feedback undermines both.
- Document the review. A verbal discussion is not a review. The written record, signed off by both the contract manager and a more senior officer (typically the Manager Contract Services or the relevant Director), is what has weight in later decisions including renewal, extension, or termination.
- Link the review to the contract management plan. Actions arising from the review should be added to the CMP. The risks identified should update the risk position in the CMP. The commercial data should update the CMP's commercial section.
- Benchmark where possible. For categories with multiple comparable suppliers (facilities, ICT, professional services), peer comparison or market benchmarking gives the review credibility and identifies whether the supplier is competitive. For sole suppliers or highly customised contracts, benchmarking is harder and should be replaced by structured value analysis.
- Feed learnings back into procurement. The performance review is a rich source of information for the next procurement in the category. What specifications worked, what KPIs mattered, what was impossible to measure, what performance issues should have been anticipated. Ensure this feedback reaches the category manager or procurement team.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good supplier performance review produces a documented, evidenced, balanced assessment of performance; a set of actions that will improve outcomes; and input to commercial decisions about the contract and the supplier relationship.

A supplier performance review must avoid the following:
- Quantitative-only assessments that miss relationship, risk, or commercial dimensions
- Scores without supporting evidence or commentary
- Reviews conducted without the supplier being present or having opportunity to respond
- Conclusions that are not supported by the underlying data
- Actions without owners or dates
- Reviews that are completed but never acted on
- A pattern where every supplier rates "Meets Expectations" regardless of evidence (the common failure of rating scales without discipline)

A supplier performance review must include the following:
- Quantitative performance against contractual KPIs and service levels, with trend
- Qualitative assessment from Council staff interacting with the supplier
- Contract compliance status
- Risk and issue status
- Commercial position including spend against budget and contract value
- An overall conclusion with reasoning
- Agreed actions with owners and dates
- Sign-off by the contract manager and a senior officer

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Supplier performance reviews at Queensland local governments are part of the contract management discipline expected under the Queensland Government Contract Management Framework and the QGCPO Contract Management Guide. While neither is mandatory for local government, they are the standard reference and are frequently cited in QAO audit work.

For Gold Coast and other councils operating under strategic contracting (LGR Chapter 6, Part 2), supplier performance reviews should be consistent with the Contract Manual and the relevant Significant Contracting Plans. Performance review requirements may be specified in the Significant Contracting Plan and should reflect the category and the contract's significance to the Council.

For contracts awarded under LGR s230 and s231 (approved contractor list) or under LGA arrangements (LGR s234, Local Buy), supplier performance data should feed back to the list manager and inform decisions about continued inclusion on the list.

The QAO has identified contract management as a persistent audit focus and has specifically raised concerns about the quality of supplier performance monitoring at Queensland local governments. A robust supplier performance review process, evidenced in documentation, is a key protection against adverse audit findings.

Performance issues that indicate fraud, corruption, or misconduct must be handled consistently with the Crime and Corruption Act 2001 (section 38) notification obligations. The review process should distinguish between performance issues (a matter for the contract) and integrity issues (a matter for the CCC notification pathway).

WHS performance of contractors is subject to the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld) obligations on Council as a Person Conducting a Business or Undertaking (PCBU). The performance review should include a specific WHS compliance element for contractors undertaking work with inherent WHS risk.

Performance of suppliers handling personal information should include review of compliance with the Information Privacy Act 2009 (Qld), including any privacy incidents and management of access controls.

Performance data may be subject to the Right to Information Act 2009 (Qld). Drafts of performance reviews should be prepared on the assumption that they may be disclosed under an RTI application, and the language should reflect this.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Review summary: The supplier, [name], has been contracted for facilities maintenance services since April 2026. This is the 12-month performance review, covering the period April 2026 to March 2027. The review was prepared by the Contract Manager and endorsed by the Manager Contract Services on 8 April 2027.

Quantitative performance: Category A response time KPI achieved in 11 of 12 months (minimum 92 percent, target 90 percent). Category B response time KPI achieved in 9 of 12 months (minimum 87 percent, target 90 percent). Overall monthly service score averaged 91.2 percent (range 82 percent to 96 percent). Customer satisfaction survey results 4.1 out of 5 (benchmark 3.8, prior supplier 3.6). Completion of planned preventive maintenance at 96 percent of schedule (target 95 percent).

Qualitative performance (consolidated from Council staff feedback): Response to urgent requests is consistently fast and reliable. Communication on scheduled work is good; communication on issues or delays is less consistent. Supplier's site manager is well-regarded by Council staff. Planned maintenance reports are comprehensive. Invoicing accuracy is high (one query in 12 months resolved within a week).

Compliance: All insurance certificates current at each monthly check. WHS incident reports submitted per the contract schedule (7 incidents reported, 0 notifiable events). Modern slavery declaration submitted and current. Subcontractor list maintained and current. Two minor late submissions of monthly reports, both within 3 business days of the due date and notified in advance.

Risk and issues: The risks identified at execution remain the primary risk exposures. Supplier financial health indicators reviewed in October and March, both acceptable. One formal complaint received from a resident in November, related to a timing issue on reinstatement after a works event. Resolved through the supplier's internal complaint process within 10 days.

Commercial position: Actual spend for the period was $1.38 million against a budgeted $1.41 million, 2 percent below budget. Three variations executed totalling $42,000 (all within delegation, all arising from works requested outside the original scope). No pending variation requests. CPI adjustment of 2.9 percent applied from 1 April 2027 per clause 9.3, within the 3.5 percent cap.

Conclusion: Meets Expectations. The supplier is performing in line with the contract. Category B response times are an area for attention but have not translated into customer satisfaction impact. Overall relationship is strong.

Actions: (A1) Supplier to present a Category B response time improvement plan by 31 May 2027. Owner: Supplier Account Manager. (A2) Council to review the incident communication protocol with the supplier site manager at the next quarterly meeting. Owner: Contract Manager. (A3) The supplier's first extension option (1 x 1 year) is recommended to be exercised, subject to Council approval under the Manager Contract Services delegation. Decision required by 31 December 2028.
