SKILL: How to Manage a Contract Dispute

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Contract Management

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

A contract dispute is a disagreement between Council and a supplier that has moved beyond operational discussion and into formal contractual or legal territory. It differs from a performance issue (which is a gap in delivery managed through performance mechanisms) and from escalation (which is the process of elevating a performance issue). A dispute exists when the parties disagree about their respective rights and obligations under the contract and that disagreement requires formal resolution. This skill covers how to manage a dispute from the moment it crystallises through to resolution, in a way that preserves Council's position, keeps options open, and avoids decisions being made under pressure. It is the enhanced companion to the base-level escalation skill.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "manage" or "handle" a contract dispute
- User asks to "respond to" a formal claim or notice from a supplier
- User asks to "prepare" a claim or dispute notice on behalf of Council
- User asks to "resolve" a disagreement that has moved beyond operational discussion
- User asks about "mediation", "adjudication", "expert determination", or "arbitration" under a contract
- User asks how to brief the CEO, Executive, or Council on a dispute
- User asks to manage the "legal hold" on documents and communications during a dispute

 

STRUCTURE

Dispute management has five phases. The phases are sequential but a matter can resolve at any phase.

Phase 1 Crystallisation: The point at which the matter becomes a dispute. Typically marked by the issue of a formal notice under the contract, a formal claim, a response rejecting a claim, or a notice of dispute. At this point, Council should formally acknowledge the dispute internally, establish a dispute management file, place a legal hold on related documents and communications, and brief senior management and legal services.

Phase 2 Assessment: A structured analysis of the dispute. Covers: the nature of the dispute (what specifically is contested); the contractual basis (the provisions each party relies on); the factual basis (the events and documentation that each party points to); the legal position (based on legal advice); the commercial position (the amount or value in dispute, the cost of pursuing or resisting the claim, the relationship implications); the precedent implications (does this matter set a precedent for other contracts or claims); and the range of outcomes (best case, worst case, most likely). Assessment is documented in a brief for the CEO and, where necessary, Council.

Phase 3 Strategy: Based on the assessment, develop a strategy. Strategy includes: whether to engage in formal dispute resolution or attempt an informal resolution first; which dispute resolution pathway to use (the contract's specified mechanism, typically mediation, expert determination, adjudication, or arbitration, followed by court proceedings); the negotiation parameters (opening position, target position, walk-away position); the authority for the negotiation (which officer can settle to what value); and the communication plan.

Phase 4 Execution: Follow the strategy. This may involve: formal notices under the contract's dispute clause; "without prejudice" negotiations with the supplier; mediation or adjudication proceedings; external legal representation; expert witnesses. Throughout, maintain the documentary discipline that will serve the position if the matter escalates further.

Phase 5 Resolution and closure: Document the resolution. Whether the dispute is resolved by settlement, by determination, by the other party withdrawing, or by Council withdrawing, the outcome must be documented in a form that closes the matter. Update the contract file, the risk register, the litigation register (if one exists), and brief those who need to know. Capture lessons learned for future contracts and dispute management.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- Do not improvise. Contract disputes run on specific pathways set out in the contract. A party who ignores the pathway can prejudice their position. Read the dispute clause carefully before taking any action.
- Legal advice is not optional for contract disputes. Even a modest dispute can have significant consequences if handled badly. Legal services should be involved from the moment a dispute crystallises, and external legal advice should be sought for matters that exceed the internal team's capability or capacity.
- Preserve the documentary record. The moment a dispute is anticipated, place a legal hold on all documents and communications relating to the matter, including emails, file notes, meeting minutes, system records, and text messages. Deletion of relevant documents can be treated as spoliation and seriously damage Council's position. Brief affected staff.
- Keep operational and dispute channels separate. The contract manager continues to manage the contract operationally. The dispute is managed through a separate channel, typically involving legal services, a senior officer, and external advisors. Mixing the two is a common mistake and can compromise both.
- "Without prejudice" is not magic. Communications marked "without prejudice" are generally not admissible in court as evidence of admissions, but the protection is not absolute. Do not write in "without prejudice" correspondence anything that would be damaging if it were disclosed.
- Settlement is usually better than adjudication. Most contract disputes are resolved without formal proceedings because the cost and time of proceedings typically exceed the value in dispute. Settlement should always be on the table, and the conditions for settlement (financial, operational, and precedent) should be analysed as carefully as the conditions for proceedings.
- Authority matters. Officers with delegation to manage the contract may not have delegation to settle a dispute. Settlement typically requires authorisation at a higher level, and the authority should be established before settlement discussions, not during.
- Consider the relationship. Most contract disputes occur in ongoing relationships where the parties will need to continue working together. A dispute that destroys the relationship can cost more than it recovers. Consider what resolution allows the contract to continue effectively.
- Escalate early to senior management. Disputes should not be managed quietly at contract manager level. The CEO, the relevant Executive Director, and legal services should know of a dispute as soon as it crystallises, not after it has worsened.
- External communications are high-risk. Media enquiries, information requests, and communications from politicians or community groups during a dispute must be managed through Council's communications function and, where relevant, through legal services. Statements made publicly can become evidence.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

Good dispute management preserves Council's position, results in an outcome that is commercially sound, and avoids creating precedent or damaging the contractual relationship beyond what is required.

Dispute management must avoid the following:
- Continuing operational discussion as though the dispute did not exist, thereby waiving rights or creating inconsistent positions
- Ignoring the contract's dispute resolution pathway
- Engaging in substantive negotiation without clear authority to settle
- Allowing the matter to be managed at contract manager level without senior management oversight
- Deletion, alteration, or informal disposal of documents after the dispute has crystallised
- Letting the dispute run for months without a defined strategy or plan
- Public statements or media engagement that compromise the legal position
- Settling without understanding the precedent implications for other contracts

Dispute management must include:
- A documented crystallisation event and a designated dispute management file
- A legal hold on documents and communications
- A structured assessment of the dispute covering contractual, factual, legal, commercial, and relationship dimensions
- A documented strategy with authority for the range of outcomes
- A dispute manager designated at senior level, distinct from the contract manager
- Legal advice from Council's legal services function, and external legal advice where warranted
- Regular briefing of senior management and, where required, Council
- A resolution document and updates to the contract file and other records

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Contract disputes at Queensland local governments are managed consistently with the dispute resolution provisions in the specific contract, usually involving a hierarchy of informal discussion, formal notice, mediation, and escalation to litigation as a last resort.

For contracts involving building and construction work, the Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Act 2017 (Qld) governs payment-related disputes. The Act has specific statutory processes for payment claims, payment schedules, and adjudication that run in parallel with, and sometimes over the top of, the contract's own dispute resolution pathway. Time frames under the Act are strict and failure to respond within the specified periods can result in the other party's claimed amount becoming payable. Where a dispute arises on a construction contract, specialist advice on the Act is essential.

For contracts for goods and services not involving construction, the Queensland Civil and Administrative Tribunal may have jurisdiction for smaller matters, and the courts for larger matters. The contract itself usually specifies the jurisdiction and governing law.

Decisions to commence legal proceedings, to accept a material settlement, or to terminate a contract in the context of a dispute typically exceed the Chief Executive Officer's delegation (made under LGA s257) and require Council resolution. The briefing to Council should include legal advice, a risk assessment, the range of outcomes, and the rationale for the recommendation. Council papers relating to disputes are typically considered in closed session under the confidentiality provisions of the LGR (chapter 8 meetings) to protect Council's position.

Legal professional privilege applies to communications between Council and its lawyers for the dominant purpose of giving or receiving legal advice, and for the dominant purpose of use in actual or anticipated litigation. This privilege is an important protection during disputes. Documents and communications that might attract privilege should be identified and managed through legal services to preserve the privilege.

Documents and communications relating to a dispute form part of Council's records and are subject to the Public Records Act 2002 (Qld). They may also be subject to the Right to Information Act 2009 (Qld), subject to exemptions including for information that would be privileged, confidential, or would prejudice Council's legal position. Where documents are sought under RTI, Council's RTI decision-maker considers the exemptions on a case-by-case basis.

Disputes that indicate or touch on fraud, corruption, or misconduct engage the reporting obligations under the Crime and Corruption Act 2001 (section 38). A dispute can shift from a commercial matter to an integrity matter as facts emerge, and the dispute management process should remain alert to this possibility.

Modern slavery obligations under the Modern Slavery Act 2018 (Cth) can become relevant in disputes involving suppliers who are not meeting their declared modern slavery obligations.

The QAO may raise observations in the management letter about the handling of specific disputes, particularly where financial impact or governance issues are involved. Dispute management documentation should be prepared on the assumption that it may be reviewed by the external auditor.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Crystallisation brief (extract): On 2 April 2026, the supplier issued a formal notice under clause 20.1 of the contract claiming an entitlement to $1.8 million in additional costs, comprising $1.1 million for delay costs attributed to Council's late provision of site access and $700,000 for scope variation in relation to the southern precinct works. The notice is the first formal claim of this size under this contract. The matter is now a dispute for the purposes of the contract's dispute resolution clause (clause 20). This brief is prepared by the Contract Manager, in consultation with the Manager Contract Services and Legal Services, for the Chief Executive Officer.

Assessment summary (extract): Contractual basis: The supplier relies on clauses 12.3 (site access obligations) and 8.2 (variation mechanism). Council's preliminary position, subject to legal advice, is that the site access delay was caused by a condition requiring Aboriginal cultural heritage assessment which was known to the supplier at tender, and that the scope variation claim relates to work that is clearly within the original scope. Commercial position: The claim represents approximately 8 percent of the total contract value. Council's preliminary position is that the majority of the claim is not substantiated. Legal costs to contest the matter to adjudication are estimated at $120,000 to $200,000. Settlement in the range $0 to $400,000 is potentially available based on initial informal discussion. Precedent implications: Two other contracts in the infrastructure program have similar site access provisions and could be affected by the outcome of this dispute.

Strategy: Proceed through the contract's dispute resolution pathway. Decline the claim in full through a formal response under clause 20.2 within the 14 day response period. Seek to negotiate through the mediation pathway in clause 20.3 with a target outcome in the range $100,000 to $250,000, representing the legitimate portion of the claim only. Authority to settle up to $250,000 delegated to the Chief Executive Officer. Authority to settle above $250,000 requires Council resolution. External legal representation engaged from 3 April 2026. Mediation pathway to be initiated by 30 April 2026 if informal negotiation does not produce an acceptable resolution.

Communications: All correspondence with the supplier on the matter to be through Legal Services, not the Contract Manager. Operational performance management of the contract to continue through the Contract Manager in the ordinary course. No external or media communications on the matter without reference to Communications and Legal Services. Council to be briefed confidentially at the May 2026 Ordinary Meeting, with an update prior if the matter moves to formal mediation.

Record keeping: Legal hold placed on all documents and communications relating to the contract from 2 April 2026. Dispute management file opened and maintained separately from the operational contract file. All meetings with the supplier on the dispute to be minuted. External legal correspondence held under legal professional privilege.
