SKILL: How to Structure a Complex Services Contract

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Contract Management

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

A complex services contract is a contract for services that involves some combination of: high value, long term, multiple interdependent deliverables, shared performance obligations, pricing mechanisms beyond a fixed fee, significant risk allocation, technology or specialised intellectual property, or ongoing change during delivery. Examples at Queensland local governments include outsourced ICT services, facilities maintenance across multiple sites, waste services, leisure facility operations, major construction with an ongoing operations phase, and professional services panels with continuing engagements. Structuring a complex services contract well is one of the highest-leverage activities in procurement; a well-structured contract prevents most disputes and enables good performance, while a poorly structured contract creates friction for years. This skill covers the enhanced practitioner approach to structuring these contracts, intended for use at the pre-tender and tender-drafting stages.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

STRUCTURE

A complex services contract has ten core elements. Each element must be considered deliberately; default positions rarely produce a fit-for-purpose contract.

Element 1 Scope architecture: How the services are described and structured. Most complex contracts use a hierarchical structure: a statement of work or services schedule at the top, broken down into service categories or service lines, each with defined deliverables, service levels, and acceptance criteria. Poor scope architecture is the single most common failure; get this right and most other problems are manageable.

Element 2 Term structure and extension options: The initial term, the number and length of extension options, the mechanism for exercising extensions (automatic, by notice, conditional on performance), and the total possible term. For contracts supporting essential services, the total possible term should allow for genuine market re-testing without service disruption. For innovation-heavy services, shorter initial terms with multiple extensions may preserve flexibility.

Element 3 Performance regime: How performance is measured, reported, and managed. The regime includes: service levels or KPIs with defined measurement methods; the reporting cadence and format; service credits or liquidated damages for shortfall; performance improvement mechanisms; step-in rights; and the relationship between performance and contract term (for example, performance gates on extension options). The performance regime should incentivise the behaviours Council wants and should be measurable without excessive overhead on either party.

Element 4 Pricing architecture: How the supplier is paid. Options include fixed price, cost plus, unit rate, schedule of rates, gain-share or pain-share, and combinations. The pricing architecture should align incentives with outcomes; fixed price incentivises efficiency but can lead to corner-cutting if poorly specified; cost plus incentivises quality but can lead to escalation; gain-share aligns incentives but requires mature commercial relationships. Also includes: price review mechanisms (CPI, benchmarking, market testing), volume thresholds, and any pain-share or gain-share arrangements.

Element 5 Risk allocation: The four-clause backbone (indemnity, limitation of liability, insurance, warranty), plus specific risk allocations for known risks (site conditions, latent defects, regulatory change, force majeure, data security, intellectual property infringement). Risk should be allocated to the party best able to manage it, but a risk allocated to a party who cannot bear it is not risk transferred; it is risk disguised. Insurance requirements should reflect actual exposure; excessive insurance requirements inflate price without commensurate benefit.

Element 6 Change control: How changes to scope, service levels, or pricing are managed during the life of the contract. A good change control process specifies: who can initiate change; the form of a change request; the pricing and time impact analysis; the approval threshold; and the documentation of the executed change. For complex contracts, a formal change committee or joint governance forum is often the right vehicle for significant changes. See the related variation skill.

Element 7 Governance and relationship architecture: How the parties will work together. Governance tiers typically include an operational level (daily interactions, contract manager and supplier account manager), a management level (monthly or quarterly meetings of senior managers), and an executive level (half-yearly or annual strategic reviews involving executives from both parties). For very complex contracts, a joint steering committee with formal terms of reference is warranted.

Element 8 Transition and change of circumstances: How transition in at commencement, transition out at expiry, and change of circumstances during the term are handled. Transition in typically involves a defined transition period with specific deliverables, acceptance criteria, and payment milestones. Transition out includes data return, knowledge transfer, asset return, and obligations on the supplier to cooperate with any successor supplier. Change of circumstances (material change in volume, change of Council's structure or systems, change in regulation) triggers specified rights and obligations.

Element 9 Termination: The circumstances under which the contract can be terminated, by whom, and with what consequences. Typically includes: termination for convenience (often only available to Council, with specified notice and compensation); termination for cause (for material breach, insolvency, change of control); and automatic termination (on specified events). Consequences include: settlement of accounts, return of assets and data, cooperation with transition out, and liability for losses.

Element 10 Compliance and statutory obligations: The statutory and regulatory obligations the supplier must meet, expressed as contractual obligations. Includes: WHS, privacy, modern slavery, cultural heritage, environmental protection, competitive neutrality where relevant, and any industry-specific obligations. These obligations flow from Council's own statutory position and must be addressed contractually, not assumed.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- The contract is a decision-support instrument, not a legal artefact. The parties that matter most to the contract's success are the operational managers on both sides, not the lawyers. Write the contract so that operational people can understand and use it. Legal elegance that obscures operational clarity is a failure.
- Scope architecture first, then everything else. Most contract failures trace back to unclear scope. If Council and the supplier cannot agree what is in and what is out, no performance regime or risk allocation will save the contract. Spend the time on scope; it pays back many times over.
- Align incentives with outcomes. The contract should make the supplier want to do what Council wants done. Pricing, performance regimes, and term arrangements all shape behaviour. A supplier paid fixed price for a poorly defined scope will deliver the minimum; a supplier paid gain-share on a well-defined outcome will innovate to deliver more. Choose the arrangement that fits the situation.
- Design for the relationship you want, not the one you fear. A contract drafted purely to protect Council in the worst case will be adversarial from day one. A contract drafted assuming the relationship will always be collaborative will fail when pressure comes. The best contracts establish a collaborative framework with robust protections for when collaboration breaks down.
- Plan for change. Complex services contracts run for years. The world will change. Volumes will change. Regulation will change. Technology will change. The contract should contemplate change and provide mechanisms to manage it, not assume that day-one conditions will persist.
- Plan for exit. A contract without a workable exit is a contract in which Council is captive. Data, assets, knowledge, and dependencies accumulate over time. Exit provisions should be designed at the start, when Council has negotiating leverage, not at the end when the supplier has leverage.
- Match contract complexity to the actual transaction. A small, simple services contract does not need a 120-page document with elaborate governance architecture. A large, complex services contract cannot be managed by a 12-page document. Overbuilt contracts inflate procurement and management costs; underbuilt contracts leave gaps that create disputes.
- Legal review is essential but not sufficient. Legal services should review the contract for legal soundness. Operational, commercial, and risk review is separate and equally important. A contract that is legally bulletproof but commercially irrational or operationally impractical will fail in its actual use.
- Document the thinking. A record of why specific structural choices were made is invaluable later when the contract is being varied, disputed, or re-procured. The Significant Contracting Plan (under strategic contracting) or the equivalent procurement file should capture the reasoning.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good complex services contract aligns incentives with outcomes, enables operational management, provides robust protections for both parties, accommodates change, and establishes a workable exit. It is understood by the people who have to operate it, not only by the people who drafted it.

A complex services contract must avoid the following:
- Scope descriptions that are vague or ambiguous (the single biggest dispute driver)
- Performance regimes that measure what is easy rather than what matters
- Pricing mechanisms that misalign incentives with outcomes
- Risk allocation that places risk with a party who cannot manage or bear it
- Change control mechanisms so cumbersome that changes are made informally, outside the contract
- Exit provisions that leave Council captive to the supplier
- Compliance obligations assumed rather than written into the contract
- Template-driven drafting that ignores the specific features of the transaction
- Complexity that exceeds what is needed for the actual commercial relationship

A complex services contract must include:
- Clear scope architecture with service levels, deliverables, and acceptance criteria
- Term and extension structure suited to the service
- Performance regime with measurable KPIs and consequences for shortfall
- Pricing architecture aligned with outcomes
- Comprehensive risk allocation (indemnity, limitation of liability, insurance, warranty, plus specific risks)
- Workable change control
- Governance structure at operational, management, and executive levels
- Transition in, transition out, and change of circumstances provisions
- Termination provisions covering convenience, cause, and automatic events
- Compliance obligations with statutory and regulatory obligations flowed down
- Documentation of the structural choices made and the reasoning

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Complex services contracts at Queensland local governments must comply with the Council's procurement framework. For Gold Coast and other councils operating under strategic contracting (LGR Chapter 6, Part 2), the Contracting Plan and the Contract Manual govern the process. A complex services contract will typically be a significant contract under LGR s221 and require a Significant Contracting Plan.

The five sound contracting principles under LGA s104(3) apply to every procurement and should inform contract structure. Value for money is not lowest price; open and effective competition requires a tender process or documented equivalent; local business development obligations must be considered; environmental protection obligations should be flowed into the contract; ethical behaviour and fair dealing should be reflected in conflict management and probity arrangements.

Delegations under LGA s257 and s259 apply to execution. A complex services contract with a whole-of-term value above the CEO's delegation requires Council resolution. Where extensions would push the contract above delegation, this should be planned for at execution (Council resolution at the start) rather than deferred (requiring further resolution later).

Contracts for construction work or for services involving construction are subject to the Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Act 2017 (Qld). The Act's statutory provisions for payment claims, payment schedules, and adjudication run alongside the contract and cannot be displaced by the contract itself.

Contracts handling personal information must address the Information Privacy Act 2009 (Qld) through specific privacy provisions. Contracts involving sensitive personal information (health information, for example) may attract additional obligations.

Contracts where Council or the supplier is a reporting entity under the Modern Slavery Act 2018 (Cth) must include modern slavery provisions and supplier declarations. The reporting threshold for Councils is consolidated revenue at or above the amount specified in the Act; Gold Coast is a reporting entity.

Contracts involving work on land must address the Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2003 (Qld) duty of care, either through specific provisions in the contract or through documented Council procedures that the supplier agrees to follow.

WHS obligations under the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld) apply to Council as a Person Conducting a Business or Undertaking (PCBU) and extend to contracted work. Contracts must include WHS obligations on the supplier and monitoring rights for Council.

Contracts involving the release or withholding of information must consider the Right to Information Act 2009 (Qld). The contract itself, performance data, and variation documents may be subject to RTI applications, and the contract should not include provisions that attempt to prevent lawful disclosure.

Contracts in which Council's controlled entities are involved must address the Auditor-General Act 2009 (Qld) obligations around controlled entity reporting.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Structural summary: The facilities maintenance contract is a complex services contract covering the ongoing operation and maintenance of Council's facilities portfolio. Base term 5 years, with 2 x 2 year extension options. Total possible term 9 years. Total contracted value across base term estimated at $34 million; across full term up to $62 million.

Scope architecture: Services are structured into five categories (reactive maintenance, planned preventive maintenance, minor works, compliance inspections, project works). Each category has defined deliverables, service levels, acceptance criteria, and pricing schedules. The statement of services sits in Schedule 1; service levels in Schedule 2; pricing in Schedule 3.

Performance regime: 24 KPIs across the five categories, reported monthly. Service credits apply to shortfalls against specified KPIs. Two of the KPIs are "hurdle" KPIs: sustained shortfall against these over three consecutive months triggers a performance improvement plan; sustained shortfall over six consecutive months is a termination event. Extension options are conditional on overall performance above 85 percent across the preceding 12 months.

Pricing architecture: Fixed price for planned preventive maintenance and compliance inspections. Schedule of rates for reactive maintenance and minor works. Pain-share or gain-share on energy efficiency outcomes, capped at +/- 10 percent of the planned maintenance fee. CPI adjustment annually, capped at 3.5 percent, with the option for benchmarking at the end of each 3 year period.

Risk allocation summary: Indemnity (clause 17) covers third party loss, WHS breach, privacy breach, and intellectual property infringement. Limitation of liability (clause 18) capped at fees paid in the 12 months preceding the claim, with uncapped carve-outs for indemnified matters. Insurance requirements specified in Schedule 5. Warranty (clause 16) covers defects in work for 12 months from completion. Specific risk allocations cover site access (Council), regulatory change (shared), and force majeure (shared with specified exclusions).

Governance: Operational level (daily, Contract Manager and Account Manager). Management level (monthly performance meetings; quarterly business reviews). Executive level (half-yearly strategic reviews involving the Director Infrastructure and the supplier CEO; annual full contract review).

Exit provisions: On termination or expiry, supplier to provide 6 months of transition services, return all Council data within 30 days of expiry, cooperate with any successor supplier, and deliver a comprehensive knowledge transfer document by the date of expiry. Significant Contracting Plan documented the structural choices and is filed with the procurement record.
