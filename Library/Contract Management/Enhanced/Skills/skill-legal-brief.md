SKILL: How to Write a Contract Brief for Legal Review

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Contract Management

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

A contract brief for legal review is the structured document a contract manager, procurement officer, or project officer sends to Council's legal services (internal or external) to obtain advice on a contract or contract-related matter. The quality of the brief determines the quality, speed, and cost of the legal advice received. A well-written brief frames the question clearly, provides the relevant context and documents, identifies the specific issues, and asks for advice targeted to the decision at hand. A poorly written brief produces generic advice that does not address the actual question, often at high cost and delay, and frequently needs to be re-briefed. This skill covers how to write a brief that extracts the most value from legal review.

STRUCTURE

A contract brief for legal review has seven sections. Each section serves a specific purpose.

Section 1 Purpose of the brief: One short paragraph stating why the brief has been prepared, what decision or action it supports, and the deadline by which advice is required. Example: "This brief seeks legal advice on the proposed variation to Contract 2024 055 (Facilities Maintenance Services). Advice is required by 20 April 2026 to inform the Council decision at the 15 May Ordinary Meeting."

Section 2 Background and context: The essential facts a lawyer needs to understand the matter. Covers: the contract (parties, date, value, term); the supplier relationship (quality, performance, any history); the issue giving rise to the brief (what has happened or what is proposed); the current status (what has been done, what is pending, what commitments have been made). Short: two to four paragraphs. Refer to supporting documents rather than reproducing them.

Section 3 Specific questions for advice: The precise questions the brief is asking. This is the most important section. Questions should be specific, answerable, and targeted to the decision at hand. "What are the risks of this contract" is not a useful question. "Under clause 14 of the contract, does Council have the right to terminate if the supplier's overall monthly performance score falls below 85 percent in three of any six consecutive months, given that the contract defines monthly performance score in clause 8.2 but does not expressly address consecutive period measurement" is a useful question. List questions separately and number them.

Section 4 Legal issues identified: The legal issues the brief author has identified and any preliminary view. This is not a substitute for legal advice but it helps the lawyer by identifying the terrain. Covers: the provisions of the contract in play; the statutory and regulatory framework relevant to the matter (Queensland legislation, Commonwealth legislation, industry-specific); any precedent matters known to the author; any existing legal advice on related matters.

Section 5 Commercial and operational context: The non-legal context that may affect the advice. Covers: the commercial interest of Council (what outcome Council wants); the operational implications of each potential outcome; the relationship implications; time pressures; budget; any political or reputational factors; the range of decisions Council may realistically take. A lawyer advising without this context may give technically correct advice that is commercially or operationally unhelpful.

Section 6 Documents provided: A list of the documents attached or referenced in the brief. Typical documents: the contract and all executed variations, the relevant correspondence, the performance data, the procurement file (for pre-contract matters), any prior legal advice, any dispute notices or claims, any policies or procedures relevant to the matter.

Section 7 Instructions: The practical instructions to the lawyer. Includes: the form of advice sought (written memorandum, file note, marked-up draft document); the audience for the advice (the contract manager, the CEO, Council, the ARC); any confidentiality or privilege requirements; the budget or estimate sought for the work if external lawyers; and the person to whom the advice should be delivered.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- The brief is half the work. A good brief makes the lawyer's task quicker, more targeted, and more useful. Time invested in the brief is almost always recovered in the quality and timeliness of the advice.
- Specific questions get specific answers. Vague questions get vague answers. The discipline of framing precise questions forces the brief author to understand their own situation, which is itself valuable.
- Give the lawyer the context they need to be useful, not just the facts. Lawyers can only be as helpful as the context allows. A lawyer who understands what Council is trying to achieve commercially will give better advice than one who only knows the legal position.
- Do not ask the lawyer to do the operational work. The lawyer's role is to advise on the legal position and help Council weigh options. The decision about which course to take is Council's, not the lawyer's. A brief that asks the lawyer "what should we do" gets less useful advice than one that asks "what are the legal implications of each of the three courses we are considering".
- Be honest about what Council has done. If Council has made commitments, created expectations, or taken steps that affect its legal position, disclose them. A lawyer who advises on the basis of an incomplete picture gives advice that is not reliable.
- Respect privilege. Communications with lawyers for the dominant purpose of seeking or giving legal advice, or for the dominant purpose of use in actual or anticipated litigation, are generally privileged. The brief should be marked appropriately and circulated narrowly. Including non-essential recipients can waive privilege.
- Budget and scope the work. For external legal advice particularly, agreeing the scope, the form of advice, the time frame, and the cost at the start avoids surprises. A fixed-fee or capped-fee arrangement is often available for well-defined work.
- Provide all relevant documents. A brief that asks for advice on a variation without including the executed variations that have gone before it is incomplete. A brief that asks for advice on a dispute without the correspondence is incomplete. Err on the side of providing too much rather than too little, but list the documents so the lawyer can triage.
- Close the loop. When advice is received, act on it or document why not. File the advice with the brief and the related contract documents. The advice is itself a record that may be relevant later.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good brief enables the lawyer to produce targeted, useful, timely advice without repeated requests for information.

A brief must avoid the following:
- Vague questions that produce vague answers
- Missing documents that force the lawyer to request them
- Missing context that leads the lawyer to advise on the legal position without understanding what Council is trying to achieve
- Overstating or understating Council's commercial position
- Requests for advice on decisions the lawyer cannot make (operational choices, commercial judgements)
- Absence of a clear deadline or an unrealistic deadline
- Circulation beyond the minimum required, which may waive privilege

A brief must include:
- A clear statement of purpose and deadline
- Enough background and context to orient the lawyer
- Specific, numbered questions for advice
- Identification of the legal issues and any preliminary view
- Commercial and operational context
- A list of documents provided
- Practical instructions covering the form of advice, audience, and privilege

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Legal briefs at Queensland local governments should apply the principles above, with some specific local considerations.

Council's legal services may be in-house, outsourced, or a combination. Where external lawyers are engaged, engagement should be consistent with the Council's procurement framework. Most large councils have a panel of legal service providers established under the procurement framework (for Gold Coast, established consistent with strategic contracting under LGR Chapter 6, Part 2).

For matters that may involve litigation, legal professional privilege applies to communications for the dominant purpose of use in actual or anticipated litigation. The brief should be marked as "Privileged and confidential: prepared for the purpose of obtaining legal advice" or similar, circulated only to those who need it, and managed through Council's legal services.

For matters involving the integrity provisions of the LGA (Chapters 5A and 5B for councillors, Public Sector Ethics Act 1994 (Qld) for employees) or the Crime and Corruption Act 2001 (section 38), the brief may engage considerations that go beyond the specific contract matter. Early consultation with Council's legal services about the scope of the brief is warranted.

For matters involving the Information Privacy Act 2009 (Qld), the Right to Information Act 2009 (Qld), or the Public Records Act 2002 (Qld), specialist advice from Council's information access and privacy function may supplement the legal advice.

For matters involving construction contracts and the Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Act 2017 (Qld), specialist construction legal expertise is typically required. Where the matter involves payment claims and payment schedules, time frames are tight and the brief should acknowledge them.

For matters involving Aboriginal cultural heritage or Native Title, specialist expertise in these areas is typically required alongside general contract advice.

Advice received by Council is a record under the Public Records Act 2002 (Qld) and should be retained consistently with the applicable retention and disposal schedule. Advice may be subject to the Right to Information Act 2009 (Qld), though legal professional privilege is a ground for exemption.

For complex or high-stakes matters, a briefing meeting with the lawyer to walk through the brief and the documents is often more efficient than attempting to convey all the context in writing. The meeting should be documented in a file note that forms part of the brief record.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Purpose: This brief seeks legal advice on a supplier's formal claim for additional costs of $1.8 million under the Regional Water Capital Works Contract (CON 2024 031). The supplier issued a notice of claim on 2 April 2026 under clause 20.1 of the contract. Council's preferred approach is to decline the claim in substance and pursue resolution through the contract's mediation pathway. Advice is required by 23 April 2026 to support Council's formal response to the claim, which is due under clause 20.2 by 30 April 2026. The advice will inform a confidential briefing to Council at the May 2026 Ordinary Meeting.

Background and context: The contract was executed in July 2024 for a value of $22 million over a 24 month construction period. Performance to date has been satisfactory, with the project currently approximately 4 weeks behind the programmed milestone schedule. The supplier's claim is the first formal claim under the contract. The claim comprises $1.1 million in alleged delay costs attributed to Council's provision of site access, and $700,000 in alleged variation costs in relation to the southern precinct works. Council's preliminary view is that the site access issue arose from a cultural heritage condition that was known to the supplier at tender, and that the southern precinct work is within the original scope.

Specific questions for advice:

1. Based on the terms of the contract and the supplier's notice, does Council have a sound basis to decline the $1.1 million delay claim under clause 12.3 (site access) on the grounds that the cultural heritage condition was known to the supplier at tender and was therefore at the supplier's risk?

2. Based on the original statement of works (Schedule 1) and the subsequent amendment executed on 15 October 2024, does Council have a sound basis to decline the $700,000 variation claim on the grounds that the southern precinct work is within the original scope and was not separately costed at tender?

3. What are the procedural requirements under clause 20 for Council's response to the notice? Are there risks to Council if the response is issued on 29 April rather than 30 April 2026?

4. What is the likely range of outcomes if the matter proceeds to mediation and, if mediation fails, to adjudication or arbitration under the contract's dispute clause?

5. Are there any implications for Council's position in two other contracts (Southern Beaches Coastal Protection and Northern Region Water Augmentation) which have similar site access and cultural heritage provisions?

Legal issues identified: The contract includes comprehensive dispute resolution provisions (clause 20) with a mediation-first approach. The cultural heritage issue engages the Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2003 (Qld), with a specific provision in clause 12.5 addressing heritage obligations. The variation claim engages clause 8 and the pricing schedule in Schedule 3. No current legal advice on file relating to this contract.

Commercial and operational context: Council's preference is to resist the claim in substance but to resolve the matter without proceeding to adjudication. Estimated adjudication costs are $120,000 to $200,000. Council's authority to settle is being considered: the Chief Executive Officer's delegation covers settlement up to $250,000; amounts above require Council resolution. The supplier relationship is otherwise constructive and Council wishes to preserve the ability to work with this supplier.

Documents provided: (1) The executed contract with all schedules. (2) The executed amendment of 15 October 2024. (3) The supplier's notice of claim of 2 April 2026. (4) All correspondence between Council and the supplier on site access and cultural heritage. (5) The tender documents and the supplier's tender response, including the tender clarifications. (6) Monthly performance reports for the period July 2024 to March 2026. (7) The cultural heritage management plan adopted for the project.

Instructions: Advice in written memorandum form. Audience: the Director Infrastructure, the Chief Executive Officer, and the Audit and Risk Committee (via closed session). Marked "Privileged and confidential: prepared for the purpose of obtaining legal advice and in anticipation of dispute resolution proceedings". Estimated budget $15,000 for the advice, capped at $20,000 without further instruction. Delivery to the Manager Contract Services, copied to the Principal Legal Officer (Contracts).
