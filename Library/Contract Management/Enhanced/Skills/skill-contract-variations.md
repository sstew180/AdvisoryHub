SKILL: How to Negotiate Contract Variations

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Contract Management

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

A variation is a change to the terms of a contract after it has been executed. Variations are inevitable over the life of any but the most transactional contract, and the way they are negotiated determines whether the contract remains fit for purpose or whether value and accountability erode over time. Poor variation practice is the single largest driver of cost overrun, scope creep, and contract disputes at local governments. This skill covers the enhanced practitioner approach to negotiating variations, including how to prepare, how to conduct the negotiation, how to document the outcome, and how to preserve Council's position.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "negotiate" a contract variation

- User asks how to document a variation or "variation deed"
- User asks how to decide whether a proposed change is a variation or a new contract

 

STRUCTURE

A variation negotiation has seven stages. Skipping stages, particularly the preparation and documentation stages, is where variations go wrong.

Stage 1 Classify the proposed change: Before anything else, identify what the change actually is. The four common classifications are: (a) a genuine variation within the existing contract (a change to scope, specification, time, or cost, authorised by the variation clause); (b) a change that exceeds the scope of the contract and requires a new procurement; (c) a change that is not a variation at all but an attempt to recover for a matter already in the contract; (d) a resolution of a disputed matter that should be documented as a settlement rather than a variation.

Classifying correctly matters. Treating a new procurement as a variation circumvents the procurement framework and creates a governance failure. Treating a settlement as a variation can misrepresent the contractual position.

Stage 2 Establish the contractual basis: Identify the variation clause in the contract. Read it carefully. Most contracts specify who can initiate a variation, what form the request must take, how pricing is to be determined, how time impacts are assessed, and what approvals are required. A variation that does not comply with the contract's own variation clause may be invalid or unenforceable.

Stage 3 Prepare: Develop the Council's position before entering the negotiation. Preparation includes: the rationale for the variation (why is it needed, what outcome does it support); the financial analysis (cost, value, whole-of-life impact); the technical analysis (specification, risk, performance impact); the legal analysis (is it within the variation clause, is it within delegation, are there procurement implications); the negotiation parameters (opening position, target position, walk-away position); and the approval pathway (which delegate can authorise this).

Stage 4 Conduct the negotiation: Approach the negotiation with the contract in hand. The contract is the starting point; the variation is a change to the contract. Negotiate on the basis of the contract's provisions, not in general. Use data, benchmarks, and the contract's own mechanisms (price review, pricing schedules, rates).

Stage 5 Document the agreement: Every variation must be documented in writing, signed by parties with authority, and attached to the contract file. A handshake variation is no variation at all. The document should specify: what is changing, what is not changing, the effect on price, time, and scope, the date of effect, and any new obligations on either party.

Stage 6 Approve under delegation: Every variation requires approval by an officer with the relevant delegation. The delegation must cover the cumulative value of the contract as varied, not just the value of the variation itself. A variation that pushes the contract value above the original delegation requires a new approval at the higher delegation level.

Stage 7 Update the contract management plan and the register: After approval, update the contract management plan, the contract register, and any related records (the risk register if risk has changed, the budget system for financial impact). A variation that is approved but not reflected in downstream systems will cause problems later.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- Variation is a commercial negotiation, not a paperwork exercise. The supplier has interests; Council has interests. Negotiate on the basis of those interests, informed by the contract. An officer who treats variation as form-filling will accept supplier proposals without challenge.
- The contract is the reference document. Every claim by either party should be testable against the contract. If the supplier says "this is extra work", identify the provision that makes it extra. If Council says "this is within scope", identify the provision that includes it.
- Price is easier to negotiate than scope. If a supplier wants more money for the same work, the argument centres on the contract's pricing mechanisms, and there is usually a defensible position. If a supplier wants to change scope, the argument involves specifications, performance standards, and acceptance criteria, which are harder to negotiate on the fly.
- Watch for cumulative variation drift. A series of individually reasonable variations can, over time, transform a contract into something substantially different from what was originally tendered. Monitor cumulative variation value and scope change and intervene where the contract has drifted too far.
- Do not use variation to rescue a bad procurement. If the original contract cannot deliver the outcome, the answer is rarely to vary; it is usually to terminate and re-procure. Variations that try to turn a failing contract into a successful one usually compound the problem.
- Consider the procurement implications. Major variations can be effectively new procurements dressed up as changes to an existing contract. Under strategic contracting or default contracting, this is a compliance issue. The test is typically whether the variation is within the scope of the original procurement, not whether it is within the wording of the variation clause.
- Document disagreements as well as agreements. If the supplier claims entitlement that Council does not accept, record the disagreement. Do not leave the matter open. An unresolved disagreement becomes a dispute.
- Legal review is proportionate to size and complexity. Minor variations within delegation do not need legal review. Significant variations, variations involving IP or risk allocation, and variations that may be de facto new procurements should be reviewed by legal before signing.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good variation negotiation produces a documented change that is within the contract's variation clause, properly approved, appropriately priced, and aligned with the procurement framework.

A variation must avoid the following:
- Being undocumented, verbal, or implicit
- Being approved by an officer without delegation for the varied contract value
- Being used to change scope in ways that exceed the original procurement
- Being priced without analysis, benchmark, or reference to the contract's pricing mechanisms
- Creating obligations on Council that were not contemplated when the contract was signed, without proper consideration of those obligations
- Accumulating to a cumulative change that, if presented as a single variation, would have required higher-level approval or a new procurement

A variation must include:
- A written variation document signed by authorised officers
- A clear statement of what is changing, what is not changing, and the date of effect
- Pricing and time impact analysis
- A record of the approval under delegation
- Update to the contract file, the contract management plan, and related registers

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Contract variations at Queensland local governments are governed by the Council's procurement framework and the individual contract's variation clause. For Gold Coast and other councils operating under strategic contracting (LGR Chapter 6, Part 2), variations are governed by the Contract Manual and any specific provisions in the relevant Significant Contracting Plan.

Variations that materially change the nature or scope of the original contract may be treated, in substance, as new procurements. Where the varied contract would require a different procurement approach (for example, a contract originally awarded under an approved contractor list now requires work outside that list), the procurement framework obligations reassert themselves. The sound contracting principles under LGA s104(3) continue to apply and Council must still demonstrate that the varied contract represents value for money and complies with the principles.

Variations that push the total contract value above the Chief Executive Officer's delegation (or above any other threshold in Council's delegations register made under LGA s257) require Council resolution. This is often overlooked with cumulative variations that individually fit within delegation but collectively do not.

Variations to contracts for building and construction work are subject to the Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Act 2017 (Qld) to the extent the variation involves a payment claim. The Act's payment claim and payment schedule processes apply to varied work.

Variations that affect the publication of contract details under LGR s237 (for contracts at or above $200,000 including GST) should be reflected in updated publication where the change materially affects the published details.

Variations to contracts that handle personal information or that are subject to privacy obligations must maintain compliance with the Information Privacy Act 2009 (Qld). Variations that would reduce privacy protection warrant specific scrutiny.

Variations to contracts that touch on cultural heritage must maintain compliance with the Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2003 (Qld). Scope changes that move work onto land where the cultural heritage duty of care changes may require specific consideration.

Disputes about variations (for example, disagreement about whether a change is a variation at all, or about the price of an agreed variation) are typically handled through the contract's dispute resolution pathway, which should be followed before any litigation is commenced. See the related skill on managing a contract dispute.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Variation proposal analysis: The supplier has requested a variation to the Facilities Maintenance Services Agreement to increase the scheduled response time for Category B work orders from 4 business days to 6 business days, with a corresponding reduction in the KPI threshold from 90 percent to 85 percent. The supplier proposes no change to the contract fee. Classification: This is a genuine variation within the existing contract, as the response time and KPI threshold are specified in Schedule 2. The variation clause (clause 14) permits such changes by mutual agreement documented in a variation deed.

Preparation: Financial analysis: The reduction in service level is equivalent to approximately $180,000 of value in the 12 month period based on the original pricing assumptions in the tender. Technical analysis: A reduction in response time would affect the customer service performance reported to Council in the Infrastructure Directorate performance reports. Risk analysis: Acceptance of the proposed variation without compensating change would reduce the value of the contract to Council and may set a precedent for other categories. Negotiation parameters: Walk-away position is acceptance of the proposed variation only with a compensating price reduction of $180,000 annually or a compensating improvement in another service measure. Target position is either no variation (current terms maintained) or acceptance with a $180,000 annual fee reduction. Opening position is a request for the supplier to withdraw the variation proposal and maintain existing service levels.

Outcome: After three rounds of negotiation, agreement reached on the following variation: Category B response time to remain at 4 business days for April to September; temporary relaxation to 6 business days for the October to December storm season only; no change to KPI threshold; no change to fee; supplier to provide additional resourcing plan by 30 September each year. Documented in Variation 3 to the contract, signed 15 April 2026.

Delegation: Variation 3 has a nil financial impact and does not change the total contract value. Approval under Manager Contract Services delegation. Contract value post-variation remains $4.2 million base term plus extension options, unchanged from execution.

Contract management plan update: Seasonal response time provision added to the key obligations table. October to December storm season trigger added to the monitoring calendar. Additional resourcing plan review added to the annual cycle. Contract register updated to reflect Variation 3.
