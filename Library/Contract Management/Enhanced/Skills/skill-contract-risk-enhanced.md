SKILL: How to Identify and Manage Contract Risk

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Contract Management

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

Contract risk is the risk that a contract will not deliver the expected outcome, will expose the organisation to loss, or will create compliance, operational, or reputational exposure. It differs from enterprise risk in that it is tied to a specific contractual relationship and is managed through the tools of contract management (variations, performance management, dispute resolution, termination) rather than through general risk treatments. For an experienced contract manager or procurement practitioner, contract risk management is the daily discipline of spotting exposure early, acting before it crystallises, and keeping the risk position visible to the organisation. This skill covers the enhanced practitioner approach that goes beyond the base level contract risk review.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "identify" or "manage" contract risk across a portfolio
- User asks to establish a "contract risk register" or a "contract risk framework"
- User asks to "assess" or "rate" contract risks for a specific contract or program of contracts
- User asks how to "monitor" contract risk over the life of a contract
- User asks how contract risk fits into the enterprise risk register
- User asks how to manage the risk of a complex, strategic, or long-term contract
- User asks how to respond to changing risk during the life of a contract

 

STRUCTURE

Contract risk management operates at four levels.

Level 1 Portfolio: The organisation's total contract portfolio, categorised by value, criticality, risk profile, and supplier concentration. Portfolio-level risks include: dependency on a small number of suppliers, geographic concentration, aggregation of related contracts, contracts expiring in a short window, and contracts that are approaching the end of their extension options. Managed at Chief Procurement Officer or Chief Operating Officer level, with reporting to the ELT quarterly.

Level 2 Category: Groups of contracts supporting similar services or outcomes (facilities maintenance, ICT, professional services, construction). Category-level risks include: market concentration, pricing volatility, regulatory change, and substitutability. Managed by Category Managers or senior procurement staff.

Level 3 Contract: Individual contracts. Contract-level risks are the familiar areas covered in the base contract risk review (parties, scope, term, payment, risk allocation, performance, compliance), but at enhanced level the assessment extends across the full contract lifecycle: pre-contract (supplier failure, market failure), execution (performance shortfall, specification drift, scope creep), variation and change (cost escalation, dispute), and expiry (transition, handover, data return).

Level 4 Transaction: Specific events within a contract. Risks include: individual payment approvals, variation approvals, performance failures, incidents, and disputes. Managed at Contract Manager level within the contract management plan.

At each level, the discipline is the same: identify the risks, assess inherent and residual exposure, evaluate the controls available, plan treatments, and monitor. The controls and treatments differ by level.

Common contract-level risks and their treatments:

Supplier failure (financial distress, change of control, cessation of business): Monitor supplier financial health through periodic financial checks. Include step-in rights, assignment rights, and data return obligations in the contract. Maintain backup suppliers or arrangements for critical categories.

Performance shortfall: Define performance measures in the contract with specific KPIs and reporting cadences. Use service credits, performance improvement plans, and escalation pathways. Address in the contract management plan.

Scope creep and specification drift: Define a clear change control process in the contract. Require variations to be documented, costed, approved, and reflected in the contract. Resist informal or accumulated scope changes that erode value.

Cost escalation and price risk: Define price review mechanisms in the contract (CPI, benchmarking, open-book). Monitor category-level pricing trends. Consider hedging or fixed-price terms for volatile inputs.

Risk allocation mismatch: Review the interaction of indemnity, limitation of liability, insurance, and warranty clauses. Identify whether Council's residual exposure after all allocation provisions is acceptable. Seek specialist advice where exposure is large.

Intellectual property and data: Identify who owns what IP created under the contract, what rights to use data each party has, what happens to IP and data at termination or expiry.

Dispute and termination: Define the dispute resolution pathway in the contract. Understand what termination rights exist, what notice periods apply, and what the consequences of termination are. Maintain a realistic view of exit costs.

Compliance and regulatory change: Identify the regulatory environment for the contracted activity. Include obligations in the contract. Monitor regulatory change and assess impact.

Force majeure and business continuity: Understand what events suspend or excuse performance and for how long. Align with the Council's business continuity arrangements for essential services.

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- Contract risk is not static. The risk position at signing is not the risk position three years into the contract. Review risk at each contract milestone (commencement, mid-term, variation, renewal, expiry) and when external conditions change.
- Risk allocation in the contract is the first line of defence. The tighter the risk allocation, the lower Council's exposure. But risk allocated to a supplier who cannot perform or cannot pay is risk not actually transferred. Consider the supplier's capacity to absorb risk as part of the allocation analysis.
- Dependency is the single biggest portfolio risk. A Council that relies on one supplier for a critical service, or on a small number of suppliers for a category, carries concentration risk that is not usually visible in individual contract reviews. Portfolio-level analysis is required to see it.
- Total cost of risk is more than direct cost. Contract risks with low likelihood but high consequence (supplier failure during a critical period, major data breach, catastrophic WHS event) can dwarf the expected value of the contract itself. Assess accordingly.
- The contract manager is the first line of defence. The procurement function, legal, and risk can support, but the contract manager is the person who sees performance data, interacts with the supplier, and has the earliest warning of issues. A competent, empowered contract manager is worth more than any policy.
- Write contract risks into the contract management plan. The risks identified at pre-contract stage, through the base level contract risk review, should be lifted into the CMP with owners and treatments. Risks identified during the life of the contract should be added as they emerge. A contract without an active risk view is a contract being run on hope.
- Feed contract risks into the enterprise risk register at the appropriate threshold. Minor contract risks stay at the contract level. Significant contract risks that could affect strategic objectives should be escalated. The threshold should be defined in the risk management framework.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good contract risk management approach produces current, specific, assignable views of risk at portfolio, category, contract, and transaction levels, and results in action that reduces exposure or improves oversight.

Contract risk management must avoid the following:
- Treating contract risk as a one-off exercise at the time of signing
- Risk descriptions that are generic to the category rather than specific to the contract or relationship
- Risk ratings without reasoning or evidence
- Reliance on contractual risk allocation without considering supplier capacity to bear the allocated risk
- Portfolio-level exposures that are not visible because no one is looking at the portfolio
- Contract management plans that do not include active risk management
- Risks that are identified but never escalated, treated, or closed

Contract risk management must include the following:
- Portfolio, category, contract, and transaction-level views
- Risk descriptions that are specific (not generic) and traceable to evidence
- Inherent and residual ratings with reasoning
- Controls and treatments at the appropriate level
- Monitoring and reporting arrangements
- Integration with the contract management plan and the enterprise risk register

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Contract risk management at Queensland local governments sits within the organisation's enterprise risk management framework (see the related Risk Management Framework skill). The risk management framework should recognise contract risk as a category, with appropriate appetite, thresholds, and escalation arrangements.

For Gold Coast and other councils operating under strategic contracting (LGR Chapter 6, Part 2), contract risk management is integrated with the Contracting Plan and the Contract Manual. The Significant Contracting Plans required under LGR s221 should each include a contract-level risk analysis. The interaction between the strategic contracting arrangements and the contract risk framework is a specialised area and warrants specific consideration.

Contract risk relating to building and construction work must account for the Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Act 2017 (Qld). Payment claim and payment schedule processes under that Act create specific statutory risks that must be managed alongside the contract itself.

Contract risk relating to the handling of personal information must address obligations under the Information Privacy Act 2009 (Qld) and, for health information, the Health Act and equivalent. Contracts with suppliers handling personal information should include privacy obligations as flow-through terms, and the contract management plan should monitor compliance.

Contract risk relating to modern slavery applies where Council or its suppliers are reporting entities under the Modern Slavery Act 2018 (Cth). Councils with consolidated revenue at or above the reporting threshold are themselves reporting entities and must include modern slavery considerations in their procurement and contract management.

Contract risk relating to Aboriginal cultural heritage must address obligations under the Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2003 (Qld), particularly for contracts involving work on land.

WHS obligations under the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld) apply to Council as a Person Conducting a Business or Undertaking (PCBU) and extend to contracted work. Contract risk analysis should address WHS risks, the adequacy of WHS provisions in the contract, and monitoring arrangements.

Contract risks that could crystallise into fraud or corrupt conduct should be assessed against the fraud and corruption control framework and the reporting obligations under the Crime and Corruption Act 2001 (section 38).

The QAO has consistently identified contract management as an area of audit focus for Queensland local governments, and the external audit management letter frequently includes contract management findings. Internal contract risk management should anticipate and address these findings proactively.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Portfolio risk summary (extract): At 31 March 2026, the organisation had 312 active contracts with an aggregate contracted value of $487 million. Portfolio concentration: the top 10 suppliers account for 42 percent of contracted value. Three categories (facilities maintenance, ICT, professional services) represent 58 percent of contracted value. 12 contracts expiring in the next 6 months have a combined annual spend of $24 million. Portfolio risks requiring attention: (1) facilities maintenance supplier has a single dominant provider accounting for 64 percent of category spend; (2) ICT category has four contracts all expiring in Q1 2027, creating transition risk concentration; (3) no formal financial health monitoring is in place for strategic suppliers above $5 million annual spend.

Contract-level risk (example, facilities maintenance): Risk 7 (High): Supplier concentration and performance exposure. The sole contracted supplier provides facilities maintenance across 94 percent of Council sites. Performance has been Partially Effective against KPIs for the last three quarters. A significant performance failure or supplier distress would have no ready substitute. Inherent rating (before controls) Extreme. Existing controls: contract provides for step-in rights (clause 22); performance reporting monthly; quarterly relationship meetings with supplier executive. Control effectiveness: Partially Effective. Step-in rights untested; performance reporting occurring but not consistently reviewed at Executive level. Residual rating: High. Appetite position: Outside appetite (Moderate appetite for operational concentration). Treatment: (T1) Commission a category review for facilities maintenance with a view to splitting the category across two suppliers at next tender (2027). Owner: Chief Procurement Officer. Target: Category review by 31 December 2026. (T2) Introduce formal supplier financial health monitoring for all contracts with annual spend above $5 million, commencing July 2026. Owner: Manager Contract Services. (T3) Table performance reports quarterly at the Infrastructure Directorate senior leadership team meeting. Owner: Director Infrastructure. Target: Commencing May 2026.

Escalation: Risks 7 (supplier concentration), 11 (ICT transition concentration), and 16 (major construction dispute emerging) have been escalated to the operational risk register this quarter and are being monitored at Executive level. The supplier concentration risk is under appetite challenge and will be reported to the ARC at the May 2026 meeting.
