SKILL: How to Assess Contractor Compliance

VERSION: 1.0

DOMAIN: Contract Management

LEVEL: Enhanced

 

PURPOSE

Contractor compliance assessment is the structured evaluation of whether a contractor is meeting the contractual and statutory obligations that attach to the work it is performing. It is distinct from performance assessment (how well the work is being delivered) and from contract risk management (what could go wrong). A contractor can be performing well against KPIs while being non-compliant with insurance, WHS, privacy, or modern slavery obligations, and the organisation may not know until something goes wrong. This skill covers how to design and run a compliance assessment regime that detects and addresses compliance failures before they become problems, and how to do so without creating unmanageable overhead.

 

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL

- User asks to "assess" or "check" contractor compliance
- User asks to "design" or "set up" a compliance monitoring program for contracts
- User asks how to verify insurance, WHS, or other compliance obligations
- User asks to "audit" a contractor
- User asks how to handle a contractor compliance failure
- User asks what compliance documents to collect from contractors
- User asks how to differentiate compliance from performance

 

STRUCTURE

A contractor compliance assessment has five stages.

Stage 1 Identify the compliance obligations: For each contract or contract type, identify every compliance obligation that attaches to the work. Obligations fall into three groups. First, contractual obligations specified in the contract (insurance, security, licensing, subcontractor approval, reporting, data handling, IP obligations). Second, statutory obligations that apply to the contractor by virtue of the work (WHS, environmental protection, cultural heritage, privacy, modern slavery, anti-money laundering, immigration status of workers, tax compliance including for subcontractors). Third, policy obligations that Council has adopted (Council's code of conduct for suppliers, social procurement obligations, environmental policy, Aboriginal employment).

Document the obligations in a compliance register for the contract. The register should specify: the obligation, the source (contract clause, statute, policy), the evidence required to confirm compliance, the frequency of check (at award, annually, per event), and the owner (on both sides).

Stage 2 Design the assessment regime: Not every obligation needs the same level of assessment. Design a proportionate regime with three tiers: (a) Continuous monitoring for critical obligations, typically through system integration or automated checks (insurance expiry dates, licensing status). (b) Periodic assessment for important obligations, typically quarterly or annually (WHS audit results, privacy compliance, training records). (c) Event-based assessment for obligations that arise on specific events (modern slavery reporting, subcontractor approval, data breach notification). Match the tier to the obligation; over-monitoring insignificant obligations consumes resources; under-monitoring significant obligations creates exposure.

Stage 3 Execute the assessment: Run the regime. For each assessment, the contractor provides the evidence, Council verifies it, and the outcome is recorded. Common methods include: document collection (insurance certificates, licence copies, training records); site visits or inspections (WHS compliance, work quality); data requests (incident data, workforce data, subcontractor lists); third-party verification (independent audit, regulator confirmation); self-assessment by the contractor with evidence (structured declarations).

Stage 4 Document and act: Each assessment produces a documented outcome: compliant, compliant with conditions (where minor issues exist and are being addressed), non-compliant (where significant issues exist). Non-compliance triggers a defined response, typically: formal notification to the contractor, an action plan, a follow-up assessment, and, if not resolved, escalation through the contract's performance or dispute provisions.

Stage 5 Report and integrate: Compliance assessment results feed into contract management (updates to the contract management plan), risk management (the contract risk position may change), the supplier performance review (compliance is a distinct element of the review), the category management view (pattern failures across suppliers in a category may indicate a sector-wide issue), and the ARC (material compliance failures should be reported).

 

KEY PRINCIPLES

- Compliance is not performance. A contractor can be performing well and be non-compliant. A contractor can be compliant and be performing badly. Treat these as separate assessments, not as one bundled evaluation.
- The evidence matters more than the declaration. A contractor's statement that it holds the required insurance is not evidence of insurance. The certificate of currency, current as at the relevant date, is evidence. Design the assessment regime around evidence, not assertions.
- Insurance is the single most common compliance failure. Insurance policies expire. Certificates drift out of date. Coverage changes. An insurance-focused monitoring regime with automated expiry alerts catches the majority of compliance failures and should be foundational.
- WHS compliance is high-stakes. WHS non-compliance carries personal liability for officers under the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld), extends Council's own PCBU obligations, and can result in serious harm. WHS compliance for contractors should be assessed rigorously and pattern failures should be escalated quickly.
- Subcontractor compliance is a common gap. Contractors sometimes subcontract work without Council's knowledge or approval, and subcontractors may not meet the compliance standards applied to the prime contractor. The contract should require approval of subcontractors, and the compliance regime should extend to significant subcontractors.
- Modern slavery compliance requires attention. For contracts above relevant thresholds, modern slavery statements and supplier declarations are required. Many contractors do not have mature modern slavery systems, and a superficial declaration can mask significant risk in the supply chain.
- Privacy compliance is often superficial. Contractors handling personal information may have general privacy statements but not the specific practices needed for Council data. The compliance regime should test actual practice, not just policy.
- Compliance failures should be addressed through the contract. The contract's mechanisms for performance management, breach, and termination apply to compliance failures. The contract's dispute resolution pathway applies to contested compliance matters. Do not improvise outside the contract.
- Proportionality matters. Assessing every obligation on every contractor at maximum frequency would consume the organisation. Match the assessment intensity to the risk. For a minor professional services engagement, quarterly check of insurance and annual self-declaration may suffice. For a major construction contract, weekly WHS inspection and monthly compliance audit may be appropriate.

 

QUALITY STANDARDS

A good contractor compliance assessment regime detects compliance failures promptly, drives their resolution, and protects Council from the operational, legal, and reputational consequences of contractor non-compliance.

Compliance assessment must avoid the following:
- Treating performance and compliance as a single evaluation
- Relying on contractor declarations without evidence
- Point-in-time checks at award that are never repeated
- Over-monitoring trivial obligations while missing significant ones
- Failure to extend compliance to subcontractors
- Informal handling of compliance failures outside the contract's mechanisms
- No escalation pathway for repeated or material compliance failures
- Assessment results that are produced but never acted on or integrated with other contract management activities

Compliance assessment must include:
- A documented compliance register for each contract or contract type
- A proportionate assessment regime with tiered monitoring
- Evidence-based verification, not just declarations
- Documented outcomes with follow-up for non-compliance
- Integration with contract management, risk management, and supplier performance
- Escalation for repeated or material failures
- Reporting to appropriate governance forums

 

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT

Contractor compliance assessment at Queensland local governments must address the full range of obligations that attach to contracted work, including:

WHS obligations under the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld). Council is a PCBU in respect of contracted work. Council's due diligence obligations include ensuring that contractors are able to meet their WHS obligations. Compliance assessment for WHS should include: current insurance (workers' compensation and public liability), WHS management system evidence, incident reporting, training records, and supervision arrangements. For high-risk work, site-specific safety management plans and inspection regimes apply.

Building and construction obligations under the Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Act 2017 (Qld) (where applicable) include payment claim and payment schedule compliance. Compliance with the Act cannot be contracted out of.

Privacy obligations under the Information Privacy Act 2009 (Qld). Contractors handling personal information on behalf of Council must comply with the Information Privacy Principles. Compliance assessment should include documented privacy practices, data access controls, incident management arrangements, and evidence of staff training.

Right to Information obligations under the Right to Information Act 2009 (Qld). Contractor records that relate to Council's business may be subject to RTI applications. Contracts should include provisions requiring the contractor to preserve records and provide access when requested.

Records obligations under the Public Records Act 2002 (Qld). Records created or received by the contractor in performing the contract are typically Council records and are subject to retention and disposal obligations.

Modern slavery obligations under the Modern Slavery Act 2018 (Cth). Where Council is a reporting entity (consolidated revenue at or above the threshold), and where contractors are reporting entities or supply entities within reporting obligations, modern slavery statements and declarations are required.

Aboriginal cultural heritage obligations under the Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2003 (Qld). Contractors working on land must comply with the cultural heritage duty of care, and compliance assessment should include the cultural heritage management plan, consultation records, and incident reporting.

Environmental obligations under the Environmental Protection Act 1994 (Qld) and related legislation. For contractors undertaking activities with environmental impact (waste management, construction, land disturbance), compliance assessment should include environmental authorities, monitoring data, and incident reporting.

Tax and workforce obligations include PAYG withholding (where relevant), superannuation guarantee (in some structures), subcontractor payment obligations under applicable legislation, and workforce immigration status.

The five sound contracting principles under LGA s104(3) include ethical behaviour and fair dealing. Compliance assessment is one of the mechanisms by which Council gives effect to this principle across the contract life.

For Gold Coast and other councils operating under strategic contracting (LGR Chapter 6, Part 2), the compliance assessment regime should be consistent with the Contracting Plan and the Contract Manual. Category-specific compliance arrangements may be documented in the relevant Significant Contracting Plan.

Where compliance failures indicate fraud, corruption, or misconduct, the obligation to notify under the Crime and Corruption Act 2001 (section 38) applies. The compliance assessment process should distinguish between ordinary non-compliance (a contract matter) and potentially corrupt conduct (an integrity matter).

The Queensland Audit Office has raised contractor compliance in management letters across the local government sector. A documented, risk-based compliance assessment regime protects against audit findings and, more importantly, protects against the underlying compliance failures.

 

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE

Compliance register (extract, facilities maintenance contract):

Obligation 1: Current workers' compensation insurance. Source: Contract clause 16.1; WHS Act. Evidence: Certificate of currency. Frequency: Continuous (automated expiry alert 30 days before expiry). Owner: Contract Manager (Council); Supplier Account Manager (Contractor).

Obligation 2: Current public liability insurance, $20 million minimum. Source: Contract clause 16.2. Evidence: Certificate of currency showing coverage amount. Frequency: Continuous (automated expiry alert 30 days before expiry). Owner: as above.

Obligation 3: WHS management system compliant with AS/NZS 4801 or equivalent. Source: Contract clause 15.1. Evidence: Current certification, and annual audit report or equivalent. Frequency: Annual. Owner: Principal WHS Advisor (Council); supplier WHS Manager.

Obligation 4: Modern slavery statement. Source: Modern Slavery Act 2018 (Cth); contract clause 19.3. Evidence: Current statement lodged with the Modern Slavery Register. Frequency: Annual. Owner: Manager Contract Services (Council); Supplier General Manager.

Obligation 5: Approved subcontractor list. Source: Contract clause 11.2. Evidence: Current list with each subcontractor's insurance and licence details. Frequency: Quarterly. Owner: Contract Manager; Supplier Account Manager.

Assessment cycle (annual): Quarter 1: Insurance certificates verified and logged. Quarter 2: Annual WHS audit report reviewed; modern slavery statement reviewed. Quarter 3: Subcontractor list refreshed and reviewed; privacy compliance self-declaration reviewed. Quarter 4: Annual compliance summary prepared for inclusion in the annual supplier performance review.

Non-compliance response (example): In January 2026, a scheduled check identified that the supplier's public liability insurance certificate had expired three days earlier and had not been renewed. Council issued a formal notice under clause 16.4 requiring the certificate to be renewed and provided within 7 days. The supplier provided the renewed certificate within 48 hours. The incident is recorded in the compliance register and will be reviewed at the next supplier performance review. The supplier has agreed to a process change providing 45 days' notice to its broker, and Council has enhanced its expiry alert to 45 days from 30 days to avoid recurrence.

Escalation (example): In the same year, the supplier was identified as having engaged a subcontractor (a specialist HVAC firm) without Council's approval, contrary to clause 11.2. This is a second subcontractor approval failure in 12 months. Council has issued a formal notice requiring compliance and, consistent with the escalation skill, briefed the Director Infrastructure. The matter has been noted for consideration in the performance regime for the current period. If a third failure occurs within the 24 month period, consideration will be given to invoking clause 14 (material breach).
