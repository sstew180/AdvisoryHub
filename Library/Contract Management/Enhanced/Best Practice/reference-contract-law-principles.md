PRACTITIONER REFERENCE: Common Law Contract Principles for Queensland Local Government

JURISDICTION: Queensland / Australia

DOMAIN: Contract Management

LEVEL: Enhanced

CURRENCY: Prepared April 2026. General principles of Australian contract law are stable; legislation cited should be verified before reliance on specific provisions.

DISCLAIMER: This document is a practitioner reference, not legal advice. Specific contract matters should be referred to Council's legal services.

 

PURPOSE

Queensland local government officers work daily with contracts but most are not lawyers. The procurement framework under the Local Government Act 2009 and the Local Government Regulation 2012 tells an officer what process to follow to enter into a contract. It does not tell them how contracts actually work as legal instruments. This document bridges that gap. It is written for the contract manager, procurement officer, or project officer who needs to understand the legal character of the contracts they administer, without becoming a lawyer and without reading a textbook.

The document covers the elements of a contract, the rules about performance and breach, the ways in which contracts end, the standard risk allocation provisions and how they interact, and the statutory overlays that apply to local government contracts in Queensland.

 

1. WHAT IS A CONTRACT

A contract is a legally enforceable agreement between two or more parties. For an agreement to be enforceable as a contract, five elements must be present: an offer by one party, acceptance of that offer by the other, an intention to create legal relations, consideration flowing between the parties, and legal capacity of the parties to contract. Most Council contracts satisfy these elements through the formal tender and award process and the execution of a written contract document. The formal execution is not usually required for a contract to exist, but for Council contracts the written form is expected both by policy and for governance reasons.

The content of a contract is the terms agreed between the parties. Terms may be express (stated in the contract document or in correspondence) or implied (read in by courts because they are necessary to give the contract business efficacy, or because they are customary in that type of transaction, or because legislation implies them). The more comprehensive the written contract, the less scope there is for disputes about implied terms.

 

2. THE SIGNIFICANCE OF WRITING AND EXECUTION

For Council, the practical rule is that contracts should be in writing and signed by an officer with appropriate delegation. There are three reasons. First, the Council's own procurement framework requires written contracts above specified thresholds. Second, the Public Records Act 2002 (Qld) requires Council to maintain records of its transactions. Third, written contracts are substantially easier to enforce and defend than oral or partly-oral arrangements.

The party executing the contract on Council's behalf must have delegation under LGA s257 or s259 for the value and nature of the transaction. Execution outside delegation does not necessarily void the contract (the other party may still be entitled to rely on apparent authority) but it creates governance and accountability issues for Council. Contracts above the CEO's delegation require Council resolution.

Deeds are a specific form of written document that require more formal execution (typically signing, witnessing, and delivery) but do not require consideration to be enforceable. Many commercial arrangements are executed as deeds for specific reasons, including guarantees, indemnities, settlement agreements, and releases. The decision whether to use a deed or a contract is usually a legal one and should be taken with advice.

 

3. CONTRACT INTERPRETATION

When parties disagree about what a contract means, the dispute is resolved by the rules of contract interpretation. The primary rule is that contracts are interpreted according to the objective meaning of the words used, read in the context of the contract as a whole and of the commercial purpose of the agreement. The subjective intentions of the parties are not relevant; what matters is what a reasonable reader would understand the words to mean.

Several interpretation principles operate in practice. The contract is read as a whole; individual clauses are not read in isolation. Ambiguous terms may be interpreted against the party that drafted them (the contra proferentem rule) but this principle operates less strongly in commercial contracts between sophisticated parties. Business common sense is a guide where the literal meaning produces a commercially absurd result. Extrinsic material (pre-contract negotiations, subsequent conduct) is generally not admissible to interpret an unambiguous contract but may assist where the contract is ambiguous or where a specific exception applies.

For practitioners, the practical points are: write clearly to avoid ambiguity; avoid inconsistency between clauses; use defined terms consistently; and where specific commercial context matters, include it in the recitals or a background section.

 

4. PERFORMANCE AND BREACH

A party performs a contract by doing what the contract requires, in the manner and within the time specified. Performance that substantially complies with the contract is usually sufficient to entitle the performing party to be paid, though strict performance may be required for certain kinds of obligations (particularly time obligations where time has been made of the essence).

A breach of contract occurs when a party fails to perform an obligation it has accepted. Breaches are classified in several ways. A minor breach entitles the other party to damages for loss suffered but does not entitle that party to terminate the contract. A material breach (sometimes called a fundamental breach or a breach of a condition) entitles the other party to both damages and termination. Anticipatory breach occurs when a party repudiates the contract before performance is due; the innocent party may accept the repudiation and terminate, or affirm the contract and require performance.

Whether a breach is material or minor depends on the significance of the obligation breached and the impact of the breach. Some contracts specify which obligations are material breach events. Where the contract does not specify, the court assesses the seriousness in context.

For practitioners, the practical points are: document performance and any shortfalls contemporaneously; distinguish between minor performance issues (managed operationally) and material breaches (which affect contractual rights); and before taking action based on a breach (such as termination or withholding payment), obtain legal advice, because an incorrect characterisation can expose Council to its own claim for breach.

 

5. REMEDIES FOR BREACH

The primary remedy for breach of contract is an award of damages. Damages are intended to put the innocent party in the position it would have been in had the contract been performed. Damages must be proved; the innocent party must establish both the fact of the loss and its amount.

Damages are subject to several limitations. Remoteness: loss must be of a kind that was reasonably foreseeable at the time of contracting as a probable consequence of the breach. Mitigation: the innocent party must take reasonable steps to reduce its loss; losses that could have been avoided are not recoverable. Causation: the loss must flow from the breach; intervening causes may break the chain.

Where damages are not an adequate remedy, equitable remedies may be available. Specific performance (an order that a party perform the contract) is available for unique subject matter, most commonly land. Injunction (an order that a party stop doing something) is available to restrain breach. These remedies are discretionary and are available only where damages would not provide adequate relief.

Liquidated damages clauses (agreed pre-estimates of loss for specified breaches) are a common feature of construction and services contracts. They are enforceable as contractual terms provided they represent a genuine pre-estimate of loss and are not a penalty. The test is whether the amount is extravagant or unconscionable by reference to the greatest loss that could conceivably be proved. Liquidated damages clauses that fail the test are unenforceable.

For practitioners, the practical points are: assess Council's actual exposure before considering whether a breach is worth pursuing; maintain records of loss; and use liquidated damages where genuine pre-estimation is possible and useful.

 

6. TERMINATION

A contract ends in one of four main ways. First, by performance, where both parties have done everything the contract required. Second, by agreement, where both parties agree to end the contract (often documented in a settlement deed). Third, by operation of a contractual right to terminate, which may be for material breach, for convenience, or on specified events such as insolvency or change of control. Fourth, by operation of law, through frustration (an unforeseen event that makes performance impossible or radically different from what was contemplated) or repudiation.

Termination for breach requires that the breach be sufficiently serious to justify termination (see the discussion of material breach above) and that the terminating party comply with any contractual prerequisites, such as formal notice periods, opportunities to remedy, or consultation requirements. Termination that does not comply with the contract's requirements may itself be a breach, exposing the purported terminator to liability.

Termination for convenience is available only where the contract expressly provides for it. Where it exists, it is usually accompanied by a notice period and a requirement to pay the supplier for work done or commitments made. Termination for convenience is valuable for Council because it preserves the ability to exit contracts that are no longer needed; but it is rarely available to the supplier against Council, which reflects the asymmetric nature of these clauses.

Termination has consequences that continue after the contract ends. Some obligations (confidentiality, indemnities, warranties, intellectual property rights, dispute resolution) typically survive termination and remain enforceable. The contract should specify what survives.

For practitioners, the practical points are: read the termination clause carefully before invoking it; follow the prerequisites exactly (wrong form, wrong recipient, or insufficient notice can invalidate termination); plan for the consequences of termination (transition, data return, replacement); and obtain legal advice before any termination that is not by mutual agreement.

 

7. RISK ALLOCATION: THE FOUR-CLAUSE BACKBONE

Most commercial contracts allocate commercial risk through four interacting clauses. Understanding how these clauses work together is essential to understanding a contract's actual risk profile.

Indemnity clauses are promises by one party to compensate the other for specified losses. An indemnity has two dimensions: its scope (what losses are covered) and its carve-outs (what is excluded). A broad indemnity covering any loss caused by the supplier's acts or omissions is valuable to Council; a narrow indemnity covering only third-party property damage is much less so.

Limitation of liability clauses cap the amount one party can recover from the other for breach or other liability. Limits may be expressed as a fixed dollar amount, as a multiple of fees paid, as fees paid in a prior period (typically 12 months), or by reference to insurance coverage. Limitation clauses often have carve-outs for specified categories of liability (breach of confidentiality, breach of privacy, WHS breach, indemnified matters) which remain uncapped.

Insurance clauses require one party (usually the supplier) to maintain specified insurance coverage. The key features are the types of insurance required (public liability, professional indemnity, workers' compensation, product liability, cyber liability), the minimum amounts, and the duration (for some professional services, insurance must be maintained for a period after the contract ends to cover claims arising from earlier work).

Warranty clauses are promises about the quality or fitness of goods or services. Express warranties are those stated in the contract. Implied warranties are read in by law; for example, the Australian Consumer Law implies warranties about acceptable quality and fitness for purpose in consumer contracts, and common law implies warranties of reasonable care and skill in services contracts.

The four clauses must be read together. A broad indemnity combined with a low limitation of liability means that the indemnified matters are capped at the limitation amount (unless they are carved out). An uncapped limitation of liability combined with low insurance means that the supplier's ability to pay on claims is constrained by its own balance sheet. A strong warranty with no corresponding remedy mechanism gives Council theoretical rights that are hard to enforce.

For practitioners, the practical points are: read the four clauses together; model the worst-case exposure for Council (a major incident) against the allocation; consider the supplier's ability to bear the allocated risk (a small supplier with a theoretical $20 million liability may not actually be able to pay $20 million); and ensure the insurance requirements actually cover the risks the supplier is expected to bear.

 

8. GOOD FAITH AND REASONABLENESS

Australian contract law does not recognise a general implied duty of good faith in all contracts, but good faith obligations arise in several contexts. Some contracts include express good faith obligations, particularly in clauses requiring cooperation, consultation, or reasonable endeavours. Some case law implies a duty of good faith in certain types of commercial relationships, though the position is not settled and is generally narrower than in other jurisdictions such as the United States.

Where contracts require a party to act "reasonably" (for example, in approving variations, in giving consents, or in exercising discretion), that party cannot act arbitrarily or capriciously. The standard of reasonableness is not necessarily the decision a court would make, but the decision a reasonable person in the position of the decision-maker could make. Council's discretions should be exercised with documented reasoning, consistent with administrative law principles.

For practitioners, the practical points are: express good faith and reasonableness obligations are enforceable and should be taken seriously; exercising discretions without documented reasons is a risk; and arbitrary or capricious action by Council is more likely to generate disputes than action that is reasoned and proportionate.

 

9. STATUTORY OVERLAYS AT LOCAL GOVERNMENT

Queensland local government contracts operate under the general principles of contract law but are also subject to specific statutory overlays that modify or add to those principles.

Local Government Act 2009 (Qld) and Local Government Regulation 2012 (Qld): The procurement framework, the sound contracting principles under LGA s104(3), the delegation regime under LGA s257 to s259, and the specific requirements for internal audit and audit committees under LGR Part 11 apply to Council contracts. See the related legislation extracts.

Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Act 2017 (Qld): For construction contracts, statutory rights and obligations around payment claims, payment schedules, and adjudication apply alongside the contract's own provisions. Time frames are strict. Failure to respond to a payment claim within the statutory period can result in the claimed amount becoming payable by default.

Australian Consumer Law (Schedule 2 of the Competition and Consumer Act 2010 (Cth)): Applies to contracts involving the supply of goods or services to Council where Council is acquiring them in trade or commerce. The ACL implies certain guarantees that cannot be excluded. Unfair contract terms provisions may apply to small-value contracts.

Personal Property Securities Act 2009 (Cth): Security interests in personal property (including most assets other than land) are governed by this Act. Contracts involving leasing, consignment, retention of title, or other arrangements that may create security interests should be considered for registration on the Personal Property Securities Register.

Competition and Consumer Act 2010 (Cth): Prohibits anti-competitive conduct, including cartel behaviour, misuse of market power, and anti-competitive agreements. Council procurement should not facilitate anti-competitive behaviour by suppliers.

Corporations Act 2001 (Cth): Governs the conduct of corporate parties. Relevant for executing contracts with companies (verifying authority), for dealing with insolvent suppliers, and for understanding corporate structures.

Modern Slavery Act 2018 (Cth): Requires reporting entities to publish modern slavery statements. Councils at the reporting threshold are themselves reporting entities. Suppliers above the threshold must also report. Contracts should include modern slavery provisions.

Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld): Imposes obligations on Council as a PCBU and on officers personally. WHS obligations flow through to contracted work. Contracts should include WHS provisions and Council should actively monitor contractor WHS compliance.

Information Privacy Act 2009 (Qld) and Right to Information Act 2009 (Qld): Personal information handling and information access obligations apply to Council and, through contractual flow-down, to contractors handling Council information.

Public Records Act 2002 (Qld): Records of Council's transactions, including contract documents, are public records subject to retention and disposal schedules.

Crime and Corruption Act 2001 (Qld): Notification obligations under section 38 apply where suspected corrupt conduct is identified in contracting.

Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2003 (Qld): The duty of care for Aboriginal cultural heritage applies to any activity affecting land that may contain cultural heritage. Contracts for work on land should include provisions addressing this duty.

Environmental Protection Act 1994 (Qld): Environmental authorisations, pollution control, and related obligations apply to contracted work with environmental impact.

For practitioners, the practical points are: contract law does not stand alone; the statutory overlays can add obligations, modify or displace contractual provisions, and impose time frames that the contract cannot change. When advising on or managing Council contracts, consider both the contract itself and the statutory framework that applies to the subject matter.

 

10. HOW THIS APPLIES IN PRACTICE

The material above is a reference framework. Its purpose is to help practitioners recognise what they are looking at when they work with contracts and to know when to seek specialist advice.

A contract manager reading a contract for the first time should be able to identify: the parties and their capacity; the obligations of each party; the material breaches and the consequences of breach; the termination rights; the four-clause risk allocation; the dispute resolution pathway; and the statutory overlays that apply to the subject matter.

A contract manager encountering a problem should be able to identify: whether the issue is a performance matter (operational), a breach (contractual), or a potentially corrupt conduct matter (integrity); what the contract says about the issue; what the statutory overlay says about the issue; and whether the matter can be resolved internally or requires legal advice.

A procurement officer drafting a contract should be able to anticipate: the scope of obligations, the performance regime, the pricing architecture, the risk allocation, the change mechanisms, the exit arrangements, and the statutory overlays that must be addressed.

No practitioner should attempt to resolve a disputed contractual right, to terminate a contract for cause, to accept or settle a material claim, or to execute a contract outside their delegation without legal advice. The cost of legal advice is always less than the cost of a poorly handled contract matter.
