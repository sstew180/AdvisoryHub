SKILL: How to Write a Change Management Plan
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A change management plan describes how an organisation will manage the people side of a significant change -- a new system, a restructure, a new process, or a major policy change. It identifies who will be affected, how they will be engaged, what support they will receive, and how the change will be embedded. A change management plan without a communication plan and a training plan is incomplete. A project plan without a change management plan is a plan to deliver something that will not be used.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write a change management plan" or "plan the change"
- User is implementing a new system, process, or structure that will affect how people work
- User needs to ensure a project delivers intended benefits through effective adoption
- User is preparing for a significant organisational or cultural change
- User asks to "plan the people side" of a project or initiative

STRUCTURE
Change overview: What is changing, why, and what the expected benefits are.
Stakeholder analysis: Who is affected, how significantly, and what their likely response will be.
Change impact assessment: What will be different for each affected group -- processes, systems, roles, behaviours.
Communication plan: What messages will be sent, to whom, when, through what channels, and by whom.
Training plan: What capability is needed, who needs it, how it will be delivered, and by when.
Resistance management: Anticipated resistance, its sources, and how it will be managed.
Readiness assessment: How the organisation's readiness to change will be assessed before go-live.
Embedding: How the change will be sustained after go-live -- reinforcement, measurement, recognition.
Governance: Who owns the change management plan and who is accountable for adoption outcomes.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Change management is not communication. Sending emails is not change management. Change management is the structured process of helping people move from current state to future state. Communication is one tool, not the whole plan.
- Stakeholder analysis drives everything. Different stakeholder groups need different messages, different support, and different engagement. A one-size-fits-all approach to change rarely succeeds.
- Resistance is information, not obstruction. People who resist change usually have a reason. Understanding those reasons produces better change plans and better outcomes.
- Adoption is the measure of success, not go-live. A system that has been implemented but is not being used has not delivered the benefits that justified it.
- The sponsor is the most important change agent. A visible, committed sponsor who actively endorses the change is worth more than any communications campaign.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good change management plan is one that produces high adoption rates within 90 days of go-live. If the project delivered on time and budget but staff are still using the old system six months later, the change management plan failed. The plan should be evaluated against adoption metrics, not activity metrics -- "we held 12 training sessions" is an activity. "87% of staff have completed training and 94% are using the new system for all transactions" is an adoption outcome.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Change management plans for Queensland local government must be consistent with the council's enterprise agreement, particularly where the change affects employment conditions, role design, or work practices. Material changes to work practices require consultation with employee representatives under the enterprise agreement. Changes involving technology should consider the council's digital inclusion obligations for staff with accessibility needs. The council's learning and development team should be engaged early in the training planning process. For significant changes, the council's internal communications team should review and approve the communication plan. Changes that affect service delivery should include a community communication component consistent with LGA s4 community engagement principles.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Stakeholder group -- Finance officers (22 staff): Impact -- high. Current process of manual journal entry will be replaced by automated integration. Estimated reduction in manual transaction processing of 15 hours per week across the team. Likely response -- mixed. Some officers have expressed concern about job security. Most are positive about reducing manual work. Communication approach: Director Finance to address team in person before the system goes live. Q&A session with project team. Written FAQ distributed within 48 hours of announcement.
Resistance management: The primary anticipated resistance point is from three senior finance officers who have deep knowledge of the current system and have expressed scepticism about the accuracy of automated matching. Proposed response: invite these three officers to participate in user acceptance testing so they can validate the system's accuracy before go-live. Their involvement as internal champions will be more persuasive to peers than management advocacy.
Embedding -- 90-day review: At 90 days post go-live, the project manager will report to the steering committee on: (1) percentage of transactions processed through the new system vs manual workarounds; (2) error rate in automated matching vs baseline; (3) staff satisfaction with the new system (survey). If adoption is below 80%, the steering committee will convene to assess remediation options.
