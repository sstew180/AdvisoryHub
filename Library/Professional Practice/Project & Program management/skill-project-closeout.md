SKILL: How to Run a Project Close-Out
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
Project close-out is the formal process of completing a project, transitioning deliverables to operations, releasing resources, and documenting the project's outcomes. It is a governance requirement, not an optional administrative task. A project that is not formally closed leaves contracts open, resources tied up, and benefits unmeasured. Close-out is also the point at which lessons are captured while they are still fresh.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "close out a project" or "complete the project close-out"
- User needs to formally complete a project and transition it to business as usual
- User is preparing the documentation required to formally close a project
- User needs to release a project budget or return resources after project completion
- User asks to "wrap up the project" in a structured and documented way

STRUCTURE
Deliverable confirmation: Confirmation that all project deliverables have been completed and accepted by the relevant owners.
Contract close-out: Confirmation that all contracts have been finalised, final payments made, and any retention amounts released.
Financial close: Final budget reconciliation -- approved budget, actual spend, variance, and treatment of any remaining funds.
Resource release: Formal release of staff, contractors, and equipment back to their home teams or functions.
Document archiving: All project documents archived in the council's records management system.
Lessons learned: Completed lessons learned report (separate document) referenced here.
Post-implementation review: Schedule and terms of reference for the PIR (typically six to twelve months after go-live).
Sponsor sign-off: Formal acceptance of project completion by the project sponsor.
Steering committee close: Final steering committee meeting to formally close the project.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Close-out requires a decision. A project is not closed until the sponsor formally accepts it as complete. Informal wind-down is not close-out.
- All contracts must be finalised before close-out. An open contract after a project has been informally closed creates financial and legal exposure.
- Document archiving is a governance requirement. Project records are public records under the Public Records Act 2002. They must be archived, not deleted.
- Financial close must be confirmed by finance. The project manager cannot confirm financial close unilaterally. The finance team must confirm the final position.
- The PIR must be scheduled at close-out, not left to chance. A PIR that is not scheduled at close-out will not happen.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good project close-out leaves nothing open. Every deliverable is accepted, every contract is closed, every resource is released, and every document is archived. If a contract dispute emerges six months after close-out because a final payment was not processed, the close-out failed. The test is: if the entire project team walked out tomorrow and were replaced by people who had never heard of the project, could the new team pick up the archived records and understand completely what was done, at what cost, and with what outcome?

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Project close-out for Queensland local government capital works projects must include final asset handover to the asset owner and update of the asset register. The asset must be entered in the council's asset management system with correct attributes, condition rating, and maintenance schedule before the project is closed. For externally funded projects (grants, state or federal funding), the funding body may have specific acquittal requirements that must be completed before close-out. Retention amounts held under construction contracts must be released in accordance with the contract terms and the Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Act 2017. Project records must be retained in accordance with the council's retention and disposal schedule under the Public Records Act 2002.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Deliverable confirmation: All five project deliverables have been completed and accepted. The project sponsor (Director Infrastructure) has signed the deliverable acceptance register on 14 April 2026. The final deliverable -- system integration testing report -- was accepted on 8 April 2026 following remediation of two defects identified during testing.
Financial close: Approved budget: $4,200,000. Actual expenditure: $3,987,432. Variance: $212,568 favourable (5.1%). The Finance team has confirmed that all invoices have been processed and no outstanding purchase orders remain. The favourable variance of $212,568 will be returned to the Infrastructure Capital Works reserve in accordance with the council's budget management policy.
PIR scheduled: A post-implementation review will be conducted in October 2026, six months after go-live. The project manager will prepare terms of reference for steering committee approval by 30 June 2026. The PIR will assess whether the benefits committed in the business case have been achieved, with particular focus on the 18-hour-per-week reduction in manual processing time.
