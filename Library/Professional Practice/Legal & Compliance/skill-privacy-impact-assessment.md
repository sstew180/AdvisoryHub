SKILL: How to Prepare a Privacy Impact Assessment
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A privacy impact assessment (PIA) is a systematic evaluation of how a proposed project, system, program, or activity will affect the privacy of individuals whose personal information will be collected, used, stored, or disclosed. It identifies privacy risks and recommends measures to reduce or eliminate them. A PIA is not a compliance checkbox -- it is a design tool that helps build privacy protection into projects from the beginning rather than retrofitting it at the end.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "prepare a privacy impact assessment" or "conduct a PIA"
- User is implementing a new system, service, or program that will collect personal information
- User needs to assess the privacy implications of a proposed data sharing or data analytics initiative
- User is introducing new technology (CCTV, drones, biometrics, AI) that may affect privacy
- User asks to "check the privacy risks" of a new project or service

STRUCTURE
Project description: What is being proposed, what personal information will be involved, and why.
Information flows: What personal information will be collected, from whom, how, where it will be stored, who will have access, and when it will be destroyed.
Privacy risks: For each information flow, what are the privacy risks -- unauthorised access, data breach, function creep, discrimination, chilling effects?
Risk assessment: How likely and how serious is each risk?
Privacy controls: What measures will be implemented to reduce each risk -- encryption, access controls, retention limits, de-identification, consent processes?
Residual risk: After controls are applied, what risk remains?
Recommendation: Whether to proceed and on what conditions.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Conduct the PIA before the project is locked in, not after. A PIA that reviews a system already built can only remediate problems -- it cannot redesign them out.
- Data minimisation is the first principle. If personal information does not need to be collected, do not collect it. If it does not need to be retained, do not retain it.
- Identify function creep. Personal information collected for one purpose that could be used for another purpose is a privacy risk even if the secondary use seems harmless.
- Consider the most vulnerable. Privacy risks that are acceptable for most people may be unacceptable for domestic violence survivors, people with mental illness, or members of minority communities. Consider these groups explicitly.
- Document your decisions. The PIA is evidence that the council considered privacy before proceeding. It should be retained and updated if the project changes.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good PIA identifies all significant privacy risks, assesses them honestly, and produces specific and implementable recommendations. A PIA that finds no privacy risks in a project involving large-scale collection of sensitive personal information has not been conducted rigorously. The test is: if a data breach occurred after the project went live, would the PIA demonstrate that the council had identified and addressed the relevant risk?

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Privacy for Queensland local government is governed primarily by the Information Privacy Act 2009 (Qld). The Act establishes 11 Information Privacy Principles covering collection, storage, use, disclosure, and access. PIAs are not legally mandated in Queensland but are strongly recommended by the Office of the Information Commissioner and are required by some council policies for projects above certain thresholds. New surveillance activities (CCTV expansion, body cameras, drone programs) typically require a PIA. Data sharing arrangements with other government agencies should be assessed against both the Information Privacy Act 2009 and the Right to Information Act 2009. The council's privacy officer should be involved in any PIA for a significant project.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Information flow 3: The proposed smart parking system will collect real-time data on vehicle registration plates entering and exiting council car parks using automatic number plate recognition (ANPR) technology. This data will be matched against outstanding parking fines and transmitted to council's enforcement system. Registration plate images will be retained for 30 days.
Privacy risk: The ANPR system collects information about the movements of all vehicles, including those without outstanding fines. This creates a record of individuals' movements at specific times and locations that goes beyond what is necessary to enforce parking regulations. There is a function creep risk that this movement data could be used for purposes beyond parking enforcement (for example, traffic management analytics or law enforcement assistance) without adequate governance controls.
Privacy control: Implement a data governance policy that expressly limits use of ANPR data to parking enforcement purposes. Require approval from the privacy officer and Director before any secondary use. Reduce retention period from 30 days to 7 days for plates with no enforcement action. Conduct a quarterly audit of data access logs.
