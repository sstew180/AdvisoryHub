SKILL: How to Respond to a Right to Information Request
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A Right to Information (RTI) request is a formal application under the Right to Information Act 2009 (Qld) by which a person seeks access to documents held by the council. The council has a positive obligation to give access to documents unless a specific ground for refusal applies. This skill covers how an officer responds to an RTI request that has been referred to them for document location and review -- it does not replace the council's RTI officer, who manages the formal process.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "respond to an RTI request" or "help with a right to information application"
- User has been asked by the council's RTI officer to locate documents relevant to an RTI application
- User needs to understand their obligations when the council receives an RTI request
- User needs to identify whether documents can be released or whether grounds for refusal may apply
- User asks to "review documents for RTI" or "assess what can be released"

STRUCTURE
Understanding the request: What documents has the applicant asked for? What is the scope?
Document search: A systematic search of all locations where relevant documents may be held.
Document review: For each document found, assess whether it is relevant and whether any grounds for refusing access may apply.
Grounds for refusal assessment: Does any of the RTI Act's grounds for refusal apply? Cabinet, executive government, or legal professional privilege? Third party personal information? Commercial information?
Consultation (if required): Where third party information is involved, the RTI process may require notification to and consultation with those third parties.
Submission to RTI officer: A complete document set with assessment notes for each document, ready for the RTI officer to make the access decision.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- The obligation is to release, not withhold. The RTI Act creates a pro-disclosure framework. The default is to release documents unless a specific ground for refusal applies. Do not look for reasons to withhold.
- Search must be complete. An RTI search that misses relevant documents because the officer only checked their own files -- not shared drives, emails, or records management systems -- is a compliance failure.
- Assess each document individually. A document set cannot be withheld as a whole if only some documents contain exempt information. Each document must be assessed on its own merits.
- Do not consult with the subject of the request. If the request is about a contract, do not ask the contractor what they think about release. Third party consultation is managed by the RTI officer under the Act's formal process.
- Timelines are statutory. The RTI Act imposes strict timelines. An officer who delays their document search without a legitimate reason can cause the council to breach the Act.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good RTI document review is one that the RTI officer can rely on to make an accurate access decision. If the RTI officer receives the document set and identifies relevant documents the officer missed, or finds that the officer has incorrectly assessed grounds for refusal, the review has not met the standard. Officers should err on the side of identifying potential grounds for refusal and leaving the access decision to the RTI officer -- they should not make the access decision themselves.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
RTI in Queensland is governed by the Right to Information Act 2009 (Qld). Queensland local governments are subject to the Act and must have an Information Commissioner-approved RTI policy. The Queensland Information Commissioner provides guidance on the Act's application and hears reviews of RTI decisions. Exempt information under the Act includes Cabinet information, legal professional privilege, information that could endanger safety, confidential communications, and personal information the disclosure of which would be contrary to the Information Privacy Act 2009. The council's RTI officer is the decision-maker -- officers who receive an RTI referral are providing information to support that decision, not making it themselves. The council must acknowledge an RTI application within 10 business days and make an access decision within 25 business days (or extended period).

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Document search methodology: I searched the following locations for documents relevant to the request for "all documents relating to the council's 2024-25 procurement of cleaning services for council buildings": (1) my email -- sent and received -- for the period 1 June 2024 to 30 March 2026, using search terms "cleaning", "contract", "tender", "quotation", and the supplier names from the tender register; (2) the shared team drive under the Facilities Management procurement folder; (3) the council's contract management system (Ariba) under contract reference 2024-CF-089; (4) the council's records management system (ECM) under the cleaning services file. I found 47 documents. Document list attached.
Grounds for refusal note -- Document 12: This document is a legal advice memorandum from the council's solicitors dated 14 August 2024 regarding the enforceability of specific tender conditions. I assess this document as potentially subject to legal professional privilege. Recommend RTI officer seek legal confirmation before making access decision.
