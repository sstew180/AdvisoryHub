SKILL: How to Prepare an Organisational Change Proposal
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
An organisational change proposal is a document that makes the case for restructuring a team, division, or service delivery model. It describes the current state, the proposed change, the rationale, the impact on people and services, the implementation approach, and the expected benefits. It is both a decision document for the executive and a consultation document for affected employees. A proposal that is not honest about the people impacts will not survive the consultation process.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "prepare a restructure proposal" or "document an organisational change"
- User needs to justify a change to team structure, reporting lines, or role design
- User is preparing for a workforce consultation process
- User needs to document the rationale for reducing, expanding, or redesigning a service
- User asks to "develop the change business case" for a management restructure

STRUCTURE
Executive summary: The proposed change in one paragraph, the primary rationale, and the expected outcome.
Current state: How the current structure is organised, what it costs, and what problems it has.
Proposed change: The new structure, including reporting lines, role changes, and any new or deleted positions.
Rationale: Why this change is being proposed. Evidence of the problem the change solves.
People impact: How many roles are affected, whether any positions are proposed for deletion, and how affected employees will be managed through the process.
Service impact: How services will be affected during and after the transition.
Benefits: What the change will deliver -- efficiency, capability, service improvement.
Implementation plan: Key steps, timeline, and accountabilities.
Consultation approach: How and when affected employees and their representatives will be consulted.
Risks: Key risks to successful implementation and proposed mitigations.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Be honest about the people impact. If positions are being deleted, say so. Euphemisms like "role realignment" when positions are being made redundant damage trust and create legal risk.
- The rationale must be genuine. A restructure driven by a desire to remove a specific individual will not survive a Fair Work challenge. The rationale must be organisational, not personal.
- Consultation must be genuine. A consultation process that presents a change as already decided is not consultation. Employees must have a genuine opportunity to influence the outcome.
- Implementation must be sequenced. Announcing a change and then leaving employees uncertain for months about their future creates lasting damage to morale and retention.
- Benefits must be realistic. A proposal that promises large savings without accounting for transition costs, redundancy payments, and productivity loss during change will not deliver as promised.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good organisational change proposal is one that an affected employee can read and understand -- even if they disagree with it. The rationale should be clear, the people impact should be honestly stated, and the consultation process should be genuinely open. A proposal that has to be revised substantially during consultation because it was dishonest or incomplete has damaged trust and delayed the change.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Organisational change proposals for Queensland local government must comply with the relevant enterprise agreement, which specifies consultation obligations, redundancy entitlements, and redeployment processes. The council's HR team must be involved before any consultation begins. Where the change involves the deletion of roles, the council's redundancy policy and the Fair Work Act 2009 National Employment Standards apply. Changes to senior contract officer roles are governed by those officers' individual contracts. Proposals involving significant service changes may require community consultation under LGA s4. Changes that affect unionised roles may trigger consultation obligations under the enterprise agreement and the industrial relations framework. The Queensland Industrial Relations Commission has jurisdiction over disputes arising from workplace change in Queensland local government.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
People impact: The proposed restructure will affect 14 positions across the Corporate Governance branch. Six roles are unchanged. Five roles are proposed to be redesigned with revised accountabilities and classification levels. Three roles are proposed to be deleted as part of the consolidation of the risk and compliance functions. For the three deleted roles, the council will follow its redeployment process as set out in the enterprise agreement before any redundancy is confirmed. Affected employees and their representatives will be provided with a copy of this proposal and invited to a consultation meeting within five business days.
Rationale: The current structure separates risk management, compliance, and assurance into three teams with a combined total of 14 FTE. The separation was introduced in 2018 when the functions were significantly smaller. An internal review in March 2026 identified that 40% of work across the three teams is duplicated and that the structure creates coordination delays on matters that touch all three functions, with an average of 2.4 additional days per matter compared with the sector benchmark.
