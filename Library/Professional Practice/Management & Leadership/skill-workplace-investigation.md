SKILL: How to Document a Workplace Investigation
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A workplace investigation is a formal process for determining the facts of a complaint or alleged conduct breach. Documentation is not an optional extra -- it is the foundation on which a defensible outcome rests. Good investigation documentation records what was alleged, what evidence was gathered, what each witness said, what the subject employee said in response, and how the investigator reached their findings. Poor documentation means a well-conducted investigation cannot be defended if challenged.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "document a workplace investigation" or "write up the investigation findings"
- User has conducted or is conducting a workplace investigation and needs to record the process and findings
- User needs to prepare an investigation report for HR, the CEO, or an external body
- User is reviewing investigation documentation for completeness and defensibility
- User asks to "prepare the investigation file" for a conduct matter

STRUCTURE
Investigation terms of reference: What was the investigation asked to determine? Who authorised it? What was the scope?
Allegations: The specific allegations under investigation, stated precisely.
Evidence gathered: A complete record of all evidence -- documents reviewed, witnesses interviewed, physical evidence.
Witness statements: A summary of each witness's account, with the key points they said and any inconsistencies.
Subject employee's response: What the subject employee said when given the opportunity to respond to the allegations.
Findings of fact: What the investigator found to be true, based on the evidence. Each finding supported by the specific evidence on which it relies.
Conclusions: Whether the allegations are substantiated, not substantiated, or substantiated in part.
Recommendations: What action the investigator recommends based on the findings. The decision-maker (not the investigator) makes the final decision.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Findings must be supported by evidence. A finding that an employee acted improperly must cite the specific evidence that supports it. A finding based on impression or intuition rather than evidence will not withstand challenge.
- The subject employee must have a genuine opportunity to respond. Before findings are made, the subject employee must be told the allegations in sufficient detail to respond, given time to respond, and have their response genuinely considered. This is the procedural fairness requirement.
- Witness statements must record what was said, not the investigator's interpretation. "The witness said the respondent spoke to her aggressively" is what the witness said. "The respondent was aggressive" is the investigator's conclusion.
- Investigations must be impartial. An investigator who has a prior relationship with either party, or who has formed a view before the evidence is gathered, should not conduct the investigation.
- Confidentiality must be maintained. Investigation documents are sensitive. Access should be limited to those who need it for the purpose of the investigation.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good investigation report is one that a Fair Work Commission member, court, or CCC investigator could read and reach the same conclusion as the investigator, based solely on the documented evidence. If the findings rest on evidence that is not in the report, the report is incomplete. If the subject employee's response has not been genuinely considered and reflected in the findings, the process is not procedurally fair.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Workplace investigations in Queensland local government must comply with the council's HR policies, the relevant enterprise agreement, and the Fair Work Act 2009. Conduct matters that may constitute corrupt conduct or maladministration must be referred to the CCC or Queensland Ombudsman -- they cannot be managed through an internal investigation alone. The Public Interest Disclosure Act 2010 requires that employees who raise concerns in good faith are not subject to adverse action as a result. Investigation records are sensitive documents -- they should be stored securely and access limited to those with a legitimate need. Records must be retained in accordance with the council's retention and disposal schedule. The council's legal team should be involved in investigations involving potential criminal conduct, significant financial loss, or matters likely to result in termination of a Senior Contract Officer.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Finding of fact 3: On 14 March 2026, Officer Chen authorised payment of Invoice 2026-0847 for $23,400 to Apex Consulting Pty Ltd. At the time of authorising this payment, Officer Chen's financial delegation authorised payments up to $20,000. This finding is based on: (1) the payment authority record in Finance One showing Officer Chen's approval (document P-2026-0847-A); (2) the council's delegation register current at 14 March 2026 confirming Officer Chen's delegation limit of $20,000 (document DR-2026-03); and (3) Officer Chen's own acknowledgement during interview on 28 April 2026 that she authorised the payment and that she was aware of her delegation limit. Officer Chen stated she authorised the payment because she believed the invoice was urgent and her manager was unavailable. This does not constitute a defence to exceeding her delegation.
Subject employee's response: When presented with the above finding on 5 May 2026, Officer Chen acknowledged that she had exceeded her delegation. She stated she had acted in good faith and in the council's best interests, that she had attempted to contact her manager without success, and that she had not sought to benefit personally from the payment. She submitted a written response on 7 May 2026 (document INV-2026-04-SR) which has been considered in reaching the conclusions below.
