SKILL: How to Manage a Difficult Employee Situation
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A difficult employee situation is one where an officer's performance, conduct, attendance, or behaviour has reached a point where informal management alone is insufficient and a more structured response is required. This skill covers how to assess the situation, choose the right response pathway, and take action in a way that is fair to the employee, legally defensible, and consistent with the council's obligations. It does not replace HR advice -- it helps managers understand when and how to act before they engage HR.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User is managing a performance or conduct issue that has not responded to informal feedback
- User needs to understand the difference between a performance issue and a conduct issue
- User is preparing to place an employee on a performance improvement plan
- User needs to document a formal warning or formal counselling
- User asks "how do I manage this situation with my employee" in a structured way

STRUCTURE
Step 1 — Assess the situation: Is this a performance issue (the employee cannot do the job) or a conduct issue (the employee will not do the job, or has behaved unacceptably)? The response pathway differs.
Step 2 — Consult HR: Before taking formal action, consult the council's HR team. This is not optional for formal processes.
Step 3 — Gather evidence: Document what has happened -- dates, specifics, prior feedback given, prior expectations set. Without evidence, the process cannot be sustained.
Step 4 — Performance issue pathway: Informal feedback → documented feedback → performance improvement plan (PIP) → formal review → further action if no improvement.
Step 5 — Conduct issue pathway: Investigation (if facts are disputed) → show cause opportunity → formal outcome (warning, termination, or other action).
Step 6 — Procedural fairness: Regardless of pathway, the employee must be told what they are alleged to have done wrong, given a genuine opportunity to respond, and have their response genuinely considered before any outcome is determined.
Step 7 — Document throughout: Every conversation, every piece of feedback, every meeting. Contemporaneous records are essential.
Step 8 — Monitor and support: A performance improvement plan without regular check-ins and genuine support is a dismissal document, not a development tool.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Distinguish performance from conduct. Putting an employee on a PIP for dishonest behaviour, or investigating a poor performer for misconduct, will produce the wrong outcome and create legal risk.
- Procedural fairness is not optional. An employee who was not given a genuine opportunity to respond to allegations before an adverse decision was made has grounds for unfair dismissal regardless of whether they were guilty.
- HR must be involved in formal processes. Managers who run formal performance or conduct processes without HR involvement make procedural errors that invalidate the outcome.
- Document everything. An undocumented performance history cannot support a decision to terminate. A documented history of feedback, support, and improvement targets can.
- Treat the employee with dignity. A difficult employee situation handled fairly and respectfully -- even where it ends in termination -- is less damaging to team morale than one handled harshly.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A well-managed difficult employee situation is one where, regardless of the outcome, the employee can say they were treated fairly and told clearly what was expected. A process that results in an unfair dismissal claim upheld by the Fair Work Commission has failed on procedural fairness, evidence, or proportionality. HR should be able to review the documentation and confirm the process was followed correctly at each step.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Difficult employee situations in Queensland local government are governed by the relevant enterprise agreement, the Fair Work Act 2009, and the council's HR policies. The enterprise agreement specifies notice requirements, the right to a support person, and grievance processes. Conduct matters that may involve corrupt conduct or maladministration must be referred to the appropriate integrity body (CCC, Queensland Ombudsman) before or in parallel with internal action -- do not attempt to manage integrity matters through the normal conduct pathway. The Public Interest Disclosure Act 2010 protects employees who make disclosures about misconduct from adverse action. Any adverse action taken against a whistleblower -- even unintentionally -- creates significant legal exposure for the council.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Performance issue documentation: Following our conversation on 14 April 2026, I am writing to confirm the agreed improvement targets for the next 60 days. By 14 June 2026, you are required to: (1) complete all monthly risk reporting by the 5th of each month, with no more than one extension request per quarter; (2) provide me with a written update on all high-rated risks by the 15th of each month; (3) attend all scheduled ARC preparation meetings. I will meet with you fortnightly to discuss progress. I want to support you to succeed in this role and am available to discuss any obstacles you are experiencing. If performance does not meet these targets by 14 June, we will need to discuss further action consistent with the council's performance management policy.
