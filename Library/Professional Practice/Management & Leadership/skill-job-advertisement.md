SKILL: How to Write a Job Advertisement
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A job advertisement is a candidate-facing document that attracts the right people to apply for a position and discourages the wrong people. It is not the position description. It is a marketing document that translates the position description into language that speaks to what candidates want to know -- what the role involves, why the organisation is a good place to work, and what kind of person will succeed. A well-written advertisement reduces time-to-fill, improves application quality, and reflects positively on the council as an employer.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write a job advertisement" or "draft a job ad"
- User needs to attract candidates for a new or vacant position
- User is reviewing and improving a job advertisement that is not attracting suitable candidates
- User needs to translate a position description into an engaging candidate-facing format
- User asks to "write the job posting" for a recruitment campaign

STRUCTURE
Role title and location: Clear, searchable, and accurate.
The opportunity: Two to three sentences about what makes this role interesting and why it matters. Not a summary of the position description.
What you will do: Four to six key responsibilities in plain language. What a typical week involves. Not copied from the position description.
What you will bring: The three to four most important attributes the ideal candidate will have. Essential only.
Why join us: Two to three genuine reasons the council is a good place to work. Specific, not generic.
Details: Classification, full-time/part-time, salary range (if disclosed), contract type.
How to apply: Clear instructions. Contact for enquiries. Closing date.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Write for the candidate, not the HR team. The advertisement should answer: "What's in it for me? Why should I apply for this role rather than a similar one elsewhere?"
- Be specific about what the role actually does. "Contribute to the delivery of strategic outcomes" tells a candidate nothing. "Lead the council's enterprise risk framework review and present to the Audit and Risk Committee" is specific and informative.
- Include only essential requirements. Advertising a role with 12 essential criteria will deter qualified candidates who meet 10 of them. Identify the three to four things that are genuinely non-negotiable.
- Be honest about the challenge. A role that involves managing organisational change or building a function from scratch is attractive to the right candidate if described honestly. Sanitising the challenge produces applications from people who are not ready for it.
- Salary transparency improves application quality. Candidates who do not know the salary range may not apply if they assume it is below their expectation, or may apply and withdraw when they discover it is. Salary disclosure reduces waste on both sides.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good job advertisement produces a shortlist where the majority of applicants are genuinely competitive for the role. If the advertisement generates 200 applications and only 5 are suitable, either the role has been described inaccurately or the requirements have not been communicated clearly. The best test is to ask a member of the target candidate group to read the advertisement and say whether they would apply.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Job advertisements for Queensland local government must comply with the council's recruitment policy and the Anti-Discrimination Act 1991 (Qld). Advertisements must not contain discriminatory requirements unless an exemption applies. Positions classified as Senior Contract Officer (executive) roles must be advertised in accordance with the council's senior contract officer policy. Expressions of interest for temporary or acting arrangements may have different requirements. The council's inclusion and diversity strategy may specify requirements around advertisement language and placement channels to attract diverse candidates. Advertisements for positions with access to children must state that a Working with Children Check (Blue Card) is required.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
The opportunity: This is a rare chance to build something. The City of Gold Coast is growing at a pace that outstrips most Australian cities, and our risk management function needs to grow with it. This role will lead the council's enterprise risk program at a pivotal time -- we are mid-way through a framework review, preparing for a major infrastructure build, and navigating a regulatory environment that is more complex than it has ever been.
What you will do: Lead the annual enterprise risk assessment and produce the executive and ARC risk report. Drive the implementation of the revised risk appetite statement across 12 business units. Manage a small team of three risk officers and build their capability. Work directly with Directors and the CEO on emerging strategic risks.
What you will bring: You are a risk specialist who has operated at a senior level in a complex regulated environment. You know how to translate risk frameworks into language executives actually use. You are comfortable challenging the status quo and can hold your own in a room full of Directors. Experience in local government is valued but not essential -- what matters is that you can operate at this level.
