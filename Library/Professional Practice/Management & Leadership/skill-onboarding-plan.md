SKILL: How to Write an Onboarding Plan
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
An onboarding plan is a structured program that helps a new employee become effective in their role as quickly as possible. It covers the first 90 days of employment -- the period in which new employees form their impressions of the organisation, build their key relationships, and establish whether they made the right career decision. A good onboarding plan reduces time-to-productivity, improves retention, and signals to the new employee that the organisation values them.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write an onboarding plan" or "plan the induction" for a new employee
- User is preparing for a new team member to start and needs to structure their first 90 days
- User needs to ensure a new senior leader is set up for success in their first quarter
- User asks to "prepare the first week" or "plan the first month" for a new employee
- User needs to document the key meetings, documents, and experiences a new employee needs

STRUCTURE
Pre-start: What needs to happen before the employee's first day -- equipment, access, workspace, buddy assigned.
Day 1: Welcome, facilities orientation, team introductions, essential system access.
Week 1: Key meetings, essential reading, understanding the role and its context.
Month 1: Deeper engagement with stakeholders, understanding of key projects and challenges, initial performance conversation.
Days 30-60: Building relationships across the organisation, taking on initial responsibilities, identifying early priorities.
Days 60-90: Operating with increasing independence, contributing to team objectives, formal check-in on progress.
Success milestones: What does success look like at 30, 60, and 90 days?

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Onboarding is not orientation. Orientation covers facilities, systems, and policies -- it is typically one day. Onboarding covers the 90-day journey from newcomer to effective contributor. Both are necessary but they are different things.
- Relationships are the most important early investment. A new employee who knows the right people -- who to call, who to trust, who to avoid -- will become productive faster than one who knows all the policies but no one in the building.
- Structured check-ins are essential. A new employee who has problems in their first 90 days but no scheduled conversations with their manager will not raise those problems. Build in regular check-ins.
- Too much information too fast is counterproductive. An onboarding plan that requires the new employee to read 40 documents in week one will overwhelm rather than orient. Sequence information by when it is needed.
- Ask the new employee for feedback. An onboarding plan that is not evaluated and improved based on feedback is not a learning organisation.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good onboarding plan produces a new employee who, at 90 days, knows what their role is, has the relationships they need to do it, understands the organisation's culture and priorities, and has begun contributing meaningfully. If an employee at 90 days is still asking basic questions about organisational structure or still waiting for system access, the onboarding plan has failed.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Onboarding plans for Queensland local government should include familiarisation with the LGA 2009 and the council's governance framework, particularly for senior roles. New employees must complete the council's mandatory compliance training (code of conduct, privacy, WHS, fraud awareness) within the first 30 days. For positions involving financial management, the delegation register and financial management policy must be covered in week one. New elected officials (councillors) are subject to a separate statutory induction process. Senior Contract Officer roles have specific induction requirements under the council's executive employment framework. The council's WHS obligations require a workplace induction before the new employee begins their substantive duties.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Day 1 schedule: 8.30am -- Meet with direct manager for welcome conversation (30 minutes). 9.00am -- Facilities tour with office manager, including emergency procedures, kitchen, building access. 10.00am -- IT setup and system access (HR system, email, finance system, risk register). 11.30am -- Team morning tea -- informal introductions. 1.00pm -- Read and sign: code of conduct, privacy policy, conflict of interest policy. 2.30pm -- Meeting with HR to complete payroll paperwork and review benefits. 4.00pm -- Debrief with manager: first impressions, any immediate questions.
30-day success milestone: By day 30, the new Manager Risk and Insurance should have: met with all nine Directors individually; read the current enterprise risk management framework and the most recent ARC risk report; reviewed the outstanding actions from the most recent QAO management letter; and completed an initial assessment of the team's capability and workload for discussion with the Director at the month-one check-in.
