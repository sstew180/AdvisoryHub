SKILL: How to Write a Performance Review
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A performance review is a formal assessment of an employee's performance against agreed objectives, competencies, and behavioural expectations over a defined period. It is both a development tool and a governance document. A well-written performance review gives the employee a clear, evidence-based picture of how they have performed, what they have achieved, and where they need to develop. It also creates a documented record that supports reward decisions and, where necessary, performance management action.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write a performance review" or "prepare an annual review"
- User needs to document a performance assessment for an employee
- User is preparing for a performance review conversation and needs to structure feedback
- User needs to write performance review comments for a self-assessment or 360 process
- User asks to "give feedback" in a structured, documented format

STRUCTURE
Achievement against objectives: For each agreed objective, a specific assessment of what was achieved. Quantified where possible.
Behavioural assessment: How the employee demonstrated the required competencies and values. Based on observed behaviour, not personality.
Strengths: Two to three genuine strengths, supported by specific examples.
Development areas: Two to three specific areas for development, with examples. Not a list of everything that needs improvement.
Overall rating: The overall performance rating consistent with the council's rating scale.
Development plan: Agreed actions for the next period. Specific, measurable, and realistic.
Career discussion: Notes from any career development discussion.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Base every assessment on evidence. "Strong communicator" is an opinion. "Prepared and delivered four clear briefings to the Director in Q3, all received without amendments" is evidence.
- Avoid the halo and horn effects. Do not let one strong result inflate the overall assessment, or one failure deflate it. Assess each area on its merits.
- Development feedback must be specific and actionable. "Needs to improve stakeholder management" cannot be acted on. "Needs to brief the Director on significant issues before they escalate to the CEO -- three instances this year where the Director was caught unprepared" can be.
- The review should not contain surprises. If the employee did not know about a performance concern before the review, the manager has not done their job during the year.
- The rating must be calibrated. Managers who rate all their staff "exceeds expectations" are either managing exceptional teams or are not calibrating their ratings against organisational standards.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good performance review is one the employee can read and know specifically what they achieved, where they excelled, and what they need to work on. If the feedback is so general that the employee cannot point to specific things to do differently, the review has not fulfilled its purpose. The development plan should be genuinely developmental -- not a list of current-year job requirements repackaged as development goals.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Performance reviews for Queensland local government employees must comply with the relevant enterprise agreement, which typically specifies review frequency, rating scale, and the relationship between performance and remuneration. The council's performance management framework will specify the process and documentation requirements. Where performance is below standard, the review must be conducted in accordance with the council's performance improvement procedure and the Fair Work Act 2009 obligations around procedural fairness. Reviews for Senior Contract Officers (executive employees) are governed by their individual contracts and the council's Senior Contract Officer policy. Performance reviews that identify conduct issues (as distinct from performance issues) must be referred to HR for separate management.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Achievement against objective: Objective -- Complete the enterprise risk framework review and present to ARC by 30 September 2026. Assessment: Achieved ahead of schedule. The review was completed and presented to the ARC at the August 2026 meeting, one month ahead of the agreed date. The ARC endorsed the framework without amendment and recommended it to council, which adopted it at the September meeting. The review was commended by the ARC Chair for its clarity and practical focus.
Development area: Delegation of operational tasks. During the year, Scott maintained direct involvement in several operational tasks that would more appropriately have been delegated to team leaders. This limited the capacity of team leaders to develop their decision-making skills and created a bottleneck when Scott was on leave in March, when three decisions were delayed by more than two weeks. Development action: In the first quarter of 2027, identify three recurring decision types currently made by Scott and transfer them to team leaders with agreed decision boundaries. Review with Scott at the April check-in.
