SKILL: How to Conduct a Structured Interview
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A structured interview is a candidate assessment process in which all candidates are asked the same predetermined questions, in the same order, and scored against the same criteria. It is more predictive of job performance than unstructured interviews and more defensible if a recruitment decision is challenged. This skill covers how to design structured interview questions, conduct the interview, and document the assessment.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "prepare interview questions" or "design a structured interview"
- User needs to assess candidates consistently and fairly for a position
- User is preparing to conduct a panel interview for a council role
- User needs to document a candidate assessment for a recruitment record
- User asks to "write behavioural interview questions" for a specific role

STRUCTURE
Question design: Develop six to eight questions based on the selection criteria. Mix of behavioural (tell me about a time when...) and situational (what would you do if...) questions.
Scoring guide: For each question, define what a strong, adequate, and weak response looks like. Design this before the interviews begin.
Interview conduct: Same questions, same order, same time allocation for every candidate. Panel members score independently before discussing.
Probing: Where a candidate's response is incomplete, probe with neutral follow-up questions (what happened next? what was your specific role? what was the outcome?).
Documentation: Score each response immediately after the candidate answers, not at the end of the interview.
Post-interview: Panel members compare independent scores and discuss differences before reaching a consensus recommendation.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Behavioural questions are more predictive than hypothetical ones. "Tell me about a time when you had to manage a difficult stakeholder" provides evidence of what the candidate has actually done. "How would you manage a difficult stakeholder?" provides evidence of what they think they would do.
- Score during the interview, not after. Memory of a candidate's responses degrades rapidly. Scores written immediately after each answer are more reliable than scores written after the interview is complete.
- Probe without leading. "That is interesting -- what specifically did you do?" is a neutral probe. "So you would have escalated to the Director, wouldn't you?" is a leading question that contaminates the candidate's response.
- Panel members must score independently. If one panel member shares their assessment before others have scored, it anchors everyone else's scores.
- The same process for every candidate. Candidates who are liked by the panel can unconsciously receive more probing and more time. Strict adherence to the structure is the antidote.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good structured interview process is one that a panel member could defend at an Anti-Discrimination Commission hearing or Fair Work inquiry. Every candidate was asked the same questions, scored on the same criteria, and assessed by the same panel. Variations in the process between candidates create defensibility risk. The scoring guide should be specific enough that two different panels would score the same response the same way.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Structured interviews for Queensland local government recruitment must comply with the Anti-Discrimination Act 1991 (Qld) and the council's recruitment and selection policy. Questions must not directly or indirectly discriminate on the basis of protected attributes including race, gender, age, disability, pregnancy, or sexual orientation. The council's HR team should review interview questions before use. Interview records must be retained as part of the recruitment file in accordance with the council's records management policy. For Senior Contract Officer positions, the interview process must comply with the council's executive recruitment framework. Where a candidate requests a reasonable adjustment to the interview process (for example, additional time, an accessible venue, an interpreter), the council must accommodate the request under anti-discrimination legislation.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Behavioural question for risk management role: Tell me about a time when you identified a significant risk that others in the organisation had not recognised. What was the risk, how did you identify it, how did you communicate it to senior stakeholders, and what was the outcome?
Scoring guide for the above question: Strong response -- candidate identifies a specific risk with evidence of how it was discovered, describes a structured approach to communicating it (not just informal conversation), names the specific stakeholders engaged, and can describe a measurable outcome (risk was treated, escalated to board, included in risk register). Adequate response -- candidate describes a genuine risk identification experience but the communication approach is vague or the outcome is unclear. Weak response -- candidate describes a theoretical approach to risk identification without a specific example, or the example is a minor operational risk rather than a significant organisational one.
