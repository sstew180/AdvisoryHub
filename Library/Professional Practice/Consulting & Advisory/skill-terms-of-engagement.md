SKILL: How to Write Terms of Engagement
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
Terms of engagement define the scope, deliverables, timeline, and conditions under which a consulting, advisory, or professional services engagement will be conducted. They are the governing document for the relationship between the client and the service provider and serve as the basis for assessing whether the engagement has delivered what was promised. Terms of engagement that are too vague cannot be enforced. Terms that are too prescriptive prevent the engagement from adapting to what is found.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write terms of engagement" or "prepare an engagement brief"
- User is commissioning a consulting, advisory, or review engagement
- User needs to define the scope of an external review before going to market
- User is preparing a brief for a legal, financial, or specialist adviser
- User asks to "set out what we need from this engagement" in a formal document

STRUCTURE
Engagement overview: The purpose of the engagement in two to three sentences.
Background: The context that led to this engagement being commissioned. What the client organisation is and what the problem or opportunity is.
Scope: What is included in this engagement. Be specific. Use numbered items.
Out of scope: What is explicitly excluded. This is as important as scope.
Deliverables: What the service provider must produce. For each deliverable: description, format, and due date.
Methodology: Any requirements or preferences for how the engagement is conducted -- interviews, document review, data analysis, workshops.
Access and cooperation: What the client will provide -- access to people, documents, systems, and data.
Timeline: Key milestones and the final completion date.
Reporting: How the service provider will report progress, and to whom.
Confidentiality: How information obtained during the engagement will be handled.
Intellectual property: Who owns the outputs of the engagement.
Fees and expenses: Fee structure, payment milestones, and expense policy.
Acceptance: How deliverables will be accepted and what constitutes satisfactory completion.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Scope must be specific enough to be tested. "Review the procurement function" is not a scope. "Review all procurement transactions above $100,000 completed in the 24 months to 31 March 2026 for compliance with the council's procurement policy" is a scope.
- Out of scope items prevent scope creep. A terms of engagement document without explicit exclusions will be interpreted by the provider as permitting everything not mentioned.
- Deliverables must be described precisely. A deliverable called "final report" tells the provider almost nothing. A deliverable called "a written report of no more than 30 pages, in the format attached, addressing the questions in Appendix A, delivered in draft by 30 May and final by 14 June" is a deliverable.
- Intellectual property must be addressed. If the council wants to own the outputs, including any methodologies or templates developed for the engagement, this must be stated explicitly.
- Payment must be tied to delivery. Milestone payments tied to deliverable acceptance give the client leverage. Payment in full before the engagement is complete removes leverage.

QUALITY STANDARDS
Good terms of engagement are ones that both parties can refer to throughout the engagement to answer "what was agreed?" If a dispute arises about whether a deliverable has been completed satisfactorily, the terms of engagement should be the first document consulted. If they do not resolve the dispute, they are not specific enough.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Terms of engagement for Queensland local government consulting and advisory engagements should be consistent with the council's procurement framework. Engagements above the relevant threshold require a formal procurement process before the terms are finalised. The council's standard contract conditions should be used as the base document, with the terms of engagement as a schedule. Professional indemnity insurance requirements for the service provider must be specified and verified before engagement commencement. The Public Records Act 2002 applies to documents created during the engagement -- the terms should address document retention and return or destruction at engagement conclusion. For legal advisory engagements, specific provisions on legal professional privilege must be included.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Scope item 4: Review the council's current contract management procedures for completeness, currency, and alignment with the Local Government Regulation 2012 Chapter 6 Part 2 (Strategic Contracting) and the council's Procurement Policy 2024. The review will identify any gaps, inconsistencies, or provisions that are no longer fit for purpose.
Out of scope: This engagement does not include: (a) review of contracts awarded before 1 July 2023; (b) review of procurement transactions below $100,000; (c) any legal advice on specific contracts or disputes; (d) implementation of any recommendations arising from the review.
Deliverable 2: A draft recommendations report, in Word format, addressing each of the review questions set out in Appendix A. The report must include an executive summary of no more than two pages, findings ranked by priority (High/Medium/Low), and specific numbered recommendations with suggested owners and timeframes. Draft due: 30 May 2026. Council review period: 14 days. Final report due: 14 June 2026.
