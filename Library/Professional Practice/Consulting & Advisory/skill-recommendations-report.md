SKILL: How to Prepare a Recommendations Report
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A recommendations report is the primary output of a review, assessment, or advisory engagement. It presents findings and recommendations in a structured format for a decision-making audience. Its purpose is to give the reader a clear picture of what was found, why it matters, and what should be done about it -- in an order and format that enables confident decision-making. A recommendations report is not a research report, not a literature review, and not a project update.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write up the recommendations" from a review or assessment
- User needs to present findings and recommendations to an executive or committee
- User has completed a diagnostic, service review, or assessment and needs to structure the output
- User asks to "prepare the final report" for a review engagement
- User needs to present options and a recommended course of action to a decision-maker

STRUCTURE
Executive summary: The three to five most important findings and the primary recommendations. One page maximum. Decision-makers may read only this.
Background and scope: What was reviewed, why, by whom, and over what period.
Methodology: How the review was conducted and what sources were used. Brief.
Findings: The significant issues identified. Each finding should have: a clear statement, the evidence supporting it, and its significance or impact.
Recommendations: One or more specific actions for each significant finding. Numbered. Each recommendation should specify: what should be done, who should do it, and by when.
Priority and sequencing: Which recommendations are most urgent and which depend on others being implemented first.
Implementation considerations: Resources, dependencies, and risks relevant to implementation.
Appendices: Supporting data, interview lists, documents reviewed. Keep the main report clean.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Lead with the most important finding. Do not bury the critical issue on page 12 after extensive background.
- Findings must be supported by evidence. A finding that says "the process is ineffective" without specific evidence of how and when will be disputed.
- Recommendations must be actionable. "Improve governance" is not a recommendation. "The CEO to commission a review of the council's delegation framework by 31 October 2026, with findings to be reported to the Audit and Risk Committee at the November meeting" is a recommendation.
- Separate findings from recommendations. A report that mixes findings and recommendations in the same section is hard to act on.
- Acknowledge limitations. If the review was not able to examine certain areas, or if the evidence base has gaps, say so. This protects the report's credibility.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good recommendations report is one the decision-maker can act on without further clarification. Each recommendation must be capable of being tracked -- "who is responsible, for what, by when?" If the sponsor cannot answer those three questions for each recommendation, the report is not complete. The executive summary should be strong enough to stand alone as a briefing note.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Recommendations reports for Queensland local government should be structured consistently with the council's governance framework. Reports going to the Audit and Risk Committee or Finance Committee should follow the council's standard committee paper format with the recommendations report as an attachment. Where the report contains findings that may constitute misconduct or maladministration, the relevant integrity pathway must be followed before or alongside the report presentation. Reports involving personal information must comply with the Information Privacy Act 2009. Reports that will be provided to external parties (state government, LGAQ, auditors) must be reviewed by the relevant Director before release. Reports resulting from a QAO audit or management letter should use the QAO's standard management response format for tracking recommendation implementation.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Finding 2 (High priority): The council's contract management system does not support real-time monitoring of contract expenditure against approved budgets. As at March 2026, 23 of 84 active contracts (27%) had expenditure within 10% of the approved contract value with no alert or escalation triggered. In four cases, the contract had been exceeded without a variation being approved. Evidence: contract management system data extract (Appendix B), interviews with three contract managers (conducted 8-10 April 2026).
Recommendation 2: The Director Corporate Services to commission an upgrade to the contract management system to include automated alerts at 80% and 95% of approved contract value, with escalation to the relevant financial delegate required before further expenditure is approved. Target implementation date: 30 September 2026. Estimated cost: $45,000 (system configuration). Priority: High.
