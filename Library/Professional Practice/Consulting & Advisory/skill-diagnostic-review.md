SKILL: How to Structure a Diagnostic Review
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A diagnostic review is a structured assessment of a function, system, process, or organisation to identify what is working well, what is not, and what should change. It is used when the nature or cause of a problem is not yet fully understood and a systematic investigation is needed before recommendations can be made. A diagnostic review produces findings and recommendations. It is not an audit (which tests compliance) and not a service review (which focuses on value for money) -- though it may incorporate elements of both.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "conduct a diagnostic review" or "diagnose what is going wrong"
- User needs to understand the root causes of a persistent problem before recommending solutions
- User is preparing for or conducting an independent review of a function or process
- User asks to "assess the current state" before designing an improvement program
- User needs to structure a review that will produce credible, evidence-based findings

STRUCTURE
Review scope and objectives: What will be reviewed, what questions the review will answer, and what it will not cover.
Methodology: How the review will be conducted -- document review, interviews, data analysis, observation, benchmarking.
Current state assessment: What exists now. Factual, evidence-based, without editorial.
Gap analysis: The difference between the current state and the required or desired state.
Root cause analysis: For each significant gap, what is causing it. Use structured techniques (five whys, fishbone analysis) for complex issues.
Findings: The significant issues identified, ranked by severity or priority.
Recommendations: Specific, actionable changes to address each significant finding. Prioritised.
Quick wins: Changes that can be made immediately with low effort and high impact. Separate from the main recommendations.
Implementation considerations: What needs to be in place for the recommendations to succeed.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Diagnose before prescribing. The single most common failure in organisational reviews is recommending solutions before understanding causes. A finding that says "implement a new system" when the root cause is a capability gap wastes money.
- Evidence must drive findings. Do not let the answer to the review be determined before the evidence is collected. Confirmatory bias produces reviews that confirm what the sponsor already believed.
- Distinguish symptoms from causes. Late deliverables, staff turnover, and budget overruns are usually symptoms. The causes -- unclear accountabilities, inadequate resourcing, poor process design -- are what need to be addressed.
- Prioritise findings. Not all findings are equal. A review that presents 40 findings without prioritisation overwhelms the reader and produces no action.
- Recommendations must be realistic. A recommendation that requires resources the organisation does not have and cannot obtain will not be implemented. Acknowledge constraints in the recommendations.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good diagnostic review is one that the sponsor and those reviewed recognise as fair, evidence-based, and accurate -- even where the findings are uncomfortable. If the reviewed organisation disputes the factual basis of findings, the review has not gathered sufficient evidence. If the sponsor dismisses the recommendations as impractical, the review has not engaged sufficiently with constraints.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Diagnostic reviews in Queensland local government are often initiated by internal audit, an incoming director, or a CEO responding to governance concerns. Reviews involving potential misconduct or integrity issues must be referred to the appropriate body (CCC, Queensland Ombudsman, or the council's integrity officer) rather than being managed as a diagnostic process. Reviews of procurement, financial management, or contract management functions should reference QAO better practice standards. The council's information management obligations under the Public Records Act 2002 and Right to Information Act 2009 apply to review documentation.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Root cause analysis (excerpt): Finding -- contract variations are not being assessed for compliance with the significant contracting threshold before approval. Five whys: (1) Why are variations not assessed? Officers are not applying the threshold check. (2) Why not? They do not know the threshold applies to cumulative contract value. (3) Why not? The contract management procedure does not address cumulative value. (4) Why not? The procedure was written before the LGR s221 significant contracting plan requirement came into force. (5) Why has it not been updated? The procedure owner role has been vacant for 14 months. Root cause: The absence of a procedure owner has allowed the contract management procedure to become non-compliant with current legislation without detection.
