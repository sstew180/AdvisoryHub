SKILL: How to Facilitate a Workshop
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A workshop is a structured group session designed to achieve a specific output -- identifying risks, generating options, making decisions, building plans, or resolving issues. Facilitation is the practice of guiding a group through a process to achieve that output. A well-facilitated workshop produces a clear output, shared understanding, and commitment to action. A poorly facilitated workshop produces circular discussion, unresolved conflict, and no decision.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "facilitate a workshop" or "plan a workshop"
- User needs to design a session to bring a group to a decision or shared understanding
- User is preparing to run a risk workshop, strategy session, or planning day
- User needs to structure a complex group discussion that could easily lose direction
- User asks to "help me plan the agenda for this session"

STRUCTURE
Pre-workshop preparation:
  - Define the output: What must exist at the end of the workshop that does not exist now?
  - Design the agenda: Work backwards from the output. What decisions or discussions must happen to reach it?
  - Prepare materials: Pre-read, working documents, templates, stimulus materials.
  - Set participant expectations: What are participants being asked to contribute?

Workshop opening:
  - Welcome and purpose: Why are we here and what will we produce?
  - Ground rules: How will we work together? (One voice at a time, challenge ideas not people, parking lot for off-topic issues.)
  - Agenda and timing: What will we cover and when?

Workshop body:
  - Diverge before converging: Generate options and ideas before evaluating and deciding.
  - Use structured techniques for complex discussions: Silent brainstorm, dot voting, affinity mapping, force field analysis.
  - Manage time actively: The facilitator owns the time.
  - Record visibly: Key points captured on a whiteboard or shared document in real time.

Workshop close:
  - Summarise outputs: What did we produce?
  - Confirm actions: Who will do what by when?
  - Next steps: What happens after this workshop?

Post-workshop:
  - Document outputs within 24 hours while memory is fresh.
  - Distribute action list to all participants.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- The facilitator does not contribute content. The facilitator manages the process. If you have a view on the content, declare it and step out of the facilitator role temporarily.
- Diverge before you converge. Evaluating and deciding before all options have been generated produces poor decisions. Create space to generate ideas before moving to evaluation.
- Manage dominant voices. A workshop where two people talk for 80% of the time has not accessed the full group's knowledge. Use techniques (small group discussion, written input, structured rounds) to draw out quieter participants.
- Parking lot unproductive threads. Off-topic discussions should be acknowledged and parked, not suppressed or allowed to consume time.
- Document as you go. A workshop output that exists only in participants' memories will not produce action.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A well-facilitated workshop ends with: a documented output that matches what was promised, a clear action list with owners and dates, and participants who feel their contribution was heard and valued. If participants leave without knowing what was decided or what happens next, the facilitation has failed.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Workshops in Queensland local government often involve a mix of elected officials and officers. The facilitator must be clear about decision-making authority -- a workshop cannot make a decision that requires a council resolution. Risk workshops should follow the council's risk assessment methodology and produce outputs consistent with the enterprise risk framework. Workshops involving external stakeholders (community members, contractors, government representatives) must comply with the council's community engagement and conflict of interest policies. Notes from workshops may be subject to Right to Information requests and should be prepared accordingly.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Workshop output definition: By the end of this three-hour session, the Corporate Governance leadership team will have: identified the top five risks to achieving the 2026-27 operational plan; agreed a risk rating and owner for each risk; identified at least one treatment action per high or extreme risk; and produced a draft risk register extract ready for ARC presentation.
Ground rules: We will use the council's 5x5 risk matrix throughout. Ratings will be made by consensus -- if we cannot reach consensus, we will vote. We have two hours for identification and rating and 45 minutes for treatment actions. The parking lot is on the left whiteboard -- anything off-topic goes there and we will address it in the last 15 minutes if time allows.
