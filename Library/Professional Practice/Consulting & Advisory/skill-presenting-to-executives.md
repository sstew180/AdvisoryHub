SKILL: How to Present Findings to an Executive
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
Presenting findings to an executive is a high-stakes communication task. The executive has limited time, is managing multiple priorities, and needs to make decisions. An effective findings presentation gives the executive the information they need to decide or act, in the right order, at the right level of detail. A presentation that buries the finding in background, hedges its conclusions, or fails to make a clear recommendation wastes the executive's time and damages the presenter's credibility.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "present findings" or "brief the executive" on the results of a review or assessment
- User needs to structure a verbal or written presentation of complex findings for a senior audience
- User is preparing to present to a CEO, Director, or committee
- User needs to present audit results, review findings, or risk assessment outcomes
- User asks to "help me structure my presentation" for an executive audience

STRUCTURE
Opening -- the headline finding: State the most important finding in the first 60 seconds. Do not build to it. The executive should know what you found before you explain how you found it.
Context (brief): What was reviewed, why, and when. Maximum 2 minutes or two slides.
Key findings: The three to five most significant findings, in order of priority. For each finding: what it is, what the evidence shows, and why it matters.
Recommendations: What you are recommending the executive do. Be direct. "I recommend we do X by Y because Z" not "one option that could be considered is..."
Decision required: If you need a decision, ask for it explicitly. State what you need, from whom, and when.
Supporting detail: Available for questions but not in the main presentation.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Lead with the finding, not the methodology. Executives do not need to know how you conducted the review before they know what you found.
- Make recommendations, not observations. A presentation that presents findings without recommendations transfers all the thinking burden to the executive. That is not advisory value.
- Be direct about bad news. Burying a critical finding in hedging language or presenting it after three positive findings signals that the presenter is uncomfortable with the message. The executive will notice.
- Anticipate the three hardest questions. For each major finding, prepare the answer to "are you sure?" "what would it cost to fix?" and "what happens if we do nothing?"
- Respect time. If you have 30 minutes, prepare 20 minutes of content. The executive may run long on one item and appreciate not being rushed.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good executive presentation ends with the executive knowing what you found, what you recommend, and what you need from them. If the executive asks "so what are you recommending?" at the end of a presentation, the recommendation was not clear enough. If the executive needs three follow-up meetings to understand the findings, the presentation was not prepared at the right level of detail.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Presentations to Queensland local government executives should be consistent with the council's governance framework. Findings that touch on legal, integrity, or CCC matters should be cleared with the relevant officer before presentation. Findings that will result in committee papers should be structured to support the paper-writing process. Presentations to the ARC should follow the committee's standard agenda format and be supported by a written paper. Findings involving personal information or sensitive personnel matters should not be presented in a group setting -- they require a private briefing with appropriate attendees only.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Opening: I want to lead with the most significant finding from this review. The council's contract management system is not generating alerts when contracts approach their approved value. As at March this year, four contracts had been exceeded without a variation being approved. The total unauthorised expenditure across those four contracts is $287,000. I will explain what we found and what I am recommending to address it.
Decision required: I am asking for two decisions today. First, that you approve the engagement of the system integrator to configure the contract value alert within the Ariba system -- estimated cost $45,000, and I can have a contract to you by end of week. Second, that you direct the four contract managers involved in the unauthorised expenditure to appear before you individually within the next two weeks so you can understand whether this is a training issue or a compliance issue. I need both decisions today to meet the timeline I have committed to the ARC for the September meeting.
