SKILL: How to Write a Service Review
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A service review is a structured assessment of whether a council service is being delivered effectively, efficiently, and in alignment with community need and council priorities. It examines what the service does, who it serves, how much it costs, how well it performs, and whether the current delivery model is the right one. Service reviews are typically initiated by management, internal audit, or council, and produce recommendations for service improvement, redesign, or divestment.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "conduct a service review" or "review this service"
- User needs to assess whether a council service is delivering value for money
- User is preparing a recommendation on service delivery model options (in-house, outsourced, partnership)
- User needs to respond to an audit recommendation or councillor inquiry about service performance
- User is preparing for a council budget review or service rationalisation process

STRUCTURE
Service description: What the service does, who it serves, the statutory or policy basis for delivering it, and the current delivery model.
Demand and need: How many people use the service, how demand has changed over time, and what unmet need exists.
Cost and efficiency: Total cost of service, cost per unit of output, comparison with benchmarks where available.
Performance: How well the service is performing against its stated objectives. Customer satisfaction, compliance, timeliness, quality.
Delivery model assessment: Options for how the service could be delivered, with an assessment of each.
Findings: What the review has identified as strengths, weaknesses, and opportunities.
Recommendations: Specific, actionable changes to service design, delivery, or resourcing. Prioritised.
Implementation: High-level plan for implementing the recommendations, including cost, timeline, and accountabilities.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Start with purpose. Before assessing how a service is delivered, confirm why it is delivered. A service that has no clear statutory requirement and low community demand should be questioned before being improved.
- Use data. Service reviews without performance data produce opinions, not findings. Collect usage, cost, satisfaction, and compliance data before drawing conclusions.
- Consider all delivery model options. In-house delivery is not automatically the right answer, nor is outsourcing. The review should assess all realistic options against defined criteria.
- Engage service users. A review that does not include any input from the people who use the service is incomplete.
- Be honest about poor performance. A service review that finds everything satisfactory is not credible unless the evidence genuinely supports that conclusion.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good service review is one that produces recommendations a decision-maker can act on with confidence. The findings must be supported by evidence, not assertion. The delivery model options must be assessed on consistent criteria. The recommendations must be costed and prioritised. If a recommendation says "improve performance" without specifying how, by how much, and at what cost, it is not a recommendation.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Service reviews for Queensland local governments should reference the council's service catalogue and the Corporate Plan service commitments. The QAO regularly reviews local government service delivery and their findings provide useful benchmarks. Service reviews involving significant service changes may require community consultation under LGA s4. Changes to service delivery that affect staff must comply with the relevant enterprise agreement and the Fair Work Act 2009. Outsourcing or privatisation of services that were previously delivered by council staff must comply with the council's procurement framework and the Queensland Government's market testing guidelines where applicable. Service reviews that identify compliance gaps must be reported to the relevant governance body.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Service description: The council's community transport service delivers door-to-door transport for elderly and disabled residents who cannot access public transport. The service is delivered by 12 council staff using 8 vehicles and serves approximately 340 registered clients. The statutory basis is the council's obligation under the Disability Discrimination Act 1992 and the council's Corporate Plan commitment to inclusive communities. Annual cost: $1.8M.
Findings: The service has experienced a 22% increase in demand over three years while operating within a flat budget. The cost per trip ($47.20) is 28% above the Queensland average for comparable local government services ($36.80). Client satisfaction is high (87% satisfied or very satisfied) but waitlist for new clients has grown from 2 weeks to 11 weeks. The current delivery model cannot meet demand without additional investment.
Recommendation 1: Council should engage with the National Disability Insurance Agency to assess whether the community transport service can be transitioned to an NDIS-funded model for eligible clients, reducing reliance on council's general rate revenue. Responsible: Director Community Services. Timeline: Business case to Finance Committee by September 2026. Estimated saving if implemented: $420,000 per annum.
