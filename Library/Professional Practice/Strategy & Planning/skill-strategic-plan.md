SKILL: How to Write a Strategic Plan
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A strategic plan sets the direction for an organisation, division, or service over a defined period -- typically three to five years. It defines what success looks like, what priorities will be pursued, and how performance will be measured. A strategic plan that is not connected to operational plans, budgets, and performance reporting is decoration. Its value is in the decisions it enables and the focus it creates.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write a strategic plan" or "develop a strategy" for a service, team, or organisation
- User needs to set direction for a business unit over the next three to five years
- User is preparing for a council or executive strategic planning process
- User needs to align a team's work program with the council's corporate plan
- User asks to "develop a strategic framework" or "define our priorities"

STRUCTURE
Executive summary: The plan in one page. Vision, three to five strategic priorities, and the headline measure of success for each.
Context: The operating environment. What has changed, what is changing, and what will change. Specific, not generic.
Vision: Where the organisation or service will be at the end of the plan period. One or two sentences. Specific enough to be tested.
Strategic priorities: Three to five priorities that will define the work of the period. Each priority should have: a clear statement of intent, the rationale for its selection, the key initiatives that will deliver it, and the measures that will confirm it has been achieved.
Enablers: What must be in place for the strategy to succeed -- capability, systems, culture, partnerships.
Performance framework: How progress will be tracked. Leading and lagging indicators. Reporting frequency and audience.
Risks: The key risks to strategy delivery. Not a comprehensive risk register -- the three to five risks that most threaten the plan's success.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Strategy is about choice. A plan that lists every possible priority is not a strategy -- it is a work program. A good strategic plan says what will not be done as clearly as what will.
- Priorities must be connected to resources. A strategic priority without a budget allocation and accountable executive is a wish list.
- Measures must be specific. "Improve community satisfaction" is not a measure. "Achieve a community satisfaction score of 75% or above in the annual survey by 2028" is a measure.
- Context drives content. A strategic plan that does not engage with the actual operating environment of the organisation -- its challenges, its stakeholders, its constraints -- will not survive first contact with reality.
- Review and adapt. A five-year strategic plan that is not reviewed annually and adjusted in response to changed conditions is already obsolete.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good strategic plan passes the "so what?" test at every element. If a priority statement, initiative, or measure could be removed without changing anyone's behaviour, it should be removed. A senior leader should be able to read the plan and know within five minutes what the organisation is trying to achieve, how they will know if it is succeeding, and what is not on the agenda.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Strategic plans for Queensland local governments must be consistent with the council's Corporate Plan, which is required under LGA s104(4). Divisional and service strategic plans should demonstrate explicit linkage to the Corporate Plan priorities. The council's Operational Plan (required under LGA s174) translates the Corporate Plan into annual commitments and is the mechanism for connecting strategy to budget. Strategic plans involving significant capital investment must be reflected in the Long-Term Financial Forecast (required under LGA s171) and Asset Management Plans. The Queensland Government's Advancing Queensland strategic framework provides a useful reference for aligning council priorities with state government directions.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Vision: By 2029, City Infrastructure will deliver asset management and capital works services that are consistently rated in the top quartile of Queensland local governments for cost efficiency, reliability, and community satisfaction.
Strategic priority: Strengthen our asset management capability. Rationale: The QAO's 2023-24 local government asset management report identified the council as below sector average on asset condition data completeness (62% vs 78% sector average). This priority addresses the foundational data gap that limits our ability to make evidence-based investment decisions. Key initiative: Complete a condition assessment of all Tier 1 transport assets by December 2026. Measure: Asset condition data completeness to reach 85% by December 2026 and 95% by December 2028.
