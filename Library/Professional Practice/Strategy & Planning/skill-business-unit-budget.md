SKILL: How to Develop a Business Unit Budget
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A business unit budget is the annual financial plan for a team, service, or division. It allocates resources to activities, justifies expenditure against planned outcomes, and provides the baseline against which actual performance will be measured throughout the year. A well-constructed budget is built from the bottom up on realistic assumptions, linked to the operational plan, and presented in a format that supports decision-making by the executive and Finance Committee.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "build the budget" or "prepare the budget submission" for their team
- User needs to justify a budget increase or new expenditure item
- User is preparing for the council's annual budget cycle
- User needs to explain budget variances or forecast a revised position
- User asks to "develop a zero-based budget" or "review our cost base"

STRUCTURE
Budget overview: Total expenditure and revenue, comparison with prior year, and headline variances explained.
Revenue: Each revenue line with basis for the estimate -- fees, grants, recoveries, internal charges. Assumptions stated.
Expenditure: Each significant cost category with basis for estimate. Employee costs, contractor costs, materials, overheads.
Capital: Any capital items included in the budget, with linkage to the Asset Management Plan.
New initiatives: Any items not in the prior year budget, with business case justification.
Savings and efficiencies: Any reductions proposed and the basis for them.
Key assumptions: The assumptions the budget depends on. If the assumptions prove wrong, what happens to the budget.
Risks and contingency: Known budget risks and any contingency provision.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Build from actuals, not from last year's budget. A budget that starts from last year's approved allocation and applies a percentage increase embeds historical inefficiency. Start from what was actually spent.
- Separate base from new. The budget should clearly distinguish between maintaining existing services (base budget) and funding new activities or initiatives (new budget). This makes trade-off conversations easier.
- State your assumptions explicitly. A budget that cannot be explained is a budget that cannot be defended. Every significant line should have a rationale.
- Budget for the whole year. Monthly phasing matters. A budget that has 80% of spend in the last quarter is either unrealistic or undeliverable.
- Own the forecast. The budget manager is responsible for the forecast throughout the year, not just at budget preparation. Surprises that could have been flagged earlier damage credibility.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good business unit budget is one that a Finance Committee member can scrutinise and understand without the budget manager in the room. Every significant line item should be explainable in two sentences. The connection between the budget and the operational plan should be visible. If the committee asks "what does this $300,000 deliver?" the answer should be in the budget documentation.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Queensland local government budgets must be adopted by council under LGA s169 before 1 August each year. The budget must include an operating budget, capital works budget, and long-term financial forecast. Business unit budgets feed into the consolidated council budget and must comply with the council's financial management framework and the Local Government Regulation 2012 financial reporting requirements. Budget amendments during the year require a s50 resolution in accordance with the council's financial management policy. The council's internal audit function tests compliance with budget management practices. The Queensland Treasury Corporation provides guidance on financial sustainability indicators that should be considered in budget development.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
New initiative justification: Additional $120,000 for a risk management system upgrade (SaaS licence). Current system was implemented in 2014 and does not support real-time reporting to the ARC. The upgrade will reduce manual reporting preparation time from 12 hours to 2 hours per quarter (saving approximately $18,000 in staff time per annum) and improve the quality of risk information available to governance bodies. Net additional cost after efficiency savings: $102,000 per annum. Business case approved by Director Corporate Services on 14 March 2026.
Key assumption: Employee costs assume a 2.5% enterprise agreement increment from 1 July 2026 and no unplanned vacancies above 5% of FTE. If vacancies exceed 5%, savings of approximately $45,000 per 1% of vacancy will partially offset any service delivery shortfall.
