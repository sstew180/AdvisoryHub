SKILL: How to Develop an Operational Plan
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
An operational plan translates strategic priorities and budget allocations into specific commitments for the year ahead. It defines what a team, service, or division will deliver, by when, at what cost, and how delivery will be confirmed. For Queensland local governments the annual Operational Plan is a statutory document required under LGA s174. A good operational plan is specific enough to be monitored quarterly and honest enough to reflect actual capacity.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write an operational plan" or "develop the annual plan"
- User needs to translate a strategic priority into year-one commitments
- User is preparing for the council's annual operational planning cycle
- User needs to document a team's work program for the year
- User asks to "set the team's priorities for the year" in a structured way

STRUCTURE
Overview: The service or business unit, the planning period, the total budget, and the headline commitments.
Alignment: How this plan connects to the Corporate Plan and any applicable strategic or master plans.
Deliverables: The specific outputs, services, or milestones the team will deliver during the year. Each deliverable should have: a clear description, a responsible officer, a target date, a budget allocation, and a measure of completion.
Performance measures: How the quality and effectiveness of services will be assessed. Both output measures (what was delivered) and outcome measures (what changed as a result).
Resource plan: Staff, budget, and key inputs required. Flagging any constraints or dependencies.
Risk summary: The key risks to plan delivery and proposed mitigations.
Reporting: How and when progress will be reported, and to whom.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Commitments must be deliverable. An operational plan that overcommits is worse than one that is modest but achievable. Under-delivery against published commitments attracts audit attention and community criticism.
- Every commitment needs an owner. A deliverable without a named responsible officer will not be delivered.
- Measures must be collected. Do not include performance measures that you do not have the systems to collect. An unmeasured measure is misleading.
- Connect to budget. Every significant deliverable should have a budget allocation. A plan that is not connected to the budget is not a plan.
- Plan for the whole year, not just the first quarter. Operational plans that front-load deliverables without accounting for year-end pressures set teams up to fail.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good operational plan is one that can be monitored quarterly without reinterpretation. If a committee member asks "is this commitment on track?" the responsible officer should be able to answer yes or no with specific evidence. If the answer is "it depends on how you define it," the commitment is not specific enough.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
The Queensland local government Operational Plan is required under LGA s174 and must be adopted by council before the start of the financial year. It must be consistent with the council's Corporate Plan and budget. The council must report on Operational Plan progress to the community, typically through quarterly reports to council and the annual report. The annual report must include a report on the council's performance against the Operational Plan (LGA s182). Internal audit programs should test compliance with significant Operational Plan commitments. For Gold Coast, the Operational Plan must align with the council's Divisional Plans and the Mayor's priorities for the term.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Deliverable: Complete the independent review of the council's enterprise risk management framework and present findings to the Audit and Risk Committee. Responsible officer: Manager Risk and Insurance. Target date: 30 September 2026. Budget: $85,000 (external review engagement). Measure of completion: ARC receives and notes the review report at the October 2026 meeting.
Performance measure: Percentage of high-rated risks with current treatment plans in place. Target: 100% by 30 June 2027. Data source: Enterprise Risk Register (quarterly review). Reporting: Quarterly ARC report.
