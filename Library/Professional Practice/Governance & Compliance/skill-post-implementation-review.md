SKILL: How to Conduct a Post-Implementation Review
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A post-implementation review (PIR) assesses whether a project, system, or change has delivered the outcomes that were promised in the business case. It measures actual benefits against projected benefits, actual costs against approved costs, and identifies whether the implementation approach worked. A PIR conducted six to twelve months after go-live provides the evidence base for future investment decisions and holds project sponsors and delivery teams accountable for the outcomes they committed to.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "conduct a post-implementation review" or "review whether the project delivered"
- User needs to assess whether a new system or process has delivered its promised benefits
- User is responding to an audit recommendation to complete a PIR
- User needs to report to a committee on whether an investment delivered value for money
- User asks to "check if the business case was right" after a project is complete

STRUCTURE
Review scope: What is being reviewed, the time elapsed since implementation, and the review period covered.
Original commitments: What the business case promised -- benefits, costs, timeline, and key assumptions.
Actual outcomes: What was delivered -- actual costs, actual timeline, and measurable benefits achieved.
Variance analysis: The difference between what was promised and what was delivered. For each significant variance, an explanation.
Lessons learned: What worked well, what did not, and what should be done differently on similar future projects.
Outstanding actions: Any implementation items that are not yet complete and the plan to complete them.
Recommendations: Any changes required to optimise the ongoing operation of the implemented solution.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Compare against the business case, not against current expectations. The purpose of the PIR is to hold the original commitment to account. If the business case said $500,000 in savings and $200,000 has been achieved, that is the finding -- regardless of whether $200,000 is considered acceptable now.
- Measure what was promised. A PIR that measures outcomes not in the business case cannot assess whether the investment delivered. Return to the original benefit statements.
- Allow sufficient time. A PIR conducted one month after go-live will not capture benefits that take time to materialise. Six to twelve months is the typical window for a meaningful assessment.
- Be honest about failures. A PIR that finds everything on track when the business case projected $600,000 in savings and none have been measured is not credible.
- Use the findings. A PIR that produces findings but does not result in any changes to future practice has not fulfilled its purpose.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good PIR is one that a Finance Committee member can read and know whether the investment delivered what was promised. Every benefit in the business case should be assessed -- not just the ones that look good. The variance explanation should be specific enough to improve future business cases. If the PIR cannot explain why a $300,000 projected benefit was not achieved, the review has not gone deep enough.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Post-implementation reviews for Queensland local government should be conducted for all projects above the council's significant procurement threshold and for all major system implementations. The council's project management framework will specify the PIR requirement and timing. PIR findings should be reported to the Finance Committee or ARC where the original investment required their approval. Where the PIR identifies that claimed savings have not materialised, the budget team should be notified so future budgets are adjusted accordingly. The QAO regularly reviews whether councils complete PIRs for significant investments -- the absence of a PIR process is a common management letter finding. For ICT projects, the Queensland Government's ICT project governance framework provides a useful PIR template.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Variance analysis: The business case projected a reduction in manual data processing time from 22 hours per week to 4 hours per week, generating annual savings of $68,000. At the 9-month review, actual processing time has reduced to 11 hours per week, generating estimated savings of $36,000 per annum -- 53% of the projected benefit. The underperformance is attributable to two causes: (1) the data migration did not transfer all historical records, requiring manual lookups for approximately 30% of transactions; and (2) three staff members whose roles were expected to be redeployed have not yet been redeployed due to a hiring freeze. The data migration issue is expected to be resolved by September 2026 when the data cleansing project is complete. The redeployment issue requires a separate HR decision.
