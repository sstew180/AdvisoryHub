SKILL: How to Write a Procedure
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A procedure is a step-by-step document that describes how a specific task or process is carried out. It tells the person doing the work exactly what to do, in what order, and to what standard. A good procedure is specific enough to guide a new officer without being so prescriptive that it prevents experienced officers from exercising judgement. Procedures exist to ensure consistency, compliance, and quality.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write a procedure" or "document a process"
- User needs to standardise how a task is performed across a team
- User is responding to an audit finding that a process lacks documentation
- User needs to capture institutional knowledge before a key officer leaves
- User asks to "create a standard operating procedure" or "write the process up"

STRUCTURE
Procedure title: Clear, verb-first. "Processing a contract variation request" not "Contract variations."
Purpose: One sentence. Why this procedure exists and what it ensures.
Scope: Who follows this procedure and in what circumstances.
Related documents: The policy, legislation, or standard this procedure implements.
Definitions: Any technical terms or system names that need to be defined.
Steps: Numbered sequential steps. Each step is one action. Who does it, what they do, what system or form they use, and what the output is.
Decision points: Where the process branches based on a decision, use clear if/then language.
Escalation: What to do if the process cannot be completed as described.
Records: What records must be kept, where, and for how long.
Review date: When this procedure will next be reviewed.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- One step, one action. A step that contains multiple actions ("complete the form and submit it to your manager and update the register") will be followed inconsistently.
- Write for the person doing the work, not the manager reviewing it. Procedures that use management language rather than operational language are not followed.
- Include the "why" for complex steps. Where a step is not self-explanatory, one sentence of context prevents shortcuts.
- Test the procedure before publishing. Ask someone who does not currently know the process to follow it step by step. If they get stuck, the procedure is incomplete.
- Keep it current. An outdated procedure that conflicts with current practice is worse than no procedure -- it creates compliance confusion and audit findings.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good procedure is one that a new officer could follow accurately on their first day without assistance. If the procedure requires knowledge not contained in the document, that knowledge must be added. Procedures should be tested annually against actual practice -- if what people do diverges from what the procedure says, either the procedure or the practice needs to change.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Procedures for Queensland local government must be consistent with applicable legislation, council policy, and enterprise agreement requirements. Procedures that involve public records must comply with the Public Records Act 2002 and the council's retention and disposal schedule. Procedures involving personal information must comply with the Information Privacy Act 2009. Procedures that change existing work practices may require consultation with employee representatives under the relevant enterprise agreement. The council's document management system and naming conventions should be applied. Audit-sensitive procedures (procurement, financial approvals, conflict of interest) should be reviewed by internal audit before finalisation.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Procedure title: Receiving and recording a contract variation request
Step 1: When a contractor submits a written variation request, the Contract Manager records receipt in the contract management system (Ariba) within one business day. The entry must include: date received, contractor name, contract number, brief description of the variation, and the contractor's claimed cost.
Step 2: The Contract Manager assesses whether the variation is within scope of the contract or constitutes a new procurement. If the variation is outside contract scope, go to Step 6 (Refer to Procurement team). If the variation is within scope, proceed to Step 3.
Step 3: The Contract Manager obtains an independent cost estimate from the council's quantity surveyor within five business days. If the estimated cost exceeds $50,000, the Contract Manager must also obtain a second independent estimate.
