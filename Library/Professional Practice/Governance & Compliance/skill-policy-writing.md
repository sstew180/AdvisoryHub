SKILL: How to Write a Policy
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A policy is a formal statement of the council's position, intent, or rules on a specific matter. It sets the direction and boundaries within which decisions will be made and actions taken. A policy is not a procedure (which describes how to do something), not a guideline (which provides advice without binding authority), and not a strategy (which describes direction over time). Policies must be enforceable, current, and clearly linked to their legislative or governance basis.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write a policy" or "draft a council policy"
- User needs to establish the council's formal position on a topic
- User is reviewing or updating an existing policy
- User needs to develop a policy framework for a new area of council activity
- User asks to "set the rules" for a particular business activity in a formal document

STRUCTURE
Policy title: Clear, specific, and searchable.
Policy statement: One to three sentences. The council's position on the subject. What the council will do or require.
Purpose: Why this policy exists. The problem it addresses or the obligation it fulfils.
Scope: Who and what this policy applies to. Be specific about inclusions and exclusions.
Definitions: Key terms used in the policy, defined precisely.
Policy requirements: The specific rules, standards, or requirements. Numbered. Written in plain English. Each requirement should be independently enforceable.
Roles and responsibilities: Who is responsible for implementing, monitoring, and enforcing the policy.
Related documents: Legislation, other policies, procedures, and guidelines that relate to this policy.
Review date: When this policy will next be reviewed. Maximum three years for most policies.
Approval: Who approved the policy and when.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Policies set requirements, not aspirations. "The council values transparency" is not a policy requirement. "Officers must disclose conflicts of interest within 24 hours of becoming aware of them" is a policy requirement.
- Every requirement must be enforceable. If the council cannot or will not enforce a requirement, it should not be in the policy.
- Scope must be unambiguous. A policy that applies to "all staff" in one clause and "employees" in another has a scope problem.
- Policies must be current. An out-of-date policy that conflicts with current legislation is worse than no policy -- it creates compliance confusion.
- Plain English is mandatory. A policy that officers cannot understand will not be followed.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good policy is one that an officer can read in five minutes and know exactly what they are required to do, who is responsible, and what happens if they do not comply. If the policy requires interpretation to apply, it is not clear enough. Policies should be tested against a realistic scenario before finalisation -- "what would an officer do if they encountered situation X?" If the answer is not clear from the policy, the policy needs revision.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Queensland local government policies must be consistent with the Local Government Act 2009 and applicable subordinate legislation. Certain policies are mandatory under the LGA, including procurement policy (LGR s198), code of conduct (LGA s213), and complaints management (LGA s268). The council's policy framework should specify the hierarchy between council policies, CEO policies, and operational guidelines. Policies involving privacy must comply with the Information Privacy Act 2009. Policies involving employment must comply with the Fair Work Act 2009 and the relevant enterprise agreement. The council's policy register should be publicly accessible in accordance with the LGA transparency requirements.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Policy statement: The City of Gold Coast requires all officers involved in procurement, contract management, or financial decision-making to identify and declare conflicts of interest before participating in any related decision, assessment, or approval process.
Policy requirement 3: An officer must not participate in a procurement or contract management decision in which they have a personal, financial, or professional interest, unless the conflict has been declared, recorded in the conflict of interest register, and managed in accordance with the approved management plan. An interest includes interests held by the officer's immediate family members or entities in which the officer holds a financial stake.
