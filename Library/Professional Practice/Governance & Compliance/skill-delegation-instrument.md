SKILL: How to Write a Delegation Instrument
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A delegation instrument is a formal legal document by which a person or body with a statutory power or function authorises another person to exercise that power or perform that function on their behalf. In local government, delegations are the mechanism by which council delegates its powers to the CEO, and the CEO sub-delegates powers to officers. A delegation instrument that is unclear, outdated, or inconsistent with the enabling legislation creates legal risk for every decision made under it.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "draft a delegation" or "prepare a delegation instrument"
- User needs to sub-delegate a power from the CEO to an officer
- User is reviewing or updating the council's delegation register
- User needs to confirm whether a particular decision is within a delegate's authority
- User asks to "check the delegation" before approving a transaction or decision

STRUCTURE
Instrument title: Identifies the delegating body, the delegate, and the subject matter.
Enabling provision: The specific section of the Act or Regulation under which the power is delegated. Must be cited precisely.
Power being delegated: The specific power, function, or discretion being delegated. Use the language of the enabling legislation.
Delegate: The office or title (not the name of the individual holding it) to whom the power is delegated.
Conditions and limitations: Any constraints on how the power may be exercised. Financial limits, subject matter restrictions, approval requirements.
Sub-delegation authority: Whether the delegate may further sub-delegate the power and, if so, to whom.
Commencement: The date from which the delegation takes effect.
Revocation: Any previous delegation on the same matter that is revoked by this instrument.
Signature: The delegating authority's signature and date.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Cite the enabling provision precisely. A delegation that does not cite the specific section of the Act conferring the power to delegate is invalid. The power to delegate must itself be authorised by legislation.
- Delegate the office, not the person. Delegations run with the role, not the individual. When the person changes, the delegation continues.
- Financial limits must be clear. A delegation to "approve procurement" without a financial limit is a delegation to approve any procurement. Limits must be in dollar amounts, not in descriptions.
- Conditions must be enforceable. A condition that "the delegate must consult the Director before exercising this power" is unenforceable if it is not monitored. Include only conditions you intend to enforce.
- Keep the register current. An outdated delegation register creates governance risk. Every role change, legislative amendment, and organisational restructure requires a review of affected delegations.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good delegation instrument is one that a lawyer, auditor, or court could read and confirm: the delegating body had the power to delegate, the power delegated is within the scope of the enabling legislation, the delegate is identifiable by office, and the conditions are clear. If any of those four elements is missing or ambiguous, the delegation is legally deficient.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Delegations for Queensland local government are primarily governed by LGA s257 (power of local government to delegate to the chief executive officer) and s259 (power of CEO to sub-delegate). Not all powers can be delegated -- LGA s257(3) specifies powers that must be exercised by council itself, including adopting the budget, making local laws, and levying rates. Delegations must be consistent with the delegating legislation -- a power that the LGA does not authorise to be delegated cannot be delegated regardless of what the instrument says. The council's delegation register must be maintained and made available for public inspection under LGA s257(5). Delegations are frequently examined by the QAO and the CCC -- an outdated or incomplete delegation register is a common audit finding. Officers exercising delegated powers must act within the four corners of the delegation instrument.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Instrument: Sub-Delegation of Power to Approve Contract Variations
Enabling provision: This instrument is made under section 259 of the Local Government Act 2009 (Qld), pursuant to the delegation granted to the Chief Executive Officer by resolution of the City of Gold Coast Council on [date].
Power delegated: The power to approve variations to contracts for the supply of goods, services, or works, where: (a) the variation is within the approved scope of the contract; (b) the cumulative value of the variation does not cause the total contract value to exceed the officer's financial delegation limit as set out in the Financial Delegation Register; and (c) the variation does not cause the contract to become a Significant Contract as defined in the council's Procurement Policy.
Delegate: Director Infrastructure and Place, or in their absence, the Acting Director Infrastructure and Place.
Financial limit: Variations up to $500,000 (GST exclusive) in cumulative variation value per contract.
