SKILL: How to Write a Media Release
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A media release is a document sent to journalists to announce news, respond to an issue, or provide information for publication. Its purpose is to give journalists the facts they need to write an accurate story while framing the council's position clearly. A media release is not a report and not an advertisement. It must be newsworthy, factual, and written in a style that a journalist can use directly or with minimal editing.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write a media release" or "draft a press release"
- User needs to announce a council decision, project milestone, or community initiative
- User needs to respond to public interest in a council matter
- User asks to "communicate this to the media" about a significant event or announcement
- User needs to correct misinformation in public circulation

STRUCTURE
Headline: One line. The news, not the context. Active verb. No jargon.
Lead paragraph: Who, what, when, where, why. All in one short paragraph. The most important information first.
Body: Supporting detail in descending order of importance. Each paragraph should be able to be cut from the bottom without losing the core story.
Quote: One quote from a named spokesperson (Mayor, CEO, or Director). The quote must add something -- opinion, context, or emphasis -- not just repeat the facts.
Background: Factual context that gives the story depth without cluttering the lead.
Ends / Media contact: "ENDS" on its own line. Then: Contact name, title, phone, email.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Lead with the news. The headline and first paragraph must contain the story. Do not build to the point.
- Write for the journalist, not the organisation. The journalist is asking "why does this matter to the community?" Answer that question in the first paragraph.
- One story per release. A media release that tries to announce three things announces nothing.
- Quotes must sound like a person speaking, not like a corporate statement. "We are committed to delivering excellent outcomes for our community" is not a quote. "This project will cut travel times for 40,000 commuters by 15 minutes each day" is a quote.
- Keep it short. 400 words maximum. 300 is better. Journalists do not read the second page.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good media release is one a journalist can use to write an accurate 200-word news story without making a single follow-up call. If the release is missing the who, what, when, where, or why -- or if the quote is unusable corporate language -- it will not be used. The lead paragraph should be strong enough to stand alone as a social media post.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Media releases for Queensland local governments must comply with the council's communications policy and media protocol. Releases involving financial matters must not contain forward-looking statements that could be misleading under the council's financial management obligations. Releases involving legal matters, CCC investigations, or matters before the courts must be cleared by the council's legal team before distribution. The Queensland Government's Advertising and Communication Activities Policy provides a useful reference for communications standards. Releases about significant decisions should align with the council resolution and not go beyond what the council has actually decided.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Headline: Gold Coast opens Queensland's largest community solar precinct
Lead: The City of Gold Coast today opened the 2.4-megawatt Pimpama Community Solar Precinct, delivering clean energy to 1,200 homes and saving ratepayers $180,000 annually in energy costs.
Quote: "This is the largest community solar installation in Queensland and it delivers real savings to residents from day one," Mayor Tom Tate said. "We committed to making Gold Coast a renewable energy leader and this precinct is evidence of that commitment being delivered."
Background: The Pimpama precinct was funded through a $3.2M investment approved by Council in February 2025. Construction was completed six weeks ahead of schedule by Queensland contractor SolarTech Group. The precinct is expected to offset 3,400 tonnes of carbon emissions annually.
