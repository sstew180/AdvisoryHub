SKILL: How to Write a Community Newsletter Article
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A community newsletter article communicates council news, projects, services, or initiatives to residents in plain, engaging language. It is not a media release, not a committee paper, and not a technical report. Its purpose is to inform and engage the community in a way that is accessible, relevant, and readable to a general audience including people with limited English, older residents, and people who do not follow council closely.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write a newsletter article" or "prepare content for the council newsletter"
- User needs to communicate a project update, service change, or community initiative to residents
- User is preparing content for the council's digital or print community magazine
- User needs to translate a technical or governance matter into plain community language
- User asks to "write something for residents" about a council decision or program

STRUCTURE
Headline: Active, specific, community-focused. What is in it for the reader.
Introduction: The key information in two to three sentences. What is happening, where, and when.
Body: Supporting detail in plain language. What residents need to know. What they need to do, if anything.
Quote: One quote from a relevant councillor or senior officer. Human and specific, not corporate.
Call to action: What residents should do -- visit a website, attend an event, contact the council. With specific details.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Write for a year 8 reading level. Not because residents are unsimpleminded -- because clear, simple language reaches more people and respects their time.
- Answer "what does this mean for me?" in the first paragraph. Residents read newsletters to understand how council decisions affect their lives, not to follow council processes.
- Avoid jargon. "The council has resolved to allocate capital works funding to the Burleigh Heads streetscape renewal project" should be "Burleigh Heads is getting a new look -- here is what is planned."
- One article, one message. A newsletter article that tries to cover three projects covers none of them well.
- Include specific details. Dates, locations, phone numbers, websites. Vague articles frustrate readers.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good newsletter article passes the "would my neighbour read this?" test. It should be something a busy resident with no particular interest in council governance would read and find useful. If the article requires knowledge of council processes or terminology to be understood, it needs to be rewritten.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Community newsletter articles for Queensland local governments must comply with the council's communications policy and brand guidelines. Articles about council decisions must accurately reflect what was actually resolved -- do not characterise a decision differently from the council resolution. Articles involving projects under procurement must not disclose tender details or preferred supplier information. Articles about development applications or planning matters must be factual and not pre-empt outcomes. The council's communications team should review articles before publication to ensure consistency with other council communications.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Headline: Your new Coomera community centre is taking shape -- here is what to expect
Introduction: Construction of the new Coomera Community Centre is on track for completion in December 2026. The $12 million facility will include a gym, multipurpose hall, meeting rooms, and a dedicated youth space.
Quote: "This centre has been at the top of the community's wish list for years," said Division 1 Councillor Jane Smith. "When it opens, it will be the heart of a community that has grown enormously over the past decade."
Call to action: Want to have your say on the centre's programs and activities? Join the community reference group by visiting council.goldcoast.qld.gov.au/coomera-centre or calling 1300 GOLDCOAST before 30 June.
