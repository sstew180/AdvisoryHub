SKILL: How to Prepare for a Media Interview
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A media interview preparation document gives a spokesperson -- typically the Mayor, CEO, or Director -- the key messages, likely questions, and bridging responses they need to handle a media interview effectively. It is not a script. It is a structured preparation tool that gives the spokesperson confidence, consistency, and the factual grounding to handle difficult questions without being caught unprepared.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "prepare talking points for a media interview"
- User needs to brief a spokesperson before a press conference or journalist call
- User asks to "help prepare for a tough interview" or "what will they ask?"
- User needs to anticipate hostile questions and prepare responses
- User is preparing for a council media opportunity on a sensitive or complex topic

STRUCTURE
Context: What is the interview about, who is the journalist, what outlet, and what is the likely angle.
Key messages: The 3 messages the spokesperson must land regardless of what questions are asked. Short, plain, memorable.
Factual grounding: The specific facts, figures, and dates the spokesperson may need. No assumptions.
Likely questions: The questions the journalist is most likely to ask, including the hardest ones.
Suggested responses: A short response to each likely question. Not a script -- a framework.
Bridging phrases: How to move from a difficult question back to a key message without appearing evasive.
What not to say: Specific phrases, positions, or topics to avoid and why.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Prepare for the hardest question, not the easiest. The spokesperson who has only prepared for friendly questions will be derailed by the first difficult one.
- Key messages must be repeated. Media interviews are won or lost on repetition. Three messages, stated clearly, stated often.
- Never speculate. If the spokesperson does not know the answer, the response is "I will find out and come back to you" not a guess.
- Bridging is a skill. "That is an important question, and what I want to make sure people understand is..." moves the conversation without appearing evasive if used judiciously.
- Do not say "no comment." It implies something is being hidden. "I am not in a position to discuss that matter while it is before the courts" is a complete response.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A well-prepared spokesperson can deliver all three key messages, answer the hardest anticipated question factually, and handle an unexpected question by bridging to a key message. If the spokesperson leaves the interview having said something factually incorrect, the preparation has failed.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Queensland local government spokespeople operate within the council's media policy and communications protocols. Elected officials (Mayor, councillors) speak on behalf of the council on policy and political matters. Officers speak on operational and technical matters. This distinction must be maintained in interview preparation. Matters before the council (resolutions not yet made) should not be pre-empted by officers. Matters involving legal proceedings, CCC investigations, or QIRC matters require legal clearance before comment. Queensland's Right to Information Act 2009 and Information Privacy Act 2009 obligations apply to statements made about council information.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Key message 1: The City of Gold Coast takes infrastructure safety seriously and acted immediately when the issue was identified.
Key message 2: No properties were at risk and all affected residents were notified within four hours.
Key message 3: An independent review has been commissioned and we will make the findings public.
Likely hard question: Why wasn't this identified earlier in your maintenance program?
Suggested response: Our maintenance program follows a risk-based schedule set by independent engineers. The last inspection rated this asset as low risk. We have commissioned an independent review specifically to understand whether the inspection methodology needs to change, and we will make those findings public.
What not to say: Do not speculate on whether the maintenance contractor is liable. Do not confirm or deny whether legal action is being considered.
