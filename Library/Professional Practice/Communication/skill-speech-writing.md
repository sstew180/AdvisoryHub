SKILL: How to Write a Speech or Address
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A speech or address is a verbal communication delivered by a senior leader -- typically the Mayor, CEO, or Director -- at a public event, ceremony, community meeting, or council function. It is distinct from a briefing or media statement in that it is designed to be spoken aloud, to an audience, for a defined purpose and duration. A well-written speech is conversational, structured, and delivered in the voice of the speaker rather than the writer.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write a speech" or "prepare remarks" for an event
- User needs to prepare the Mayor or CEO for a public address
- User is writing opening remarks for a community meeting or council event
- User needs to prepare a formal address for a ceremony, launch, or award
- User asks to "draft talking points" for a keynote or panel presentation

STRUCTURE
Opening: A line or two that establishes connection with the audience. Acknowledge the occasion, the place, and key guests. Not a list of titles -- pick two or three acknowledgements at most.
Purpose: One or two sentences stating what this speech is about and why it matters today.
Body: Two or three key points. Each point is a short narrative segment, not a list. Stories, examples, and specifics make points memorable.
Call to action or closing message: What the speaker wants the audience to take away, do, or feel.
Closing: A brief, warm close that returns to the occasion.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Write for the ear, not the eye. Short sentences. Plain words. Rhythm matters. Read it aloud as you write it.
- Write in the speaker's voice. If the Mayor speaks conversationally, do not write in formal bureaucratic language. The speech should sound like the person.
- Specificity beats generality. "This park has served this community for 40 years -- it is where the Robina under-10s played their first grand final and where Sandra Morrison held her 90th birthday" is memorable. "This park has been an important community asset" is not.
- Respect the time allocation. A five-minute speech is approximately 700 words. Overrunning disrespects the audience and the program.
- Acknowledge the audience, not the bureaucracy. The audience came to hear something meaningful, not a recitation of project costs and funding sources.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good speech is one the speaker can deliver without looking at the page. If it requires constant reference to notes, it is too complex. Read it aloud and time it before finalising. If it sounds stilted when spoken, rewrite the stilted sentences. The opening and closing lines are the ones the audience will remember -- invest time in those two moments.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Speeches at Queensland local government events should acknowledge the Traditional Owners of the land in an appropriate acknowledgement of country. For formal events this should be a Welcome to Country delivered by a Traditional Owner, or an Acknowledgement of Country delivered by the speaker. The council's reconciliation action plan will specify the preferred form. For events involving state or federal government representatives, protocol requires acknowledgement of those representatives in the correct order of precedence. Speeches at council-related events should not contain partisan political commentary or pre-empt council decisions that have not yet been made.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Opening: I want to start by acknowledging the Yugambeh people, the Traditional Owners of this land, and pay my respects to their elders past, present, and emerging. [To the audience:] Thank you for being here. This is one of those days in a city's life that you actually remember.
Body point: When we broke ground on this facility three years ago, a lot of people told us it was too ambitious. They said a city our size didn't need an indoor sports centre of this scale. What they didn't count on was the 40,000 athletes in this city who had been training in facilities built for a population half our size. Tonight, that changes.
Closing: Gold Coast is a city that builds things. Not just infrastructure -- we build community, we build opportunity, and we build futures. Tonight we've built something else. We've built a promise kept. Thank you.
