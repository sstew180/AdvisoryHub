SKILL: How to Handle a Difficult Conversation
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
This skill helps officers prepare for and conduct conversations that involve conflict, disagreement, performance concerns, unwelcome news, or sensitive personal matters. Difficult conversations that are avoided or handled poorly cause lasting damage to working relationships, team performance, and organisational culture. This skill covers preparation, structure, and follow-through for the most common types of difficult workplace conversation in a local government context.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User needs to address a performance or conduct concern with a team member
- User needs to deliver unwelcome news (restructure, role change, rejected application)
- User needs to resolve a conflict between team members or with a colleague
- User asks to "prepare for a hard conversation" or "how do I handle this situation"
- User needs to address a complaint or grievance informally before it escalates

STRUCTURE
Step 1 — Clarify your purpose: What outcome do you want from this conversation? Be specific. "I want this person to understand that their behaviour is affecting the team" is a purpose. "I want to sort this out" is not.
Step 2 — Prepare the facts: What specifically has happened? Dates, examples, observable behaviour. Not interpretations or second-hand accounts.
Step 3 — Anticipate their response: How is the other person likely to react? What are the most likely objections, denials, or counter-arguments?
Step 4 — Choose the setting: Private, uninterrupted, sufficient time. Not in a corridor, not at the end of the day before a weekend.
Step 5 — Open directly: State the purpose of the conversation in the first 30 seconds. Do not build to it with small talk.
Step 6 — State the issue with evidence: Describe what you have observed, specifically. "On Tuesday you spoke over two colleagues in the team meeting and dismissed their contributions" not "you can be dismissive sometimes."
Step 7 — Listen: Give the other person time to respond. Do not interrupt or defend your position while they are speaking.
Step 8 — Agree on next steps: What will change, by when, and how will you both know it has changed?
Step 9 — Document: A brief note of what was discussed and agreed. Sent by email the same day.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Do not avoid difficult conversations. Avoidance makes the situation worse and signals to the team that the behaviour is acceptable.
- Separate the person from the behaviour. The conversation is about what the person did or said, not about who they are.
- Be specific. Vague feedback cannot be acted on and will be disputed.
- Listen to understand, not to respond. The other person's perspective matters even if you disagree with it.
- Document promptly. If the conversation later becomes part of a formal process, the contemporaneous note is evidence.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A well-handled difficult conversation results in a shared understanding of the issue, a clear agreed outcome, and a documented record. If the other person leaves the conversation unclear about what specifically was raised or what they are expected to do differently, the conversation has not achieved its purpose.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Difficult conversations in Queensland local government must be consistent with the council's code of conduct, human resources policies, and the Fair Work Act 2009 obligations for managing workplace behaviour. For conversations involving potential misconduct, the council's HR team should be consulted before the conversation occurs. Where a complaint or conduct matter may involve the CCC, early consultation with the integrity team is essential. Officers conducting difficult conversations with direct reports should be aware of the council's whistleblower protection obligations under the Public Interest Disclosure Act 2010 -- a team member raising concerns about a colleague's conduct may be making a public interest disclosure and must not be treated adversely as a result.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Opening: "I've asked to meet with you today because I want to talk about something that has been affecting the team. I want to be direct with you because I think that is more respectful than leaving it unaddressed."
Stating the issue: "In the last two team meetings, on 8 and 15 April, you spoke over Sarah and James when they were presenting their sections of the report. On both occasions, I observed them disengage from the rest of the meeting. I am concerned this is affecting how safe people feel to contribute."
Closing: "What I would like to agree on today is that in our next team meeting you will let colleagues finish before responding. I will check in with you after that meeting. Does that work for you?"
