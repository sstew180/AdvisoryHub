SKILL: How to Write a Submission to Government
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A submission to government is a formal written response to a consultation, inquiry, review, or call for submissions by a government body -- including Queensland parliamentary committees, state government departments, the Local Government Association of Queensland, or regulatory bodies. It represents the council's official position on a matter and may be published publicly. A well-written submission is evidence-based, clearly structured, and directly responsive to the questions asked.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "prepare a submission" to a parliamentary inquiry or government review
- User needs to respond to a call for submissions on proposed legislation or policy
- User is preparing the council's response to a regulatory consultation
- User needs to contribute the council's position to an LGAQ policy development process
- User asks to "write a response" to a government discussion paper or options paper

STRUCTURE
Introduction: Who is making the submission, in what capacity, and what is the council's interest in the subject matter.
Executive summary: The council's key positions in 3-5 dot points. Decision-makers often read only this.
Responses to consultation questions: Address each question asked in the consultation document in sequence. Do not skip questions. If the council has no view on a question, say so briefly.
Council's position: Where the submission goes beyond the questions asked, state the council's position clearly with supporting rationale.
Evidence and examples: Specific examples from the council's experience that support the positions taken. Quantified where possible.
Recommendations: What the council is asking the government to do. Numbered, specific, and actionable.
Conclusion: One paragraph summarising the council's engagement and willingness to participate further.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Answer the questions asked. A submission that addresses what the council wants to say rather than what was asked will be disregarded.
- Evidence first. Assertions without evidence carry little weight in formal consultation processes. Use data, case studies, and specific examples.
- Be constructive. A submission that only criticises without offering alternatives will not influence the outcome.
- Represent the council's position, not individuals. The submission must reflect a position that has been endorsed by the appropriate governance body -- CEO, Director, or Council resolution as appropriate.
- Observe confidentiality. Submissions are usually published. Do not include information the council would not want made public.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good submission is one that a committee member or departmental officer can read and understand the council's position without needing to contact the council for clarification. The recommendations must be specific enough to be actioned -- "the government should consider the impact on local government" is not a recommendation. "The proposed amendment to s198 of the Local Government Regulation 2012 should be deferred pending a quantified impact assessment on Category 3-8 councils" is a recommendation.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Submissions to Queensland parliamentary committees must be lodged through the committee's formal submission process and become public documents once tabled. The council's CEO or Mayor must endorse submissions on significant matters. Submissions involving financial or legal positions should be reviewed by the relevant technical team before lodgement. The LGAQ coordinates sector-wide submissions on matters affecting all Queensland councils -- the City of Gold Coast should be aware of the LGAQ position before lodging its own submission to ensure consistency or to flag where Gold Coast's position differs from the sector view. Submissions to the Queensland Competition Authority or Independent Assessor must follow those bodies' specific procedural requirements.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Introduction: The City of Gold Coast is Queensland's largest local government by population, serving 650,000 residents across a $3.8 billion annual operating budget. This submission is made in response to the Department of State Development's consultation on proposed amendments to the Local Government Regulation 2012 regarding strategic contracting thresholds.
Recommendation 1: The proposed increase in the significant contracting threshold from $1M to $2M should not proceed without a mandatory Significant Contracting Plan requirement for contracts between $500,000 and $2M. Without this safeguard, the amendment will reduce accountability for a significant volume of council expenditure with no countervailing governance mechanism.
