SKILL: How to Write an Executive Briefing
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: General
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
An executive briefing is a short, structured document that gives a senior leader — typically a CEO, Director-General, Director, or Mayor — the information they need to understand a situation, make a decision, or prepare for a meeting or public engagement. It is shorter and more strategic than a standard briefing note, focused on what the executive needs to know and do rather than on background and process.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "prepare an executive briefing" or "brief the CEO/Director/Mayor"
- User asks to "prepare talking points" for a senior leader before a meeting
- User asks to "summarise the situation for the executive team"
- User asks to "prepare a one-pager" for a Director or CEO
- User needs to brief an elected official before a council meeting or media engagement

STRUCTURE
Purpose: One sentence. What is this briefing for, who is it for, and by when does the executive need it.
Situation: 2-3 sentences maximum. What has happened or what is the current position. Stick to facts.
Key issues: The 2-3 things the executive most needs to understand. Each issue in a short paragraph, not a bullet list.
Recommended position or action: What the executive should say, decide, or do. Be direct.
If asked: Anticipated questions and short, factual answers. Maximum 3 questions.
Contact: Name, title, phone number of the officer who can provide further detail.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Write for the reader, not the writer. The executive is busy, under time pressure, and needs to act.
- Lead with the recommended position or action, not with background.
- One page maximum. Two pages only for genuinely complex matters with significant financial or political stakes.
- Every assertion must be factually grounded. Do not include claims that cannot be supported if challenged.
- Anticipate the question "so what do I do?" and answer it directly.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good executive briefing passes three tests: (1) the executive can read it in under two minutes and know exactly what to do; (2) if challenged by a journalist or councillor, every statement in it is defensible; (3) it contains no information the executive does not need. If the executive would have to ask a follow-up question to understand the situation, the briefing has failed.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Executive briefings for Queensland local government officers should reflect the governance framework under the Local Government Act 2009. Where a briefing relates to a decision, it should identify the delegated authority under which the decision will be made (LGA s257) or whether it requires council resolution. Briefings going to elected officials should be factual and avoid speculation. The Queensland Government's plain English writing standards apply.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Purpose: This briefing prepares the CEO for the media enquiry received at 2.00 pm today regarding the Coomera water main failure and Council's response.
Recommended position: The CEO should confirm that Council became aware of the failure at 6.30 am, that 1,400 properties were affected, and that supply was restored by 11.45 am. Council should not speculate on cause until the contractor's report is received on Friday.
If asked — Was Council warned about the risk of failure? Council completed a condition assessment of the Coomera main in October 2024. The assessment rated the main as requiring planned replacement within five years. Emergency repair was not flagged as an immediate requirement.
