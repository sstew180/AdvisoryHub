SKILL: How to Write a Conflict of Interest Declaration
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A conflict of interest declaration is a formal document in which an officer discloses a personal, financial, or professional interest that may -- or may appear to -- conflict with their ability to act impartially in a council matter. The declaration does not mean the officer has done anything wrong. It is a transparency mechanism that protects both the officer and the council. An undisclosed conflict of interest that later becomes known is a far more serious matter than a disclosed and managed one.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write a conflict of interest declaration" or "declare a conflict of interest"
- User has identified a potential conflict and needs to document and manage it
- User is participating in a procurement, assessment, or decision where they have a personal interest
- User needs to understand whether a particular interest requires declaration
- User asks to "help me disclose my interest" in a specific matter

STRUCTURE
Declarant details: Name, position, date, and matter to which the declaration relates.
Nature of the interest: A clear description of the interest -- what the relationship or financial interest is and how it connects to the council matter.
How the interest was identified: When the officer became aware of the potential conflict.
Assessment of materiality: Whether the interest is material (likely to influence or appear to influence the decision) or non-material (unlikely to affect impartiality).
Proposed management: How the conflict will be managed -- recusal, restricted access to information, supervised involvement, or other arrangement.
Supervisor/manager acknowledgement: Confirmation that the officer's manager has been informed and agrees with the management approach.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- When in doubt, declare. An officer who is uncertain whether an interest needs to be declared should declare it. The cost of an unnecessary declaration is low. The cost of an undisclosed conflict that later becomes an issue is high.
- Describe the interest specifically. "I have a personal relationship with a supplier" is not a declaration. "My brother-in-law, James Chen, is a director of BuildRight Pty Ltd, which has submitted a tender for Contract 2026-047" is a declaration.
- Declare early. A declaration made after a decision has been made is less credible than one made before the process begins. Declare as soon as the potential conflict is identified.
- The management plan must be proportionate. A minor non-material interest may be managed by noting the declaration and proceeding with normal involvement. A material interest in a high-value procurement decision may require complete recusal.
- Keep the register updated. Declarations must be recorded in the council's conflict of interest register. This is the evidence that the council manages conflicts appropriately.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good conflict of interest declaration is one that a CCC investigator, external auditor, or court could read and confirm: the officer identified the conflict promptly, described it specifically, and managed it appropriately. If the declaration is vague about the nature of the interest or the management approach, it provides inadequate protection for either the officer or the council.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Conflict of interest obligations for Queensland local government officers arise under the LGA s170B (councillors) and under the council's code of conduct and procurement policy (officers). Councillors have additional declarable conflict and prescribed conflict obligations under LGA Chapter 5B (ss150EA-150EW). Officers must declare conflicts in procurement processes under the council's procurement policy and the sound contracting principles in LGA s104(3). A conflict of interest involving a councillor or senior officer that is not properly managed may constitute corrupt conduct under the Crime and Corruption Act 2001. The council's conflict of interest register must be maintained and may be subject to RTI requests. For senior contract officers (executive employees), conflict of interest obligations are also governed by their individual contracts.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Nature of the interest: My spouse, Sarah Mitchell, is employed as a senior project manager by Accenture Australia. Accenture is one of three organisations invited to submit a proposal for the council's Digital Transformation Strategy (RFQ 2026-089), with proposals due 30 April 2026. Sarah has no involvement in Accenture's public sector practice and has confirmed she is not working on this tender. However, Accenture's success or failure in winning this contract could affect their staffing decisions in the Gold Coast office where Sarah is based.
Assessment of materiality: I assess this interest as potentially material given the financial connection between the tender outcome and my spouse's employer, notwithstanding the indirect nature of the connection.
Proposed management: I will not participate in the evaluation of proposals for RFQ 2026-089. I will not have access to the proposals or scoring sheets. A conflict declaration will be noted in the evaluation report. My manager, Director Corporate Services, has been informed and agrees with this approach.
