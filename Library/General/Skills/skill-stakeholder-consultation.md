SKILL: How to Run a Stakeholder Consultation
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: General
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A stakeholder consultation is a structured process for seeking input from people who have an interest in or will be affected by a decision, project, or policy. In a local government context, consultation ranges from internal officer engagement through to formal community consultation required by legislation. This skill covers how to plan, conduct, and document a consultation process that is genuine, defensible, and produces useful input.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "plan a consultation" or "run a stakeholder engagement process"
- User needs to consult the community, businesses, or internal teams on a proposal
- User asks to "develop a consultation plan" or "engagement strategy"
- User needs to meet a statutory consultation requirement under the LGA or planning legislation
- User asks to "document the outcomes of consultation" or "summarise feedback received"

STRUCTURE
Step 1 — Define the purpose: What decisions will be informed by this consultation? What is not up for consultation? Being clear about what is open for input prevents consultation fatigue and community frustration.
Step 2 — Identify stakeholders: Who has an interest? Who will be affected? Who has relevant expertise? Use a stakeholder map — internal, external, directly affected, indirectly affected.
Step 3 — Design the process: What methods will be used (surveys, workshops, submissions, public meetings, targeted interviews)? What is the timeframe? What accessibility requirements must be met?
Step 4 — Prepare materials: Consultation documents must be plain English, factual, and honest about what is being decided and what is not. Do not present a preferred option as a foregone conclusion.
Step 5 — Conduct the consultation: Implement the process. Record all engagement. Document who was consulted, when, and what they said.
Step 6 — Analyse and report: Summarise the feedback received. Identify key themes. Note where feedback has influenced the proposal and where it has not — and why.
Step 7 — Close the loop: Tell participants what happened as a result of their input. Consultation that disappears without feedback damages trust and future participation rates.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Consultation must be genuine. Do not consult on decisions that have already been made. If a decision has been made, communicate it rather than consulting on it.
- Document everything. The defensibility of a decision often depends on the quality of the consultation record. A challenge to a council decision will examine the consultation process closely.
- Accessibility is not optional. Consultation processes must be accessible to people with disability, people from culturally and linguistically diverse backgrounds, and people who cannot attend face-to-face events.
- Analysis must be honest. If 80% of submissions oppose a proposal, the consultation report must say so. Characterising opposition as "a minority view" when it is the majority is dishonest and will be challenged.
- Close the loop. Failure to report back to participants on outcomes is the single most common reason participation rates decline over time.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A defensible consultation process is one where the council can demonstrate: who was invited to participate, how they were invited, what information was provided, how feedback was collected, what feedback was received, how it was analysed, and how it influenced the outcome. A consultation report that a reviewer cannot match to a specific decision because the analysis is too vague has not met the standard.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Queensland local governments have statutory consultation obligations under the Local Government Act 2009, the Planning Act 2016, and various other pieces of legislation depending on the subject matter. The LGA s4 community engagement principles require councils to engage meaningfully with their communities. For planning scheme amendments, the Planning Act imposes specific public notification and submission requirements. For budget consultation, the LGA s170 requires annual budget engagement. The Queensland Government's Better Practice Guide to Community Engagement provides a useful reference framework. Consultation involving Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities must comply with free, prior, and informed consent principles and the council's reconciliation action plan commitments.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Purpose statement: This consultation will seek community input on the proposed location of the new Coomera district park. The council has decided to develop a new district park in the Coomera area — that decision is not up for consultation. The consultation will inform the selection of the preferred location from the three shortlisted sites.
Closing the loop: The council received 847 submissions during the consultation period. Of these, 612 (72%) expressed a preference for Site B at Foxwell Road. The council resolved on 14 May 2026 to proceed with Site B. The primary factors influencing this decision were community preference (72% in favour), proximity to the highest-density residential catchment, and lower estimated land acquisition cost ($1.8M compared with $3.2M for Site A).
