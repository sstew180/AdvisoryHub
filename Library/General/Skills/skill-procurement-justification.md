SKILL: How to Write a Procurement Justification
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: General
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A procurement justification is a document that records the reasons for a procurement decision — why a particular supplier was selected, why a particular process was used, and why the outcome represents value for money. It is a governance and accountability document required for purchases above specified thresholds. A procurement justification that is written after the fact, or that does not address the actual decision criteria, is a governance failure.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write a procurement justification" or "document a purchasing decision"
- User needs to justify a single-source or sole-supplier procurement
- User needs to document why a preferred supplier was selected over others
- User asks to "prepare a contract award recommendation"
- User needs to record the reasons for using a standing offer arrangement or panel

STRUCTURE
Procurement details: What is being procured, the estimated value, the procurement method used, and the contract term.
Procurement method justification: Why this method was used. For open tender, confirm the process followed. For limited tender or single source, the specific grounds that justify departing from open competition.
Evaluation summary: How offers were evaluated — the criteria, their weightings, and how each supplier performed against them.
Recommended supplier: Who is recommended, their total offer price, and the primary reasons for their selection.
Value for money assessment: Why the recommended outcome represents value for money. Address price, quality, risk, and whole-of-life cost.
Probity confirmation: Confirmation that no conflicts of interest exist, or disclosure and management of any that do.
Approval: The delegate approving the decision, their delegation level, and confirmation that the expenditure is within their authority.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Write the justification before finalising the decision, not after. A justification written after a supplier has been selected and told tends to rationalise rather than justify.
- Single-source justifications require specific grounds. "We have always used this supplier" is not a ground. "The supplier holds the intellectual property rights to the system being maintained and no other supplier can perform this work" is a ground.
- Value for money is not just price. A justification that selects the cheapest supplier without addressing quality, risk, and whole-of-life cost is incomplete.
- Conflicts of interest must be declared and managed before the decision is made. A conflict of interest that is managed after the recommendation has been made is not properly managed.
- Evaluation criteria must have been established before the market was approached. Criteria developed after offers are received to favour a preferred supplier is a probity failure.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good procurement justification is one that a probity auditor, internal auditor, or QAO reviewer can examine and confirm: the correct process was followed, the evaluation was conducted on pre-established criteria, value for money was demonstrated, and no conflicts of interest affected the outcome. If the justification cannot answer "why this supplier over the others?" with specific evidence, it is inadequate.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Queensland local governments must comply with the Local Government Act 2009 s104(3) sound contracting principles: value for money, open and effective competition, risk management, ethical behaviour and fair dealing, and accountability and transparency. Gold Coast operates under the strategic contracting framework (LGR Chapter 6 Part 2) which requires Significant Contracting Plans for procurements above the threshold in the council's Contract Manual. Procurement decisions must be made by the officer holding the relevant financial delegation under LGA s257. The Queensland Procurement Policy 2026 applies to state government entities but provides useful reference standards for local government. All procurement documentation must be retained as public records under the Public Records Act 2002 and may be subject to RTI applications.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Procurement method justification (single source): This procurement is recommended as a single-source arrangement on the ground that the proposed supplier, Schneider Electric, is the original equipment manufacturer of the council's existing SCADA platform (EcoStruxure v4.2) and holds the proprietary software licence required for system maintenance and upgrades. No other supplier is technically qualified to perform this work without voiding the existing system warranty.
Value for money: The proposed contract value of $284,000 over three years ($94,667 per annum) represents a 4.2% increase on the expiring contract. An independent market assessment commissioned in February 2026 confirmed that comparable OEM maintenance arrangements for equivalent SCADA systems are priced at $90,000-$115,000 per annum. The proposed price is within the lower range and the contractor's performance under the expiring contract has been assessed as satisfactory in the March 2026 supplier performance review.
