SKILL: How to Read and Interpret a Financial Report
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
This skill is for officers who are not financial specialists but need to read, understand, and draw conclusions from a financial report -- a monthly management report, a budget variance report, a committee financial update, or an annual financial statement. It covers how to find the important information quickly, what questions to ask, and how to distinguish a healthy financial position from a concerning one.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "interpret a financial report" or "help me understand these financials"
- User needs to brief a Director or committee on the financial position from a report
- User is preparing to present financial information to a non-financial audience
- User needs to identify key issues in a financial report before a committee meeting
- User asks to "explain what these numbers mean" from a council financial update

STRUCTURE
Where to start: The executive summary or dashboard. If there is no summary, start with the bottom line -- is the organisation ahead of, on track with, or behind budget?
Revenue: Is income coming in as expected? What are the major variances? Are they timing differences or genuine shortfalls?
Expenditure: Is spending tracking to budget? Where are the significant variances? Are they controllable or driven by external factors?
Capital works: What is the spend rate against the capital program? Is it on track for the year? Underspent capital budgets often signal delivery problems.
Cash position: Is there enough cash to meet obligations? Is cash higher or lower than expected, and why?
Key ratios: Operating surplus/deficit, working capital ratio, debt service ratio -- what story do they tell?
Red flags: Any items that warrant follow-up questions.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Focus on variances. The most important information in any financial report is not the absolute numbers but the variance from budget or prior year. Start there.
- Ask "why" for every significant variance. A $200,000 unfavourable variance in maintenance costs might be a timing difference, a genuine cost increase, or a budget error. You cannot know without asking.
- Distinguish timing from structural. A revenue shortfall in October may be a timing difference that will correct by December. A pattern of shortfalls across multiple months is structural. These require very different responses.
- Capital underspend is often a delivery risk. A council that has spent 20% of its capital budget by March when 75% of the year has passed is unlikely to deliver its capital program on time.
- One number never tells the whole story. Always compare the number to: last year, budget, and the prior month trend.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A person who has read a financial report using this skill should be able to answer five questions: Is the organisation within budget overall? Where are the significant variances? Are there any cash or liquidity concerns? Is the capital program on track? What one or two issues require follow-up? If these questions cannot be answered, either the report is inadequate or it has not been read with sufficient attention.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Queensland local government financial reports are prepared under the Local Government Regulation 2012 financial reporting requirements. Monthly management reports are presented to the Finance Committee or similar governance body. The council's annual financial statements are audited by the QAO and must comply with Australian Accounting Standards. Key financial sustainability ratios for Queensland local governments include the operating surplus ratio, net financial liabilities ratio, and asset sustainability ratio as defined by the Queensland Treasury Corporation's financial sustainability framework. The Audit and Risk Committee receives financial reports and should focus on risks to financial sustainability, not just the current period position.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Revenue interpretation: Total operating revenue is $2.3M unfavourable to budget at the end of February, representing a 1.8% shortfall. The primary driver is rates revenue, which is $1.9M below budget due to a higher-than-forecast rates arrears balance. The finance team has advised this is a timing difference -- the arrears balance is expected to normalise by April as payment arrangements conclude. Fee-for-service revenue is $400,000 unfavourable due to lower-than-expected development application volumes, which is consistent with the Queensland-wide slowdown in residential development approvals reported in February.
Red flag: The Infrastructure division has spent only $12.3M of its $48M capital budget at the midpoint of the financial year (26%). This is the second consecutive year of significant capital underspend in this division. The Finance Committee should seek an explanation from the Director Infrastructure at the March meeting, specifically addressing whether the underspend reflects genuine delivery delays or budget allocations that are unlikely to be fully spent.
