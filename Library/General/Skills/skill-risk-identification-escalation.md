SKILL: How to Identify and Escalate a Risk
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: General
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
This skill is for officers who are not risk specialists but need to identify a risk, assess it at a basic level, and escalate it through the correct channel. It covers the practical steps from recognising that something may pose a risk through to getting it in front of the right person or governance body. It does not replace specialist risk assessment — it supports non-specialists in fulfilling their obligation to identify and report risks within their area of responsibility.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks "how do I report a risk I've identified?"
- User asks to "add a risk to the risk register" or "escalate a risk to my manager"
- User has identified something that could go wrong and needs to know what to do
- User asks to "document a risk" for a project or operational area
- User needs to explain a risk to a non-specialist audience

STRUCTURE
Step 1 — Identify the risk: Describe what could go wrong, what might cause it, and what the consequence would be. Use the source-event-consequence structure: what is the source of the risk, what event might occur, and what would happen as a result.
Step 2 — Assess the risk: Rate the likelihood (how probable is the event) and consequence (how serious would the impact be) using the council's standard risk matrix. Most councils use a 5x5 matrix with five levels from rare/insignificant to almost certain/catastrophic.
Step 3 — Identify existing controls: What is already in place to prevent the event or reduce its consequences. Be specific — a general reference to "processes and procedures" is not a control.
Step 4 — Determine the residual risk: After considering existing controls, what is the remaining risk level.
Step 5 — Escalate: Determine who needs to know based on the residual risk rating. Use the council's escalation matrix. As a general guide: low risk — document and monitor; medium risk — notify line manager and include in team risk register; high risk — escalate to senior manager and risk team within 24 hours; extreme risk — immediate escalation to Director and risk team.
Step 6 — Document: Record the risk in the council's risk register or project risk log.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Identify the risk before assessing it. Do not combine identification and rating in one step — it leads to under-rating.
- Be specific. "Reputational risk" is not a risk description. "Failure to complete the community consultation before the planning submission deadline, resulting in a council decision being challenged and media coverage of the omission" is a risk description.
- Controls must be real. A control is a specific action, system, or process that is actually in place and functioning. A planned action is not a control.
- When in doubt, escalate. It is better to escalate a risk that turns out to be low than to fail to escalate one that materialises.
- Document everything. A risk that is not documented does not exist from a governance perspective.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A well-identified and escalated risk is one where the reader understands: what could go wrong, why, how likely it is, how serious it would be, what is already being done about it, and who is responsible. A risk description that could apply to any organisation in any sector is too vague. A risk that has been identified but not escalated within the required timeframe represents a governance failure regardless of whether it materialises.

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Queensland local governments are required to maintain a risk management framework under LGR s207. Individual officers have an obligation to identify and report risks within their area of responsibility. The council's risk appetite statement defines the level of risk the organisation is willing to accept — risks above appetite must be escalated and treated. Where a risk involves potential corrupt conduct or maladministration, the officer also has an obligation to report to the appropriate integrity body under the Crime and Corruption Act 2001 s38. Workplace health and safety risks must be reported under the Work Health and Safety Act 2011.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Risk identification: Source — the single-source supplier arrangement for SCADA maintenance. Event — the supplier becomes unavailable due to insolvency or key-person departure. Consequence — the council is unable to maintain or repair water treatment SCADA systems for an extended period, resulting in regulatory non-compliance and potential service interruption to 180,000 properties.
Risk assessment: Likelihood — possible (rating 3). Consequence — major (rating 4). Inherent risk — high (12/25).
Existing controls: The current maintenance contract includes a 90-day notice period for termination. The supplier has provided basic system documentation. No alternative supplier has been identified or qualified.
Residual risk: High (controls are partially effective — documentation is incomplete and no alternative supplier exists).
Escalation: Risk to be escalated to Director Infrastructure and Risk team by end of business today. To be included in the Q2 strategic risk register review.
