SKILL: How to Write a Business Case
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: General
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A business case is a structured document that justifies an investment of money, time, or resources by demonstrating that the benefits outweigh the costs and risks. It gives decision-makers — a Director, CEO, Finance Committee, or Council — the information they need to approve, defer, or reject a proposal. A business case is not a project plan, not a concept paper, and not a wish list. It is a decision document.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "write a business case" for a project, system, service, or investment
- User asks to "justify" a proposed expenditure to the executive or Finance Committee
- User needs to seek approval for a new initiative, system, or significant purchase
- User asks to "make the case for" a new position, contract, or capital works project
- User needs to document the rationale for a decision before proceeding

STRUCTURE
Executive summary: One page maximum. The proposal in one sentence, the recommended option, the total cost, and the primary benefit. Decision-makers often read only this.
Problem or opportunity statement: What problem is being solved or what opportunity is being captured. Specific and evidence-based.
Options considered: At least three options — including the do-nothing option. Brief description and initial assessment of each.
Recommended option: Which option is recommended and why. The analysis that supports the recommendation.
Benefits: What the organisation will gain. Quantify where possible — cost savings, efficiency gains, risk reduction, compliance outcomes. Where benefits cannot be quantified, describe them precisely.
Costs: Full lifecycle costs — capital, implementation, operating, and maintenance. Include one-off and recurrent costs separately. Include contingency.
Risk assessment: Key risks associated with proceeding and with not proceeding. Include mitigations.
Implementation approach: High-level timeline, key milestones, and resource requirements. Not a full project plan.
Recommendation and approvals required: What is being requested, from whom, and at what level of delegation or council authority.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Always include the do-nothing option. Decision-makers need to understand the consequence of inaction, not just the merits of the proposal.
- Quantify benefits wherever possible. A business case that lists only qualitative benefits will not withstand scrutiny from a CFO or Finance Committee.
- Costs must be complete. Hidden costs discovered after approval damage credibility. Include contingency explicitly.
- The recommended option must be clearly stated. Do not present a balanced analysis and leave the decision-maker to draw the conclusion.
- Assumptions must be explicit. State what the business case assumes and what happens if those assumptions prove wrong.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good business case is one a Finance Committee member with no knowledge of the project can read and decide on confidently. If the reader has to ask "but what does it actually cost?" or "what happens if we do nothing?" the business case is incomplete. The recommendation must be actionable — "the committee is asked to approve $X for Y by Z date" not "the committee is invited to consider this proposal."

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Business cases for Queensland local government should align with the council's procurement framework and financial delegations under LGA s257. For capital works above the council's significant procurement threshold (under the strategic contracting framework for Gold Coast), a Significant Contracting Plan may also be required under LGR s221. Business cases involving external borrowing must address Local Government Borrowing Act requirements. Where the investment relates to an asset, the business case should link to the council's asset management plan and long-term financial forecast. The Queensland Government's Project Assessment Framework provides a useful reference for business cases on significant projects.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Problem statement: The council's current fleet management system (FMS) was implemented in 2011 and has not been updated since 2019. The system does not integrate with the council's financial system, requiring manual data entry for 2,300 fleet transactions per month. An internal audit finding in March 2026 rated this control gap as significant. Three fleet officers spend an estimated 18 hours per week on manual reconciliation.
Recommended option: Replace the current FMS with a cloud-based integrated fleet management platform (Option 2). Estimated total cost over five years: $1.24M. Estimated annual saving from reduced manual processing and improved maintenance scheduling: $280,000. Payback period: 4.4 years.
Costs: Capital implementation $420,000. Annual licence fee $96,000 (years 2-5: $384,000). Data migration and training $85,000. Contingency (15%) $133,000. Total five-year cost: $1,242,000.
