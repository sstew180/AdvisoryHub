SKILL: How to Respond to a Complaint Formally
VERSION: 1.0
DOMAIN: Professional Practice
LEVEL: Base
JURISDICTION: Queensland

PURPOSE
A formal complaint response is a written reply to a resident, customer, or stakeholder who has made a complaint about a council service, decision, officer, or action. It acknowledges the complaint, explains what the council investigated, states what was found, and explains what will be done. A good complaint response resolves the issue, restores confidence in the council, and closes the matter. A poor one escalates it.

WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
- User asks to "respond to a complaint" or "write a complaint response letter"
- User needs to formally reply to a resident who has complained about a council service or officer
- User is preparing a response to a complaint that has been escalated to a Director or CEO
- User needs to respond to a complaint lodged with the Queensland Ombudsman or other external body
- User asks to "write up the outcome of a complaint investigation"

STRUCTURE
Acknowledgement: Thank the person for bringing the matter to the council's attention. Reference the complaint by date or reference number.
Investigation summary: What the council investigated and how. Brief -- two to three sentences.
Findings: What the investigation found. Honest and specific.
Outcome: What the council will do as a result. If the complaint is upheld, state the remedy. If it is not upheld, explain why clearly.
Right of review: The complainant's options if they are not satisfied with the response.
Contact: Name, title, phone, and email of the officer handling the matter.

KEY PRINCIPLES
- Acknowledge first. Never start a complaint response by defending the council's position. Acknowledge that the person raised a concern and that it was taken seriously.
- Be honest about findings. If the council made a mistake, say so plainly. "Our investigation found that the officer did not follow the council's noise abatement procedure and we apologise for the inconvenience caused" is better than evasive language.
- State the remedy specifically. "We will fix this" is not a remedy. "We will arrange for the damaged section of footpath to be repaired within 10 business days -- our contractor will contact you to arrange access" is a remedy.
- Explain the right of review. Every formal complaint response must tell the complainant what they can do if they are not satisfied. In Queensland local government this includes the Queensland Ombudsman.
- Do not be defensive or legalistic. A complaint response that reads like a legal submission will escalate the matter rather than resolve it.

QUALITY STANDARDS
A good complaint response resolves the complaint at first response. If the complainant reads the response and their first instinct is to escalate further, the response has failed. The test is: does the response acknowledge the concern genuinely, explain what was found honestly, and propose a remedy that actually addresses the problem?

QUEENSLAND CONTEXT
Complaint responses for Queensland local governments must comply with the council's complaints management policy under LGA s268. The council must acknowledge a complaint within five business days and provide a substantive response within 20 business days unless a longer timeframe is agreed. If the complainant is not satisfied with the council's response, they may complain to the Queensland Ombudsman (for administrative actions) or the Crime and Corruption Commission (for corrupt conduct). Complaint responses must not disclose personal information about third parties without consent under the Information Privacy Act 2009. Responses about development application decisions must be consistent with the Planning Act 2016 and advice from the planning team. Responses about financial matters must be consistent with the council's adopted budget and financial policies.

EXAMPLE LANGUAGE
Acknowledgement: Thank you for your complaint dated 14 April 2026 regarding noise from construction works on Foxwell Road, Coomera. I appreciate you taking the time to bring this to our attention and apologise for the delay in responding.
Findings: Our investigation found that our contractor, BuildRight Pty Ltd, conducted concrete pour work on the evening of 12 April between 8.30 pm and 10.15 pm. This work was outside the approved hours of 7.00 am to 6.00 pm Monday to Saturday. The contractor did not seek an extension of hours approval as required under the Development Approval conditions.
Outcome: I can confirm that we have issued the contractor with a formal written warning and required them to implement a site-specific noise management plan. All future out-of-hours work will require prior approval from our project manager and written notification to affected residents at least 48 hours in advance. We will not be authorising any further out-of-hours work on this project unless genuinely unavoidable for safety reasons.
Right of review: If you are not satisfied with this response, you may contact the Queensland Ombudsman at www.ombudsman.qld.gov.au or on 07 3005 7000.
